The clinic began to fail before the raids came.

Not from siege. From absence. The runner who brought surgical bandages to the eastern quarter didn't come. The rotation chart that had kept triage moving—intake, sort, direct—was written in only Nara's head and had left with her body. The clinic's rhythm had never existed on paper. It had lived in one surgeon's practiced hands and her refusal to stop naming costs while paying them.

Yarrow understood this before I did.

He came to my command room while I was still pretending the ledger work would matter now that the archive was half-burned and Nara's absence had opened questions I couldn't answer while standing. The room smelled like old stone and the scar-lattice ache that never fully released anymore.

"Chimney Court clinic hit surge," Yarrow said, no preamble. "Wound rot outbreak in the quarry workers quarter. Fourteen cases, three already in decline spiral. The morning crew finished intake but there's no one to set triage priority for treatment. They've got bandages. No routing. No sorting sequence."

I looked at my hands. They were shaking. Not visibly. Internally. The way old machinery shakes when it hasn't yet decided to fail.

"Halvek?" I asked.

"Can advise. Cannot work the clinic floor. The patients need the hands that know them."

"Send runners to the eastern houses. Medical trades. Anyone who—"

"Already tried," Yarrow said. His voice had that administrative flatness he used when delivering news that was already decided by circumstance. "No one will come without Nara vouching them. They don't trust the clinic anymore. They trust *her*. And she walked out."

This was the shape of what we had built: a structure so particular to its keystone that removing it didn't create problems. It created cascades.

I stood, and the room tilted briefly, the shoulder-scar from Marrow Court complaining about the motion. Halfway to the clinic I remembered the blood basin Nara had set down beside my stretcher, remembered her face at the moment of refusal, and had to stop in an empty corridor to breathe like a man learning the motion for the first time.

By the time I reached the clinic, the early-morning ward smelled like festering wound cloth and the sharp ammonia of bodies too far past intervention.

Renn Tal was conscious.

She was one of the quarry workers who'd ended up in Nara's ledger due to contract violation. Deep laceration that had started infected three days prior. Which meant Nara had treated her, meant she had survived long enough to matter, meant she was dying now because the thing keeping her alive had decided it couldn't keep paying the price.

I knelt beside the pallet.

Her eyes focused slowly.

"Where's the surgeon?" she asked.

"Gone."

She didn't ask where. She knew what gone meant now. Everyone knew.

"I was supposed to get back to the quarry," she said. Her voice had that papery quality that meant sepsis was climbing into the secondary organs. "Three days past my assignment. They're pulling my debt tier. Other family, too. It's how the contracts work."

I looked at the wound. Nara would have opened it, cleaned the deep corruption, set something that would prevent the secondary infection already happening. I could do those things. I could follow a procedure.

I could not do it the way she did, which was with the understanding that every hour of success meant a person got to walk back into a world that had kept moving without them, and sometimes the cruelty of survival was that the world didn't care.

"I'm going to start," I said. "But we'll need help."

While I worked, Yarrow moved through the other patients with the cold efficiency of someone doing triage by elimination. Which beds were saveable today. Which ones needed pain management instead of recovery hope. Which ones could wait because waiting meant they would probably solve themselves through the mercy of decline.

By noon he had reorganized the clinic ward into something that looked like order.

Not Nara's order. Hers had felt like survival. His looked like accounting.

"We're going to lose three of the fourteen," he said quietly when we were in the back room preparing more bandages than we had clean cloth for. "Possibly four. The wound rot is old enough that it's already crossed into the blood channels. Nara would have caught them two days ago. The runners would have brought them sooner."

"If you have a solution, name it."

He didn't look up from rolling the bandages.

"Let them die. Save the ones we can keep. Ration the supplies based on recovery probability rather than intake sequence. It's more efficient."

"They came to us."

"And they came too late," he said, and there was no judgment in it, which was somehow worse. "That's not a failure of the system. That's the system working as designed. Nara's absence isn't the problem. Nara was the thing that made us pretend the problem wasn't real."

I wanted to argue.

I couldn't, because he was right in the way that efficient things are right, which is a way that has nothing to do with mercy.

The first raid came at dusk.

Not the clinic. One of the eastern storage depots. Two buildings burning in the ration district by the time runners brought the news. Sereth's mark on the fire: old house sigils carved into the loading dock in accelerant, meant to be read as message rather than random attack.

*I AM STILL HERE.*

*YOU CANNOT BURY ME IN PAPER AND ASH.*

Morn came in from the perimeter watch with powder burns on his uniform.

"Organized," he said. "Five-person strike team, military timing. Knew the depot guard rotation and the quick-exit routes. What they didn't know was that someone—" he looked at Yarrow "—had already repositioned watches based on pattern inference from prior weeks."

Two of the five raiders died. Three escaped.

Yarrow had shifted two patrol groups backward by six hours based on probability. He was barely grown and had learned to predict violence the way a weather reader learns cloud formations.

The second raid came three hours later. Clinic supply corridor. Three people burning in the north ward before anyone realized it was active incendiary. Halvek was moving patients with the deliberate care of someone who refused to panic, but his hands were shaking more than the pain typically made them.

Irix and his people got to the fire before it could cross the old timber wall.

"Message raid," Irix said afterward. "They're not trying to destroy the clinic. They're trying to prove they can burn it. There's a difference."

"Strategic intent," I asked.

"Psychological dominance. You've taken the ledgers. You've kept the routes running despite Nara leaving. You're still governing," he said flatly. "Sereth needs you to know that none of that matters because she can set fire to it whenever she chooses."

I understood that calculus. It was the calculus I had been running against Drov for months. Prove that you still exist. Prove that you can hurt on your own terms. Prove that governance is fragile and power flows from who has the nerve to destroy rather than preserve.

It was a good strategy.

It was also an admission. If Sereth had the Origin coordination Drov suspected, she would have burned the clinic completely and moved to siege. Instead she was proving her existence through controlled fire. Which meant she wasn't strong enough for absolute pressure. Which meant she was still building something.

"Bring me a map," I said. "And find Lio."

The map showed the raid points. The depot on the eastern trade route. The clinic. Three of the old shrine buildings by the market square. A sudden concentration of pressure on the logistics infrastructure I'd spent months tightening.

Lio arrived with the exhaustion of someone who hadn't slept in two days and the clarity that comes from having spent those days reading patterns in chaos.

"Sereth is stalling," he said without preamble. "The raids are good, professional, timed with precision. She's not trying to win. She's trying to prove she's not beaten while she waits for something else to develop."

"For what?"

"That," he said, "is the question that kills us. Because Matriarch Ys doesn't move without calculus, and Sereth wouldn't be running raids unless someone was telling her they were useful. Which means she's got backing. Which means we're not fighting Sereth."

"We're fighting Matriarch Ys through Sereth as proxy."

"Worse," Lio said. "We're fighting something neither of them is willing to name yet. And until they do, every choice we make looks like response instead of strategy."

By the third day of raids, the pattern had shifted.

Not to larger strikes. To distributed pressure. Small fires across seven wards. Patrol ambush in the northern bridges. A warehouse breach in the quarry sector that didn't steal anything but left everything deliberately scattered. Sereth was teaching the city that safety wasn't real. She was teaching it slowly. She was teaching it while I was still trying to stabilize the clinic's triage and Yarrow was still trying to keep the depots from collapsing under the pressure of proving that interruption was different from defeat.

Nara's absence had made the city fragile. Sereth's raids were testing that fragility in directions we couldn't defend simultaneously.

I was in the command room again when Halvek came to me with the news about the Binders.

"The healers are asking internal transfer," he said. "They want privileges that move them outside the district medical network. They say the raids prove this infrastructure won't hold."

"What did you tell them?"

"That I couldn't speak for the Commander," he said carefully. "That if they wanted to leave the district, the question wasn't really a question."

I understood what he meant. The Binders were one of the older medical operations. They had autonomy. They had old contracts. They could leave without betraying anyone. The moment one medical group left, the cascade would begin.

"Can we stop them?" I asked.

"No," Halvek said. "Nor should we. Keeping people here by threat is just governance turning into hostage situation."

"And if they leave, who do the eastern wards see a surgeon?"

"That becomes their problem," he said, and there was something in his voice that suggested he believed this was right even though it was cruel. "You built a system that required one woman to hold it together through personal sacrifice. She stopped sacrificing. The system is showing you what it was always meant to show: that power built on dependency is just slavery with better bookkeeping."

I wanted to argue.

I couldn't, because watching the clinic cascade while trying to run military response to Sereth's raids had made it clear that Halvek was describing exactly what I had built.

By the fourth night, the Binders had transferred three personnel to the western district under neutral house vouching. The isolation spread like the wound rot had spread through the quarry workers. By the end of the week, two smaller medical groups had also made exit requests.

Yarrow came to me with new procurement numbers.

"Bandage stock will drop thirty percent in three weeks," he said. "The suppliers are pulling orders from the clinic network because the Binders leaving means the district can no longer guarantee they'll have a stable customer base. We can try to replace that through contraband routes or direct procurement from neighboring houses."

"Cost?"

"Higher price. Less reliability. More bribes to the border watch."

"Do it," I said.

"It will strain the ledger currency," he said.

"I don't care about the ledger anymore. I care about keeping the clinic running until..." I stopped because the until didn't have an end in it. Until Sereth was defeated? Until the city stopped burning? Until I stopped pretending that one person leaving meant the machine was failing rather than finally showing what it actually was?

"Until what?" Yarrow asked.

"Until something breaks that hasn't yet," I said.

The fourth raid came on the eighth night of the campaign. Not fire. Not theft. A message carved into the water gates that fed the civic cistern. Old letters this time. Not Drov's administrative hand. Not Sereth's fresh sigils. 

Matriarch Ys's mark. The one she used when she wanted something understood clearly enough to murder for.

*TIME TO CHOOSE.*

*PAY IN BONE OR PAY IN BLOOD.*

*ONE OF THESE WILL HAPPEN.*

I stood at the cistern edge and understood that the raids had never been about the depot or the clinic or the bridge guard. They had been about proving the premise: that everything I had built was fragile, that every system depended on consent and vulnerability, and that Matriarch Ys had the patience and power to teach me that these two things were not the same as strength.

Below the cistern, the water held the carved words like a threat that would ripple outward for weeks.

Yarrow stood beside me in the night.

"What does she want from us?" he asked.

"To fail in front of the city," I said. "To watch us choose between keeping people alive and keeping ourselves alive. To demonstrate that this isn't governance. It's just the prettiest form of hostage situation."

"So what do we do?"

I thought about Nara leaving. About the clinic cascade. About the raid pattern that was designed not to break us but to show the city that we were already broken, just too operational to admit it yet.

"We choose," I said. "And we accept that there is no choice that doesn't cost. That's the mathematics of what we are now."

Yarrow's face didn't shift. He had learned to accept horror as simply the price of keeping moving.

"What's the choice?" he asked.

I turned from the cistern toward the glowing district, still burning small fires in seven wards, still breathing because we had not yet taught it that survival required surrender.

"We go to the front," I said. "We meet Sereth where she's actually running the pressure. We stop testing and we start paying. Because Matriarch Ys is right about one thing: time is currency now. And we're running short."

The first outward march began before dawn.

Not a full mobilization. A probe force. Yarrow, Morn, twenty-three runners who had chosen violence over the clinic's slow collapse. Halvek came because someone had to understand what we were walking into. Irix and his perimeter team took flanking routes into the western district where Sereth's forward operations had been sighted through smuggler gossip and intercepted rider talk.

Behind us, the city held its breath.

The ledgers burned. The medicine ran short. The raids kept coming just frequently enough to prove the threat was real and not frequently enough to be devastating. It was exactly what psychological dominance looked like when it had time on its side.

I was walking toward the first real battle of the attrition war with hands that still shook like machinery failing, with a body that was paying off three months of accelerated aging through every step forward, with the knowledge that Nara was somewhere beyond the district boundaries holding our daughter and the distance between us had become the most permanent thing left in my ledger.

The city had stopped burning small fires.

Black Weight pressed into my chest from inside like a thing learning to breathe in a body that was already breathing fire.

And Sereth was waiting at the first roadblock with hunger in her eyes and the two stolen ledger pages worth blood held open in her hands like a prayer to something that didn't believe in mercy anymore.
