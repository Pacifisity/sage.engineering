The split with Nara lasted less than a day before Sereth used it.

Neutral corridors had always run on trust plus speed. A marked wagon got checked once at entry, once at destination, and never stopped in between because every delay costs blood. That was the whole point. By the time my inspectors realized something was wrong, two depot yards were empty and six clinics were dosing patients with counterfeit antifever tincture cut with grave-alkali.

The first ward to discover it was Chimney Court Annex. Three children convulsed in sequence on cots that still smelled of smoke from the last shelling. Nara's junior physician broke the bottle against a wall and sent the shards to my chemists with a note that read: THIS IS NOT MEDICINE. THIS IS TARGETING.

I imposed verification protocols before noon. Triple stamp checks. Needle tests for alkali. Courier identity challenge at every gate. Armed escorts on all neutral carts. It was efficient on paper and catastrophic in motion. Triage lines doubled in length by dusk. Men bled waiting for signatures.

Every commander knows this version of failure: if you keep things open, infiltrators kill your patients; if you close the channels, your own systems do it for them.

Nara came to command and dropped a ledger on my table so hard the binding split. "You are treating suspicion like anesthesia," she said. "It does not numb pain. It just delays the scream." I pointed to the poison reports. She pointed to the waiting line outside Ward Three where two amputees had collapsed before reaching assessment.

Neither of us had a clean answer. We had competing rates of death.

That night I walked the old ward circle near the bellworks where dogs kept refusing to cross. The chalk line we had abandoned weeks earlier was glowing under rainwater, faint but rhythmic, as if a heartbeat were trying to remember how to be a signal. No caster stood inside it. No active anchor should have remained. I wrote the anomaly into black ledger pages and locked the book with both hands shaking.

At first bell I split the protocols: strict verification on bulk depots, rapid lane restoration on frontline clinics with rotating chemical teams. It saved some. Not enough. Never enough. When dawn came, the corridor signs still said neutral, but everyone reading them now understood the word as a prayer, not a promise.

Sereth hit before those split protocols had even settled into habit.

That was when I understood the poisoned tincture had not been the main blow. It had been calibration, a way to measure how quickly we could be forced into throttling our own medical arteries before the fires came.

They lit both hospitals at the same hour.

Dusk had just turned the roofs copper when signal smoke rose from River Ward and South Lintel together, black columns rolling upward like matched banners. The first reports said stove explosion. The second wave said lamp spill. The third wave said locked doors.

By the time I reached River Ward, rafters were dropping into the surgical bay. Medics were breaking windows with bone saws to pull patients through, dragging cots across gravel while resin smoke filled their lungs. A nurse with both forearms burned to blister strips kept counting names even after she lost her voice.

We pulled seventy-one alive between the two sites. One hundred thirty-four died in fire or crush. Twelve more by morning from inhalation failure. Numbers are blunt instruments. They still cut.

Every senior aide told me the same thing in different words: if we answered with kin reprisals, we would recruit a generation against us in one night. I listened. I nodded. Then I signed the order anyway.

We hit three compounds tied by blood or finance to known infiltrator cells. No trials. Forced relocations, property seizures, controlled burns of upper floors, curfew branding at district gates. The operation took six hours and bought me immediate compliance on road inspections. It also took whatever remained of my right to speak about justice without tasting ash.

Nara met me in the gutted shell of South Lintel after midnight. Rain was hissing on charred beams. "You punished families for a strategy," she said. "You know exactly what that creates." I said fear closes doors faster than reason. She answered, "Fear also teaches children what to become."

Irix did not bother to hide his disgust. "Strategically vacant," he called it, and for once he was too tired to make the phrase elegant. Yarrow, standing at the edge of the firelight, watched all of us like he was choosing which lesson to keep.

When the litters moved out, several of the dead stared upward with eyes that would not close despite cloth and pressure. An old superstition says the unburied choose a witness. I refused superstition for years. That night I wrote each open-eyed name by hand and did not sleep.

Irix waited until the room was empty before he spoke.

He set three folders on my desk: insurgent recruitment estimates, road compliance metrics after the reprisals, and projected sabotage rates if we continued current policy. Then he placed his ring knife on top of them like punctuation.

"Strategically vacant," he said. "Morally rotten. Operationally self-harming by week three."

I told him the roads had stabilized overnight. He told me terror always stabilizes first and detonates later. He was not angry. That made it harder to dismiss. Anger can be filed under temperament. Calm has to be answered.

I reassigned him anyway. Border courier intercepts, high-value document extractions, route codex verification in monastery corridors where half the doors are false and the other half are traps. I called it trust. He called it delaying the inevitable.

For two days he performed perfectly, because he always did. He returned with map fragments, cipher slates, and a seized dispatch naming three Veyr-financed cutouts in the marsh quarter. On the third day Yarrow reported that Irix had quietly requested duplicate travel writs from two separate quartermasters, both stamped for opposite directions.

Departure planning. Clean, quiet, professional.

I confronted him in the archive vault. He did not deny it. "I cannot keep teaching boys to do what we did at South Lintel," he said. "Not if you intend to call it necessary until it becomes normal." I asked whether he thought leaving would cleanse him. He answered, "No. It only keeps me from sinking deeper."

Outside the vault, mirror-glass in a discarded cabinet reflected my breath late again, a fraction behind my ribs. I looked away before anyone noticed.

I ordered Irix to remain on mission rotation under direct oversight, no solitary route assignments, all codex access logged. Useful men are hardest to release when the machine is starving. I told myself this was prudence. In truth it was fear that if he left now, his absence would become a verdict everyone could read.

Yarrow caught him at the monastery switchbacks before sunrise.

The scouts found Irix in a mule cart with false grain sacks. Under the top layer: route codices, casualty ledgers, and a packet of ward frequencies that would buy him safe passage through three active fronts if sold to the right broker. He had packed with professional restraint, no personal keepsakes, no farewell letters, just utility and distance.

They brought him in under armed escort but without shackles. That was Yarrow's decision. He said he wanted the camp to see we were still a command, not a lynch mob. At sixteen, he already understood theater better than most colonels.

In tribunal Irix admitted everything except intent to sell. "I meant to bury the codices and disappear," he said. "Take one truth out of the war before we weaponize it too." The judges argued for summary execution. The quartermasters argued for branding and labor line assignment. I dismissed both panels and kept only two witnesses: Yarrow and Nara.

I offered Irix one final mission.

Not because he deserved mercy. Because he still had a skill set no one else in my ranks could replicate under pressure, and because executing him would confirm every rumor that dissent now meant death in my camp. I called it clemency in the official order. In private ledger terms, it was leverage wrapped as grace.

The mission was simple on paper and lethal in reality: penetrate Sereth's marrow sanctum through the old catacomb aqueducts, verify the condition of the stolen core, and return with objective proof. If he succeeded, full release under sealed exile terms. If he failed, no body to argue over.

Irix looked at the order, then at me. "You are very good at making cages that sound like choices," he said. I did not disagree.

As they left chamber, the abandoned chapel bell rang once in the distance, no keeper, no rope, same as the last two nights. Yarrow flinched. Nara did not. I wrote the sound down and underlined it twice. Patterns matter, even when they wear the face of superstition.

Irix returned at dawn carried between two scouts and wrapped in a tarp that had once covered grain.

The catacomb aqueducts had skinned him raw. His left shoulder was burned through coat and muscle by contact wards, and one eye had clouded milk-white from marrow vapor exposure. He was still conscious long enough to shove a waxed packet into my hands before collapsing on the command table.

Inside were sketches, timing marks, and residue samples sealed in glass thimbles.

Sereth's stolen core was rejecting.

The diagrams showed instability fractures along graft lines that matched the failure model we had only theorized in black archive notes. She had prolonged function with serial bleed rituals and high-density anchor fields, but the rejection curve was steepening. By itself, that would have meant we could outlast her.

Then I read the last page.

Irix had mapped a second chamber under the sanctum, larger than the marrow theater, ringed with intake sluices feeding from district cistern lines. Not a survival chamber. A conversion chamber. The notes named it in Sereth's own hand: CIVIC LATTICE. Whole-population pressure harvest, distributed through municipal water grids, then focused into one command node.

She was building something bigger than herself.

Nara reviewed the samples and confirmed blood-alkali traces at concentrations impossible in standard healing practice. "If this goes live," she said, "she won't need fronts anymore. She can turn every fever ward and every drinking line into a sacrament engine." Irix woke once, heard her say it, and whispered, "Not a throne. A weather system."

I signed his release papers anyway. Exile corridor, no pursuit, sealed identity transfer. He laughed blood into his sleeve and said he no longer had enough body left to run. Then he handed Yarrow a narrow brass key cut with monastery script and told him which vault to open if I ever stopped recognizing my own orders.

After dark the dogs refused the same alley again. The cobbles there were warm despite rain. I stood at the edge and felt a low pressure behind my eyes, like altitude sickness inside a city. We had proof. We had almost no time. And we had become exactly the kind of people who can act quickly only after first paying in someone we cannot replace.
