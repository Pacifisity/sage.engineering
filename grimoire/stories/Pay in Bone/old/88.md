# Chapter 88 - Hospital Fire

They lit both hospitals at the same hour.

Dusk had just turned the roofs copper when signal smoke rose from River Ward and South Lintel together, black columns rolling upward like matched banners. The first reports said stove explosion. The second wave said lamp spill. The third wave said locked doors.

By the time I reached River Ward, rafters were dropping into the surgical bay. Medics were breaking windows with bone saws to pull patients through, dragging cots across gravel while resin smoke filled their lungs. A nurse with both forearms burned to blister strips kept counting names even after she lost her voice.

We pulled seventy-one alive between the two sites. One hundred thirty-four died in fire or crush. Twelve more by morning from inhalation failure. Numbers are blunt instruments. They still cut.

Every senior aide told me the same thing in different words: if we answered with kin reprisals, we would recruit a generation against us in one night. I listened. I nodded. Then I signed the order anyway.

We hit three compounds tied by blood or finance to known infiltrator cells. No trials. Forced relocations, property seizures, controlled burns of upper floors, curfew branding at district gates. The operation took six hours and bought me immediate compliance on road inspections. It also took whatever remained of my right to speak about justice without tasting ash.

Nara met me in the gutted shell of South Lintel after midnight. Rain was hissing on charred beams. "You punished families for a strategy," she said. "You know exactly what that creates." I said fear closes doors faster than reason. She answered, "Fear also teaches children what to become."

Irix did not bother to hide his disgust. "Strategically vacant," he called it, and for once he was too tired to make the phrase elegant. Yarrow, standing at the edge of the firelight, watched all of us like he was choosing which lesson to keep.

When the litters moved out, several of the dead stared upward with eyes that would not close despite cloth and pressure. An old superstition says the unburied choose a witness. I refused superstition for years. That night I wrote each open-eyed name by hand and did not sleep.
