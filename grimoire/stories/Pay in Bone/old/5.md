# Chapter 5 - Bone Promissory

Debtbound caravans are not prisons. They are rolling auctions.

On day two, a quartermaster walked the wagon line with a slate and stylus, pricing us like freight.

"Strong back, eight ashmarks. Ritual blood, twelve. Missing fingers, discount. Fever, burn after sale."

When he reached me, he frowned.

"Former Veyr. Bad spine. Expensive risk."

He marked me for pit transfer and moved on.

We traveled under warden escort through rain and industrial runoff. At night they chained us around axle posts and fed us thin grain broth. A deserter beside me whispered that the next stop was the marrow pits outside Cinder Gate.

I would not survive those pits.

By day three my back was failing. Sereth's extraction scar reopened every time the wagon hit a rut. I started tracking guard habits anyway: who got bored, who checked locks properly, who carried keys loose.

At fourth watch I used the only rite I could still force without a core: a tongue-vow.

I bit my cheek until blood filled my mouth, cut a line in my tongue with a scrap of tin, and made the trade clear.

One year of life for one minute of controlled blood-fire.

First attempt failed. Second burned. Third held.

Heat ripped down my throat. I exhaled at my shackles and the chain links glowed, then softened. I freed one wrist, then the other.

"Move," I told the deserter next to me.

He froze.

The alarm rune started lighting. I hit the nearest guard with the loose chain, grabbed his throat, and kept pulling until he dropped. The second guard speared my shoulder before I burned him off me. Horses panicked. Lanterns fell. Tarps caught fire.

I cut harness lines, shoved flame under axle grease, and crawled into the ditch while the convoy burned behind me.

A dead guard's coat gave me ten cinders, a cheap knife, and a folded warrant list.

My name was already on it, marked recapture priority, sanctified execution allowed.

By dawn I made the tree line north of the road, bleeding through the coat sleeve. Small spells almost blacked me out. Without a core, even concealment felt like dragging wire through nerves.

I moved in short blocks: fifty steps, stop, listen, move.

Near noon I reached a culvert where refugees had scratched directions into stone.

GLOAM MARKET.
QUARRY CONTRACTS.
PAY IN BONE.

I laughed until I coughed blood.

The vow had worked, but my body felt older in a single night. Hands shaking. Joints stiff. Mouth raw where I cut the line.

Still, I was no longer cargo.

I washed in ditch water, wrapped the spear wound, and headed for the city edge where debt surgeons worked with fewer questions than temple clinics.

At the district toll arch, a clerk shouted rates.

"Pedestrian, two cinders. Freight, variable. Corpses, half-rate if wrapped."

No one noticed me until I stumbled and left blood on the tax stone.

Then everyone noticed.
