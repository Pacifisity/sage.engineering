# Chapter 1 - The Missing Patriarch

My father taught me House power on a road map, not in a throne room.

He would spread route charts across the long table, tap trade lanes with two fingers, and make me recite what moved through each one: grain, salt, ore, levy men, temple ink, debt writs. He said a House that only studies bloodline dies the first time a bridge closes.

Most mornings started the same way.

He read levy summaries while I read route changes. Sereth came in from dawn rites with ash on her cuffs and corrected my district totals without looking up from her tea. Mother took reports in the east hall and only raised her voice when clerks brought numbers without signatures. Uncle Drov drifted between all of us, smoothing tempers and collecting what people forgot to file.

At midday Father would pull me from formal lessons to walk the store courts. He made me check grain bins, water seals, and wagon axles myself.

"You don't inherit control," he told me once, tapping a cracked wheel pin with his boot. "You audit it."

On storm nights, he sat with me in the map room after last bell while rain hit the upper glass. He spoke in that low road-rough voice and made me repeat one line until I got tired of hearing it.

"Know the route before you count the enemy."

He was not warm in public. He was consistent. In House Veyr, that counted as kindness.

Then he left for a northern tribunal and never came back.

No body. No signed death seal. No witness testimony strong enough for final rites.

At first everyone called it a delay. Then they called it weather. Then they stopped saying his name in full and switched to "the patriarch" like he was already an archive entry.

House Veyr kept moving anyway.

Servants still lit the brass saints. Ward clerks still stamped grain contracts. Uncle Drov still ran morning levy ledgers with the patience of a man who believed numbers outlived people. My mother, Matron Ys, took interim authority under emergency law. Sereth took over ritual chambers and public rites.

I kept waiting for the front doors to open and my father to walk in, coat wet from road rain, already asking for route briefs.

At seventeen, I still believed he would return in time to tell me what came next.

The House did not share that belief.

By the third month, legal clerks from the Cinder Throne were eating at our table twice a week. They carried narrow black folios and spoke about continuity, emergency inheritance, and internal risk containment. They never addressed me directly unless required by form.

Sereth started asking for my training schedules. Mother started asking who had night access to my wing and why my room assignments kept changing.

Neither question felt like concern.

One evening I found my father's traveling compass locked in Matron storage, tagged for reassignment with other "inactive office assets." I signed it out under my own name and put it in my coat.

Small theft. Small defiance.

That night, from the upper gallery, I watched my mother and uncle review sealed papers with a Throne clerk while Sereth stood at the window, hands clasped behind her back, listening more than speaking.

When I stepped into the light, all three stopped talking.

"You should rest," Mother said.

It sounded kind.

It sounded final.
