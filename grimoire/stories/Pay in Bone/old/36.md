# Chapter 36 - Yarrow's Knife

Yarrow was fourteen when he left a body between two checkpoint stones and waited for me to see it.

The cut was clean. The corpse was arranged with intent. Not rage. Not panic. A message.

"Informer," he said, wiping his knife on a strip of cloth. "He crossed twice in four days with two names and one satchel code. I confirmed the pattern, then ended it."

I asked how long he had been watching.

"Long enough to know you would have done the same."

That landed harder than the corpse.

I had spent years telling myself I kept him near to keep him safe. First errands, then ledgers, then guard details, then work where mistakes got people killed. I called it training. I called it necessity. Now a boy I met at ten had become efficient at murder and fluent in my methods.

"Good work," I said, because command has habits.

He gave me a thin smile that was not a child's smile. "You mean useful work."

Halvek found me later, still staring at the scene before we cleared it.

"He is not your ward anymore," Halvek said. "He is your junior officer. Decide if you trust him as one."

I did.

Over the next weeks Yarrow built an intelligence grid better than anything my veterans had assembled: checkpoint runners, merchant ledgers, patrol cross-checks, false manifests to bait moles. He saw patterns quickly and trusted numbers over excuses.

The network worked. Smuggling stabilized. Ambushes dropped. Revenues rose.

I logged the result in the officer book: Yarrow, authority expanded.

I did not log the other truth: I had taught a child to survive by becoming hard in exactly the shape of me.

By nightfall I signed three orders and one condolence note, then moved to tomorrow's problems. That frightened me more than the body ever could.
