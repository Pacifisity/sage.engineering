# Chapter 44 - Tithe Docks Siege

The wardens closed the harbor at dawn and called it purification.

They came in white tabards painted with temple ash and a sunburst sigil that had never once fed a hungry child. Sanctified wardens, the kind the councils deployed when they wanted war to look holy. They blocked the river mouths with chain booms, cut off medicine barges, and posted bell-priests at each pier to bless the siege as if starvation could be consecrated.

By noon, Nara's runners were out of gauze, tincture, and clean blades.

"If the next barge doesn't break through," she told me in the tannery cellar, "I start choosing who receives pain dullers and who screams through amputation."

I left before she could keep speaking. I already knew what she was about to say: that this was still my fault, even if I had not lit the first torch. Every road now curved toward my choices.

Irix and I mapped the docks from roofline to bilge. Tithe Docks had old embalming canals under the plankways, built in plague years to carry preservative oil for mass dead processing. The city had forgotten they existed. I had not.

"If we open them and spark," Irix said, tracing routes in charcoal, "we trap wardens between fire gutters and tide walls."

"And my river crews?"

He looked at me for one long beat. "Half, maybe more, if wind turns wrong."

The mirror-voice in a window behind him whispered yes before I answered.

At dusk I gave the order.

Yarrow moved with demolition teams under the pier lines, setting charges to crack the old canal caps. He did not flinch when two men vanished to crossbow fire beside him. Morn Hal's defected officers held the south docks with borrowed pikes and oilskin shields while my boat crews rowed straight into chain booms and hacked links under arrow rain.

When the caps blew, embalming oil rose like black breath through every seam in the docks.

I cast from the watchtower with both hands, feeding spark-lines down soaked planks. Fire raced outward in branching veins, turning each pier into a furnace trench. Sanctified wardens burned where they stood, then leapt into water already slick with flame. The tide itself caught. Men died screaming sermons.

So did mine.

Half my river crews never reached shore. Some burned in boats. Some drowned under burning tar. One captain kept rowing even after his sleeve caught, trying to clear wounded men before his own skin failed. I watched him go under still gripping an oar.

By midnight, the siege was broken. Medicine barges entered under smoke cover. Nara's cellar got its blades and cloth and pain syrup. The wardens withdrew to higher streets, leaving charred dead at low tide.

I walked the piers alone after first bell. Burned rope, boiled fish, and fat smoke clung to everything. I found Yarrow sitting on a crate, washing soot from his knife with dock water that reflected no stars.

"We won," he said.

"Yes."

"Then why do you look like we lost?"

Because we had. Because victory that requires burning your own crews into the tide is not victory, only arithmetic with better propaganda. Because everyone still breathing would remember who lit the canals.

"Because this is what winning costs now," I said.

He nodded like I had confirmed weather.

At dawn, Nara sent casualty totals and supply intake lists. I sent her names of the dead boat captains and ordered funeral ash stipends for each family. It was bureaucratic mercy. It changed nothing except the neatness of the ledger.

By evening, rumors were already moving through market lanes: the warlock turned water into fire; the warlock can burn tides; the warlock saved the docks; the warlock killed his own.

All of them were true.
