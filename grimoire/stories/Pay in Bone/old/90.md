# Chapter 90 - Exit Attempt

Yarrow caught him at the monastery switchbacks before sunrise.

The scouts found Irix in a mule cart with false grain sacks. Under the top layer: route codices, casualty ledgers, and a packet of ward frequencies that would buy him safe passage through three active fronts if sold to the right broker. He had packed with professional restraint, no personal keepsakes, no farewell letters, just utility and distance.

They brought him in under armed escort but without shackles. That was Yarrow's decision. He said he wanted the camp to see we were still a command, not a lynch mob. At sixteen, he already understood theater better than most colonels.

In tribunal Irix admitted everything except intent to sell. "I meant to bury the codices and disappear," he said. "Take one truth out of the war before we weaponize it too." The judges argued for summary execution. The quartermasters argued for branding and labor line assignment. I dismissed both panels and kept only two witnesses: Yarrow and Nara.

I offered Irix one final mission.

Not because he deserved mercy. Because he still had a skill set no one else in my ranks could replicate under pressure, and because executing him would confirm every rumor that dissent now meant death in my camp. I called it clemency in the official order. In private ledger terms, it was leverage wrapped as grace.

The mission was simple on paper and lethal in reality: penetrate Sereth's marrow sanctum through the old catacomb aqueducts, verify the condition of the stolen core, and return with objective proof. If he succeeded, full release under sealed exile terms. If he failed, no body to argue over.

Irix looked at the order, then at me. "You are very good at making cages that sound like choices," he said. I did not disagree.

As they left chamber, the abandoned chapel bell rang once in the distance, no keeper, no rope, same as the last two nights. Yarrow flinched. Nara did not. I wrote the sound down and underlined it twice. Patterns matter, even when they wear the face of superstition.
