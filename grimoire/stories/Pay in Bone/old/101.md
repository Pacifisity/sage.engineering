# Chapter 101 - The New Idiom

By autumn, my slogans had turned into village proverbs.

Count your ash twice.

Hide the bones.

Pay in bone.

Mothers said them while measuring soup grain. Toll wardens said them before opening gate chains. Children scratched them into bridge pilings with nail tips and broken chalk. Language had accepted what conscience refused.

Road law under my riders was brutally effective. Merchant caravans arrived on schedule. Bandit gangs dissolved or enlisted. Chapel tithes traveled under armed seal without interception for the first time in a decade.

At every checkpoint, though, ritual levies deepened. Oath fractions expanded from emergency policy into routine tax practice. Households learned to budget pain the way they budgeted oil and salt.

Safety and dread became the same service.

In one mountain market, a bookseller sold broadside ballads about the Ash Drought King next to manuals for relay compliance. My quartermaster bought both, filed one under morale intelligence and the other under civil administration. Empires are born when song and paperwork agree.

Nara's clinics kept recording what the ledgers ignored: rising psychosis after repeated low-grade extraction, seasonal collapse in labor stamina, mothers unable to remember their youngest child's face after oath overdraw. She requested suspension of relay quotas in twelve wards. I granted four.

She stopped asking in person.

At a dusk review, Yarrow asked whether this was what winning looked like. I answered with supply stats and convoy survivability. He waited for the human answer. I did not have one.

That night the dogs finally crossed the bellworks alley.

None came back out.
