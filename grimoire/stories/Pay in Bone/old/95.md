# Chapter 95 - Throne Offensive

The Cinder Throne answered our rise with a map full of arrows.

Crusade columns advanced along king roads in three fronts, each protected by relic artillery and chapel-sanctioned levy swarms. River forts closed their gates and taxed grain into starvation. Priests read emergency edicts from pulpits while armored tax teams nailed seizure notices to barn doors.

On paper it was a classic choke: isolate our districts, force surrender by winter, then cleanse remaining command tiers in spring.

On the ground it looked like children eating boiled leather.

We had no way to meet all fronts with steel, so I used weather.

Not storms in the old sense. Pressure corridors. Cold-drop shelves. Wind shear timed to marching cadence. We let one crusade column enter the Red Wheat belt, then collapsed a heat inversion over their baggage line. Wagons overturned in mud that had been dry an hour earlier. Powder spoiled. Horses panicked. Their flank officers spent two days trying to restore formation while local raiders harvested their stranded carts like fallen fruit.

It worked. That is the worst part of certain decisions.

The same inversion stripped half-ripe grain from twelve villages under our own tax protection. Stalks flattened. Seed stores contaminated with black rot from standing floodwater. We broke the enemy timetable and broke next year's harvest with it.

In council, Halvek called it a necessary asymmetry. Yarrow called it survival. Nara called it weather debt and asked who would pay the interest when spring came hungry.

I signed ration seizure waivers and emergency milling orders through midnight. Every signature moved suffering from one column to another, never removed it. Sometimes command is just a hand assigning pain to different names.

At third watch a chapel bell rang in the old quarter with no rope attached. Same anomaly, same hour, third night running. I sent two ward teams and neither crossed the threshold; both returned nauseous, with nosebleeds and identical reports of pressure behind the eyes. Sereth's lattice was already testing city acoustics.

I sent new orders before dawn. Faster. Sharper. Less human each week. The fronts held for now. The cost did not wait for accounting.
