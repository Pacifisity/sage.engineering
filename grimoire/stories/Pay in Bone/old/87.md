# Chapter 87 - Release Cost

The split with Nara lasted less than a day before Sereth used it.

Neutral corridors had always run on trust plus speed. A marked wagon got checked once at entry, once at destination, and never stopped in between because every delay costs blood. That was the whole point. By the time my inspectors realized something was wrong, two depot yards were empty and six clinics were dosing patients with counterfeit antifever tincture cut with grave-alkali.

The first ward to discover it was Chimney Court Annex. Three children convulsed in sequence on cots that still smelled of smoke from the last shelling. Nara's junior physician broke the bottle against a wall and sent the shards to my chemists with a note that read: THIS IS NOT MEDICINE. THIS IS TARGETING.

I imposed verification protocols before noon. Triple stamp checks. Needle tests for alkali. Courier identity challenge at every gate. Armed escorts on all neutral carts. It was efficient on paper and catastrophic in motion. Triage lines doubled in length by dusk. Men bled waiting for signatures.

Every commander knows this version of failure: if you keep things open, infiltrators kill your patients; if you close the channels, your own systems do it for them.

Nara came to command and dropped a ledger on my table so hard the binding split. "You are treating suspicion like anesthesia," she said. "It does not numb pain. It just delays the scream." I pointed to the poison reports. She pointed to the waiting line outside Ward Three where two amputees had collapsed before reaching assessment.

Neither of us had a clean answer. We had competing rates of death.

That night I walked the old ward circle near the bellworks where dogs kept refusing to cross. The chalk line we had abandoned weeks earlier was glowing under rainwater, faint but rhythmic, as if a heartbeat were trying to remember how to be a signal. No caster stood inside it. No active anchor should have remained. I wrote the anomaly into black ledger pages and locked the book with both hands shaking.

At first bell I split the protocols: strict verification on bulk depots, rapid lane restoration on frontline clinics with rotating chemical teams. It saved some. Not enough. Never enough. When dawn came, the corridor signs still said neutral, but everyone reading them now understood the word as a prayer, not a promise.
