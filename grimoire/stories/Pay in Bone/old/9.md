# Chapter 9 - The Salt Lantern Debt

The Salt Lantern looked respectable from the river side.

The cellar told the truth.

Tamsin Rook handled deals under the casks, away from public tables. The room smelled like lamp oil, brine, and polished metal. Hook racks lined the beams. Nothing in there suggested mercy.

I came in with Yarrow behind me.

Tamsin studied my coat, my posture, the way I protected my left side.

"House Veyr dropped you hard," she said.

"You have a route or not?" I asked.

She slid a slate forward.

One week off-grid passage. Two clean lanes. Forty ashmarks.

I put two cinders on the slate.

She tapped the second line.

"Alternate payment. One living body with equivalent debt value."

Yarrow shifted beside me. I kept my eyes on Tamsin.

"I can pay in information," I said. "Checkpoint timings. Patrol blind spots."

"Information turns stale," she said. "Collateral doesn't."

A minute later, her guards dragged in a bounty hunter bleeding through both hands.

I knew him from my convoy escape. He knew me too.

"Help me," he gasped. "I'll split the reward."

Tamsin looked from him to me.

"He's breathing," she said.

I had options on paper. In real time, I had one.

"Take him," I said.

Tamsin stamped wax, signed route clearance, and handed me a marked token.

No speech. No judgment. Just transaction.

Outside in rain, Yarrow finally spoke.

"Was he dead already?"

"No."

"Then now he is because of us."

"Because of me," I said.

We boarded her barge before dawn, hoods down, lanterns masked. The hull rode low with contraband grain and shrine brass. Ward towers swept the opposite bank with mirror lamps while we drifted under fog.

Yarrow kept the route token in his fist even when he slept.

At first light we reached a mud dock beyond direct warden authority. Tamsin's runner gave me a red-notched strip.

"Blood marker," he said. "When she calls, you answer."

I tucked it in my sleeve.

I had bought passage and time.

Both came with debt.
