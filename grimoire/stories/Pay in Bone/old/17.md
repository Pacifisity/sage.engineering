# Chapter 17 - The Quarry Lesson

Red Quarry was where old regimes buried things that still needed to work.

The outer pit held broken statues, collapsed ward engines, and condemned oath stones. The inner galleries held people: refugees, deserters, and debt-fugitives hidden behind slate curtains and false rockfall. Halvek used it as a training ground because no sound escaped the walls cleanly.

He brought me there before dawn with one instruction.

"Today you hold a ward until I tell you to stop."

"Against what?"

"Against failure."

He drew the circle in quarry grit mixed with my blood and furnace ash. Anchor points at cardinal cuts. Relief channels toward a drainage trench in case I convulsed. Nara stood outside the ring with water and clotting packs, already angry.

"This is not training," she said. "This is attrition."

Halvek did not argue. "Exactly."

I took position at center and opened the lattice.

The ward's purpose was simple: hide the inner galleries from sweep mages passing along the ridge road. If the shield dropped, the refugee pockets behind us would light up on sanctified mirrors like lantern targets.

For the first hour, it was manageable. Controlled pain, steady breath, defined output.

By hour three, the quarry wind changed and started carrying stone grit into my eyes and mouth. Each blink scratched. Each inhale stung. I kept the line anyway.

By hour five, my left hand started trembling hard enough to blur the circle marks when I re-traced them with blood. Nara tried to step in with a stabilizer injection. Halvek blocked her.

"If he can't hold this without sedation," he said, "he dies in the first siege that matters."

"He might die now," she snapped.

I wanted to tell both of them to shut up. I needed the breath for the ward.

At hour six, ridge scouts signaled incoming.

Not sweep mages. Warden skirmishers.

The shield had to widen to cover the approach tunnel where noncombatants were moving deeper into the quarry. Widening a blood ward while exhausted is how you shred channels permanently. I did it anyway.

Pain surged down my left arm like molten wire and never fully left. The ward held. Skirmishers passed above without locking target. Refugees stayed unseen.

When Halvek finally cut the circle, I collapsed face-first into grit and vomit. I could not close my left hand around the water skin Nara tried to give me. Fingers stuttered, then failed.

She looked at my hand and went very still.

"Tremor damage," she said. "Could settle. Could stay."

It stayed.

By evening I could still cast, but fine control in that hand was gone in waves. Cup lifting, thread tying, writing on narrow lines, all unreliable. Small losses that multiply under stress.

Halvek called it acceptable.

"You kept two hundred people alive," he said. "Your hand paid. Good trade."

Nara turned on him so fast I thought she might hit him.

"He is not a ledger line," she said.

Halvek didn't answer. He just looked back at the quarry wall like it had settled the argument for him.

I almost struck him and missed because of the tremor.

That night I practiced gripping a knife until my forearm cramped, then switched to the right hand and practiced again. Yarrow sat nearby pretending to clean boots while watching every failure.

"Looks bad," he said finally.

"It is bad," I said.

"You still alive though."

I looked at the shaking fingers and nodded.

"For now."

In the ledger, the entry took three attempts to write legibly.

Quarry ward successful. Left-hand tremor permanent under load.

I underlined permanent twice, each line a little crooked.
