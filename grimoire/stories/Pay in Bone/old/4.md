# Chapter 4 - The Stolen Sigil

When second bell rang, the light over House Veyr died.

Daylight went gray, and every brass idol in House Veyr looked like black glass. Servants stopped speaking when I entered a room. Ward candles burned low. Even the hall clock sounded slow, like someone dragging metal across stone.

I was seventeen and still thought family status could protect me.

By midnight I woke strapped to a slate table in the surgery chamber.

Iron cuffs pinned my wrists and ankles. A leather wedge held my jaw open. Sereth stood over me in a white rite coat. Our mother, Matron Ys, stood beside a legal clerk from the Cinder Throne and three witnesses. Uncle Drov watched from the back and kept his hands folded like this was a tax hearing.

Sereth's hands shook as she set out marrow hooks.

"Don't fight," she said. "If this fails, we both die."

I tried to answer and could only choke on leather.

They painted my spine with prayer ink and started the extraction chant. The first hook went between vertebrae and lit my whole body with pain. I smelled clove oil, hot wax, blood, and burnt marrow. The Origin Sigil fought as she pulled. She pulled harder.

The clerk asked for legal grounds. Mother pressed her seal into wax and declared me unstable and expendable. The witnesses approved. That was enough to make theft into law.

When the sigil thread tore free, Sereth flinched like she'd touched live wire. They set the shard in her basin. They burned my transfer writ before the ink dried.

She looked at me once.

"I'll carry it better than you," she said.

I spat blood at her boots.

Before dawn, Mother signed a debt conversion order and sold me to a Debtbound convoy for thirty-two ashmarks.

They took my House ring. They crossed my name out in inheritance ledgers. By sunrise I was chained in a transport wagon with quarry thieves and deserters.

The lead warden read my intake slate like comedy.

"Former High House. No sigil. Bad spine. Good ritual stock."

His men laughed every time he said former.

I memorized their faces anyway.

When the wagon rolled out under the bridge arches, no one from my family came to watch. One old kitchen worker made a ward sign as we passed. Everyone else stayed busy with grain tallies and permit stamps.

Losing the sigil felt physical, like a missing tooth you keep touching with your tongue, except the gap was in my chest and spine.

The city didn't pause for it. Bells still rang. Markets still opened. Clerks still stamped bread permits while I became collateral on a slate.

That was the hour revenge turned from feeling into plan.

I listed what I still had: one torn shirt, one split lip, no core, and a clear memory of that room. Where each witness stood. Who looked away. Who didn't.

At the first toll post, I made two decisions.

No law written by my family meant anything to me now.

If power belonged to people willing to take it, I would learn the price better than they did.
