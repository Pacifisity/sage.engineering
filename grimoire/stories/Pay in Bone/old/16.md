# Chapter 16 - Guild Chains Notice

The bounty arrived with etiquette.

A runner in polished boots delivered the notice to three hundred public boards by noon, each copy stamped with Guild Chains seal wax and Cinder Throne co-signature. Reward tiers. Proof standards. Pardon clauses. A full legal menu for hunting me as employment.

Alive paid best.

By dusk, strangers started measuring my lanes.

You can spot a professional hunter by what they do not ask. They do not ask your name. They ask which rooftops hold weight, which bridges ring under sprint boots, which backroom surgeons take cash over faith. They buy silence first, maps second.

I made counter-moves fast. No route used twice. No sleep in the same district two nights running. Message chains split across three couriers, one always false. Yarrow handled decoys with the competence of someone too young to have acquired it.

"Three new faces at fish stair," he reported. "One missing two fingers, one monk haircut with crossbow callus, one woman pretending drunk but counting exits."

I circled the locations on the map.

Nara tightened my lattice dressings and said what both of us knew.

"You cannot outrun market logic forever. Someone will sell your night for their winter."

She was right. Someone did.

The first attempt came from a roofline near Brass Alley. Bolt from sixty paces, poison-waxed head. I only survived because the shot hit the wall where my neck had been half a second earlier when a gutter tile snapped under my heel and made me shift instinctively. Luck, not skill.

By the time I reached cover, the shooter was already gone.

The second attempt came cleaner.

I was crossing a laundry bridge over canal lane with Yarrow two steps behind when three crossbows rose from opposite roofs at once. Triangulated, disciplined, no warning calls. Perfect setup.

Then all three shooters dropped.

One bolt through each throat, fired from a higher angle I had not clocked.

A figure in dark cord cloth landed on the bridge rail, knife out, eyes amused.

"You're expensive," he said. "I wanted to meet the merchandise before I sold it."

Irix Thorn.

He introduced himself with a shallow bow as if we were at a banquet and not standing over bodies on wet boards.

"Guild Chains retained me," he said. "But your mortality profile improved after observation."

"You mean you changed your price."

He smiled. "I mean I like complicated work."

I should have killed him there. Instead I took his offer: temporary collaboration under mutual surveillance. He brought hunter methods, target sketches, and names of two brokers who had posted secondary bounties in cinders for anyone willing to bring my left hand as proof.

In exchange, he wanted freedom to leave whenever contract geometry shifted.

"You trust him?" Yarrow asked later.

"No," I said. "I budget him."

"That sounds like trust with paperwork," Yarrow said.

"It sounds like staying alive," I answered.

For three days, Irix proved useful and infuriating in equal measure. He disarmed one trap I missed, insulted Halvek's theology, and recited casualty math like poetry while cleaning his blades.

"Paranoia is expensive," he told me. "But cheaper than funerals."

By the end of the week, every safehouse we touched had to be burned, vacated, or rotated. Not because they were all compromised. Because eventually one would be.

I slept in stairwells, roof gutters, and one abandoned mint cellar where old coin dies still hung from chains like teeth.

On the seventh night, Nara handed me a folded notice with my own face on it and a new annotation in guild shorthand.

TARGET ADAPTING. EXPECT ESCORTS.

I laughed once without humor.

"Good," I said. "Let them adapt."

But when the lamps went out, I stayed awake with knife in hand and counted doors until morning.
