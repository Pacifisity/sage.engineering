# Chapter 15 - Ledger Heist

Warden Archive House Three had no windows on the ground floor and no pity in its filing system.

The building sat behind a low wall of sanctified brick near the old rail quarter, guarded less like a fortress and more like a tax church: disciplined shifts, clear lines, too much confidence in procedure. Debt is easiest to protect when people think paperwork is neutral.

We hit it on storm night.

Nara drew the floor plan from memory of earlier seizure hearings. Yarrow mapped patrol loops from two days of gutter recon. Halvek prepared a silence knot for the outer bell rope. I prepared blood and accepted, in advance, that this cast would cost more than bone-fire.

Entry route was through soot vents above the records hall. The vents were narrower than Nara remembered. I had to exhale to pass the first bend and tore the lattice seam on a rusted edge before my boots even hit tile.

Inside, rows of shelves held the city hostage by ink: promissories, collateral rolls, child claims, labor transfer orders, penalty appendices stamped with temple seals. People think cages are iron. Most are paper.

Yarrow moved fast between aisles, marking bundles with wax chalk. Nara found the dock family ledgers and handed them to me without speaking. Her face did all the speaking needed.

Then the alarm rune lit on the far door.

A junior clerk had seen our lamp shutter reflection in a polished file plate and reached for the bell lever. Halvek's knot should have delayed the pull. Should have.

I did not have time for should.

I cut my forearm and cast freeze across the bell chain, forcing the metal to seize in place. The spell took, but not cleanly. Without core stability, the channel pulled from lifespan to complete itself. I felt the transaction in my knees first, then in my lungs: three months gone in one breath.

The clerk screamed anyway. We moved anyway.

We loaded what we could carry, then set fire to what we could not. Wet ledgers smoke black and slow, thick enough to blind hallways and trigger panic before flame reaches rafters. Wardens rushed the front doors into their own records room inferno while we exited through the lime chute Nara had marked in charcoal two hours earlier.

Rain hit us outside like thrown nails.

By dawn the dock square was full. I stood on a cargo crate with burnt ledgers at my feet and read names aloud while Yarrow fed promissories to the brazier. Men cried. Women screamed blessings and threats in the same breath. One old porter kissed my blood-streaked hand and told me his grandson might live the winter now.

I let him believe that.

Because then came the bill.

By noon, Cinder Throne issued emergency seizure authority to every warden captain inside three districts. Medical carts were searched. Temple granaries were locked to civilian access. New patrol powers allowed house entry without warrant in "debt instability zones," which now meant all of Tithe Docks and half the lanes around it.

Nara counted inventory and swore so quietly I barely heard her.

"They'll choke medicine first," she said. "Punishment by infection."

She was right. Two of her suppliers vanished by evening. A third was found in the canal with his wrists wired to a promissory stamp block.

At sunset, the dock bell rang again, and this time no one cheered.

Cask Bren stood under it with ten foremen and one question.

"We burned the records," he said. "Why are wardens still collecting?"

"Because law here doesn't protect paper," I said. "It protects power."

He looked at me for a long moment, then nodded like a man accepting weather he hates.

"Then next time," he said, "we burn men too."

I did not answer. I did not have to. Everyone there heard what came next in the silence.

That night, I added two lines to the ledger and watched the ink spread in rainwater drips from my sleeve.

Ledger strike successful.

Retaliation phase begun.

I left a third line unwritten for a long time.

Yarrow asked before sleep if old debts were really gone now.

"Some are," I said.

"And the rest?"

I looked at the wet ink and told him the only honest thing I had.

"The rest changed shape."
