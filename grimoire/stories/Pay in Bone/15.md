The vault sat under a chapel skeleton in mountain snow.

My mother's marker opened the first door.
Her second note opened the second.

The lock took regret as fuel.

I fed it the ring-memory loss and the seal gave way.

Inside, there was no luminous core waiting in a reliquary.

There were codices.

Skin-bound manuals, copper diagrams, ritual notebooks describing recursive Origin splitting: fragment power, bind each fragment to separate trauma streams, stabilize with lifespan burn, route through civic conflict, repeat.

Not power as weapon.
Power as economy.

Halvek read three plates and turned pale. "No practical ceiling if input keeps rising," he said.

"Input meaning?"

"Death. Fear. Oath debt. Self-erosion."

We copied what mattered, took core texts, left decoys, and exited before dawn. Burning the vault would advertise it. Sealing it would only delay the next scavenger.

In ledger I wrote three lines:

Ceiling removed.
Cost structure absolute.
Future measured by what I am willing to lose.

I performed the first fragment rite beneath Nine Chimneys in the old turbine vault.

Halvek laid Second Origin geometry with seven copper pins. Lio logged every phase. Yarrow stayed outside the threshold so one person with full memory might remain if I failed.

Dual fuel was mandatory.

Fuel one: lifespan.
Fuel two: memory.

I paid six years and one anchored childhood season.

The split entered like ice through bone.

For a moment I held two channels at once: original current and fragment current, braided but different. Range expanded immediately. I could maintain defense lattice while projecting strike nets across district lines without relay collapse.

Then payment landed.

Rain lost smell.
A winter memory vanished clean: forge light, cracked blue cup, my father's laughter near dawn. I knew the shape. Content gone.

I burned the ledger edge after writing the losses so future me would trust the entry.

Morn called the rite breakthrough.
Nara called it self-amputation with theology.

Both were correct.

By week's end, fragment operations buckled enemy lines in two sectors and triggered panic in three civilian districts where power signatures appeared without warning.

That night my reflection lagged by half a breath and smiled first.

I recorded the summary and did not soften it:

Fragment acquired.
Erosion accelerated.

The fragment frequency struck Sereth's stolen core like a fork against weak glass.

Miscasts appeared first in her rear lines: wrong-color signal braziers, shield collapses mid-volley, friendly detonations from desynced command chants.

By week two, her allies began stepping back to survive.

Cousin branches sent token escorts instead of full companies. Two councils declared neutrality. A shrine battalion refused close support while she was casting.

She remained dangerous.
She was now dangerous to her own side as well.

Karel did not abandon her.

He pulled her from two failed command zones where her own backwash had killed allies. At one ridge fight I saw him anchor her stance with one hand while throwing knives with the other.

Morn wanted immediate decapitation.
Irix wanted pressure and attrition.
Halvek warned that destabilized stolen cores break unpredictably.

I chose pressure.

Not mercy. Risk control.

In private ledger I wrote three lines:

Enemy structure cracking.
Enemy center volatile.
Decay is not safety.

Yarrow should have died on Cemetery Road.

His scout column got pinned at dusk between a grave wall and a shrine ditch. Three down in first volley, comms cut, retreat lanes gone.

He ran alone through marker stones and met Karel at knife range.

Karel had position, surprise, and no witnesses.

He let Yarrow go.

Yarrow returned after midnight, sleeve torn, blood not his own. In debrief he delivered one line exactly:

"Karel said, 'Children are not trophies,' then stepped aside."

Morn called it psychological warfare.
Irix called it code signaling.
Halvek called it mercy from a professional killer.

I heard judgment.

Karel had shown restraint I no longer possessed and done it in front of my own command culture.

I issued new directives:

No child scouts on Karel lanes unless unavoidable.
Set false-target operations designed to pressure his code boundaries.
Assign witness teams to record every breach attempt.

After council, Yarrow asked, "Do you want me off field?"

"No," I said. "I want you alive."

In private ledger I wrote and boxed both words:

Grateful.
Furious.

I left them unresolved.

I went back to the wind cemetery at dusk because daylight would have made it look like theft.

The escort dead were buried on a ridge where the wind never settled. Prayer wheels hung from iron poles and clicked all night like weak teeth. I had promised those families the graves would remain untouched, no requisitions, no military use, no bargaining with sanctity. Then Sereth pushed her channels through the low roads and I ran out of range where range mattered.

The only anchor line left was beneath my own men.

I brought six ward casters and two stonewrights. We dug narrow, careful shafts between marker posts and threaded cinder wire through old coffin nails, finger bones, belt buckles, and rusted badge pins. The veil took in stages, a pressure drop first, then the smell of wet copper, then the sensation that distance itself had become thick. By midnight our patrol chain could cross three districts without signal flare. By midnight I had also broken a promise I once thought unbreakable.

Families arrived before dawn, faster than my scribes. A mother found her son's cairn ring split open and the prayer wheel snapped from its axle. She did not scream. She held the broken wheel against her chest and asked me if I remembered his name. I did. That made it worse.

Word outran us. By first bell, markets were whispering grave-theft and oath betrayal. By second bell, agitators were printing ash slips with my seal redrawn as a hook through a skull. Yarrow asked if we should seize presses. I told him no. Burning paper makes martyrs; leaving it alive makes informants.

Nara came to the ridge in a rain cloak dark with road mud. She looked at the fresh cuts in the earth and then at me. "You used the dead as fence posts," she said. Not loud. Not theatrical. A diagnosis. I told her it bought us ten miles of protected triage transport. She said ten miles at this price would not hold for long.

She was right. Every wheel we broke became three new hands willing to lift a knife.

I signed compensation orders by the stack: grain, lamp oil, burial cloth, amnesty coupons for draft evasion. I knew exactly what they were worth. Less than one prayer wheel. Less than one intact promise. More than nothing, which is the currency empires call mercy.

That night the veil hummed across the ridge and I stood among the poles listening to wind move through the dead. The range held. The roads stayed open. Somewhere inside my skull, an old part of me kept asking how many victories it takes before a man can no longer be told apart from the thing he swore to kill.

Hollow Spire rose before dawn with funeral banners tied to pitchfork shafts and old militia helms hammered flat into shields.

They used the prayer wheels as standards. Some still had dirt on them from the ridge.

The first barricade was carts and timber. The second was stone ripped from culvert walls. The third was people kneeling in the street with wrapped faces and open hands, daring us to fire through them to reach the fighters behind. My captains wanted immediate volleys. I ordered smoke canisters and sleep gas first, full masks for our line, medics staged two alleys back.

It slowed the front for fifteen minutes. Then the wind shifted and brought the gas back into our own flank while the rebels held soaked rags over their mouths and advanced behind funeral boards. They had learned from every raid, every pamphlet, every mistake we left on a road for someone else to study.

When the west barricade held against rams and fire hooks, I gave the order I had sworn I would postpone as long as possible. Live rounds. Controlled lanes. No arc fire over rooftops. Precision is what commanders call it when they need men to kill without believing themselves butchers.

By sunrise, Hollow Spire's lower market was ours again. Bodies lay in gutters with ration ash stuck to blood like flour. A boy no older than Yarrow's first knife lesson had died clutching a snapped prayer wheel spoke. Two of my rifle teams vomited behind a tannery wall after stack counting. I did not permit them the luxury of collapse for more than five minutes.

Nara found me at the old customs arch while litters were still moving. "You turned grief into targets," she said. I told her grief had turned itself into fortifications first. She replied that this is what men in command say when they want language to do the work of absolution.

We imposed curfew by district, collected helms and long blades, reopened three water points under armed watch, and salted the rumor channels with stories that the revolt had been financed by Veyr holdouts. Some of it was true. Most of it only needed to sound plausible until exhaustion took hold.

At last light I stood where the first barricade had burned and listened to a chapel bell ring from a tower with no rope attached. I wrote the sound into my ledger and marked it under operational anomalies, as if naming it would pin it to paper. The street was ours. The city was not. Not in any way that survives daylight.

Nara chose the grain square for it because that was where everyone would hear.

By midday the plaza was packed with queue lines, ration clerks, veterans on canes, and families waiting for burial tags. She climbed onto a grain crate still stamped with my requisition seal and called my name once, not shouted, just clear enough that the entire square turned at the same time.

"He says this is survival," she said, pointing toward the militia cordon and the sandbagged machine nests. "So did the Throne. He says this is temporary cruelty. So did every House we bled out of power. If method and outcome are the same, what exactly did we overthrow?"

No one moved for three breaths. Then a woman in the front line clapped once, hard, and the sound spread like sparks in dry thatch. Yarrow's hand went to his sidearm. Irix, pale and exhausted, watched me with the expression of a man waiting to see if a bridge will hold one more load.

I ordered Nara detained under emergency speech restrictions. No manacles. No hood. One night in civic holding, clean cot, physician access. I signed the order myself and felt every eye in the square measuring the exact point where necessity becomes cowardice.

The prison captain asked if he should process her as a seditionist. I told him to write "public endangerment" and keep the ledger private. Words matter less than outcomes, I used to say. That day words were outcomes.

At dawn I released her before the pamphleteers could print a martyr edition. She stepped out carrying her own satchel, looked at me, and said, "You still think control is a cure." I answered, "I think collapse kills faster than shame." She nodded once as if confirming a diagnosis she already expected.

We did not touch. We did not raise our voices. We did not pretend this could be repaired by apology.

That evening dogs refused to cross one alley near the old bellworks. They whined, backed away, and lay flat with their ears against the cobbles as if listening to something below the stone. I marked the location for ward inspection and told no one what I felt there: a pulse in the ground, faint and regular, like a second heart under the city.
