Sentencing day filled Chimney Court beyond capacity.

Nara argued for exile.

"Killing teaches fear," she said. "Exile teaches consequence without finality."

Irix argued for execution.

"Route leaks kill convoys," he said. "Mercy here buys graves."

I split the decision and satisfied no one.

The clerk and sergeant were executed at dusk.
Sera was exiled under permanent mark and river boundary ban.

Nara did not attend the executions. She waited in the clinic stairwell and met me with a face I had never seen her wear.

"You wanted one line in your report that said restraint," she said.

That night she sent formal notice:

The clinic remains open.
The clinic withdraws from strategy councils.

At the next war table her chair stayed empty.

Operations got faster without medical argument in the room. Casualty quality got worse. Evacuation corridors narrowed. Retaliation proposals passed in half the time.

Yarrow asked if the split was temporary.

"No," I said.

By week's end we had two systems sharing one city.

My command machine optimized control.
Nara's clinic machine optimized survival.

Coordination remained. Trust did not.

I signed the next convoy plan with a steadier hand than I felt and wrote the only honest summary afterward:

We did not fracture in battle. We fractured in procedure.

Sera returned on the third rain with warden scouts in the tannery drains.

They hit during shift change, when clinic security was thinnest and lantern sightlines were poor. First contact happened in the lower surgery corridor. By the time alarms carried upstairs, six people were already down.

Close quarters turned the cellar into a knife fight over cots and medicine carts.

When we pushed the attackers back, they retreated into the limestone processing room where quicklime bins lined the walls. Five dropped weapons and surrendered. Two kept fighting and were cut down.

I ordered the doors sealed.

No tribunal. No witness rail. No measured sentence.

We flooded the chamber with quicklime and pipe water.

The screaming lasted until the steam did.

Nara reached the corridor in time to see white slurry leaking under the threshold.

"Where is Sera?" she asked.

I did not answer.

She looked at the door and understood.

"You could have tried them," she said.

She walked past me to triage and did not speak to me again that night.

By dawn we held the clinic and counted cost: six medics dead, three patients dead, two orderlies missing.

In public report I wrote: TANNERY BREACH CONTAINED.

In private ledger I wrote what mattered:

Nara now looks at me as if my body is still here but the person is not.

The vow breach looked minor until my body disagreed.

Convoy Oath 17-C required grain delivery to South Ward before night bell unless roads were impassable. I diverted around an ambush lane to save carts and broke the wording.

Backlash began before midnight.

Fever. Black-string blood cough. Sound distortion so severe that Yarrow's voice came through like bells underwater. My knees locked. I collapsed in a loft above dry stores.

Halvek arrived with leech bowls and reverse-meter prayer work.

"Vow debt compounds," he said while binding cords over old oath scars. "You are carrying too many live promises. One breach now detonates the stack."

He made me handwrite every active vow while shaking: supply, charter, quarry, protection, ritual, child-clause, transport. Six pages.

"New rule," he said. "No vow without exit plan. Discharge before expansion."

By dawn the seizure passed and left me older by another inch.

Nara checked pulse and stepped back before our hands touched.

"Maybe pain will teach what argument cannot," she said.

By afternoon I was back in command with hearing still warped and Yarrow repeating each order twice to confirm I had heard correctly.

I added a standing line to the operations book in ash ink:

NO VOW WITHOUT EXIT.

Then I resumed planning for the seventh name and the next breach point against Sereth, because recovery had become a pause, not a direction.

Saint Vellum's tower still leaned where I had cracked it months earlier.

Pilgrims now knelt to the ruin itself. Faith had adapted to rubble.

I came for bell bronze.

Halvek needed sanctified alloy for the harmonic forks in the seven-name rite. The basilica frame was the last nearby source with enough purity.

When my crews hauled rigging into the nave, people cursed and prayed in the same breath.

"You broke it once," an old woman shouted. "Now you will strip it too?"

"Yes," I said. "We are out of cleaner options."

We cut segments from cracked yokes while roof scouts watched for sniper flash. Bronze rang dull under rain. Every shard held warped reflections; every reflection carried whispers I pretended not to hear.

Yarrow caught me answering one aloud.

"Who are you talking to?"

"Wind," I said.

He did not believe me.

By dusk we hauled seven loads and buried two riggers crushed by a frame slip. Halvek tested the metal and gave one short nod.

"It will hold."

Nara sent no message about the dead.

That silence landed harder than accusation.

In the logistics ledger I recorded weight, purity, and casualties.

In my private book I added the line I almost erased:

The city worships ruins now because I keep proving ruins are honest.

The seventh name waited below Saint Vellum in a crypt older than the church above it.

Lio carried the bone key. Halvek carried the wrapped bronze fork. I carried six names written on my forearm in ash ink where sweat would not blur them.

The reliquary opened with a wax crack and a smell like old leather.

Inside lay a skin strip marked in needle script:

Ilyra Veyr-Caith.

I spoke it once.

Stone answered.

Not light. Pressure. Voices in the joints of the crypt, ancestral tones shaped like judgment. One blamed Sereth. One blamed me. One said both.

Halvek watched my hand shake.

"This rite will not leave you clean," he said.

"Nothing has for years," I answered.

We left in rain, and I repeated the name every hundred steps so bell gaps could not steal it before cast time.

At camp, I wrote all seven names from memory, burned the page, and wrote them again. All seven returned.

For the first time in months, the objective was complete enough to execute and terrible enough to fear.

We cast at the west graves under rain and no lanterns.

Halvek placed the bronze forks. Lio marked ash lines and sequence checks. Morn held perimeter. Yarrow stood inside the outer ring with slate and timing marks.

I spoke the names in order.

First: silence.
Second: ground hum.
Third: distant signal braziers flutter.
By the sixth, vow scars along my ribs burned like wire.

Seventh: Ilyra Veyr-Caith.

The city stuttered.

Warden signal chains misfired. Tower orders crossed. Sereth's command quarter sent contradictory runners in open view. For seven minutes, her stolen Origin lost rhythm.

For seven minutes, she was vulnerable.

Then backlash took payment.

Not pain first. Loss.

A memory lifted clean: dusk courtyard, laughter, silver ring flash. Face blurred, name gone, voice fading last. I reached under my collar and found the plain ring still there, suddenly heavy with grief I could not place.

Halvek cut the rite before collapse. Yarrow and Morn dragged me out of the mud.

By dawn my shoulder barely turned and white had spread further through my hair.

Nara held up the ring during exam.

"Whose is this?"

"I don't know," I said, and meant it.

She gave one instruction only.

"Write down what you lost before you lose that too."

I logged the result:

Seven-name destabilization succeeded for seven minutes.
Major memory payment extracted.
Object retained: silver ring, provenance erased.

It was a turning point.

Not toward peace. Toward a sharper version of the same war.

After the grave-circle trial, the city could not contain the war's logistics.

We pushed into the ashland road belt with convoy lines, tribute clerks, and rotating pike screens. I called it expansion. Villages called it arrival.

At first they offered grain and pledges.

Then the ledgers started coming in light.

Undercounted sacks. Hidden sons. Daughters moved to monastery roads before recruitment days. No open revolt, just quiet refusal stitched into routine.

Morn wanted punitive fire.

"Burn one keep," she said. "The rest stop testing."

Irix wanted measured pressure.

"Fear is expensive. Accounting is cheaper."

I chose audits and fines because winter roads still needed to function.

Bone clerks set scales in market squares, seized hidden stores, and drafted offenders into labor penalties. Compliance rose on paper while faces hardened in silence.

On the sixth stop, a child at Bracken Rise asked why my hair looked like ash if I was not old.

"Storm debt," I said.

She handed me dried apple and walked away before I could decide whether to smile.

By month end we held eight minor keeps, three road segments, and one river crossing formerly tied to Sereth's cousins.

Territory gained. Insurgency seeded.

In campaign notes I wrote both lines side by side and stopped pretending one canceled the other.

Tribute circuit turned village squares into courts without benches.

I offered three payment channels: deferred harvest credit, direct cinder transfer, or bonded fighters for seasonal defense.

Publicly, elders thanked us for protection.
Privately, they called it polished extortion.

Both were accurate.

Dunmere paid in paper and reluctant spears.
Flint Hollow paid in cinders and hid draft-age sons.
Old Mire begged escort after refusing fighter quotas; I granted it at double interest.

"Punishment?" Yarrow asked.

"Instruction," I said.

By the fourth village, resistance was visible before speech: shutters closing on children, men standing too straight, priests blessing ledgers longer than graves.

Morn urged force normalization.
Irix argued for controlled resentment over open rebellion.

I followed Irix until the third night, when mirror prompts and bad weather wore my patience thin. We seized collateral lands from a council that missed tribute by two days and stamped transfer papers in mud while their elders knelt.

No blood spilled.
The message traveled anyway.

Next circuit compliance jumped.
So did hate.

I logged the month in two columns:

Resources improved.
Resentment embedded.

Both marked green.

Karel found me on Saint Marrow Ridge where prayer wheels rusted in frozen wind.

We were traveling light: me, two escorts, one mule, no banners. It should have been invisible movement.

First sign was a nail-slice across Bren's cheek from nowhere.
Second was my mule dropping with a bolt through the eye.
Third was Karel's voice behind the wheel line: "You are easiest to track when you pretend to travel humble."

We ran for an ossuary wall and found him already ahead of us.

I cast emergency displacement.

The ward needed two blood-close anchors.
The escorts understood a half-second before it closed.

"Do it," Bren said.

I did.

Karel fired as the gate snapped. Bren took a throat shot. Talin caught backlash shrapnel and folded into the mud screaming. I displaced thirty yards into shadow and survived.

Karel did not pursue. He watched from the ridge and called down, "Distance bought. Dignity spent."

He was right.

I came back with Bren's tags and Talin's knife wrapped in map cloth.

At council I reported enemy contact and survival.
I did not report geometry.

In private ledger I wrote the true line:

Escaped by spending loyal men as coordinates.

Bren's mother and Talin's sister led forty-seven relatives into Chimney Court carrying two empty helms.

No shouting. No riot flags. Just discipline sharp enough to cut.

"Tell us exactly how they died," Bren's mother said.

I gave compensation decree instead.

Two thousand ashmarks per family.
Lifetime ration rights for first-degree kin.
School exemptions for dependent children.

The clerk read. The gallery murmured approval. The families did not move.

"We asked cause," Talin's sister said.

"Enemy ambush," I answered.

True and incomplete.

I sealed cast details under operational secrecy. If displacement mechanics spread, every assassin would bait escort clusters to force the same choice again.

Rumor filled the silence by nightfall.

Some said witch-fire took them.
Some said I traded them for distance.

The second version held more truth.

Yarrow asked if we should issue denials.

"We confirm service. We deny specifics," I told him.

Recruitment data arrived a week later: escort-family volunteers down hard across two circuits.

Compensation preserved order.
Silence seeded distrust.

I put Bren's tags and Talin's knife in the same box as the unknown silver ring and locked it before dawn.
