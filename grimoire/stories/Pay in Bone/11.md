Success invited repetition faster than caution could object.

The second bell cast came five days later.

Three merchant convoys carrying grain and clot wash were coming through the eastern kiln road under Lantern token cover. Wardens had pre-positioned inspection teams on the visible route and a seizure unit at the bridge scaffolds. If the convoy turned back, two wards lost meal distribution inside two days and Nara started amputating without fever tincture again.

Yarrow came to the forge office after dark with the route model already redrawn.

"We can move them through the old lime road," he said. "But only if the warden bells near South Trench all hear the same false order at once."

"How many towers?"

"Three."

Halvek, leaning in the doorway with the expression of a man who has spent his life being disappointed by institutions and is only occasionally surprised by people, said, "Two rings if you want district breadth. One won't carry the shape far enough."

"One day," I said.

Yarrow did not flinch.

He had learned, from me, to hear the price in the plan and leave it there unless asked.

"I can move the convoy masters before dawn," he said. "If you cast at first bell, they'll be in the new lanes before the wardens realize they are waiting for ghosts."

"You already told them?"

"I told them to be ready for new instructions. Not what the instructions would be."

There it was again: the narrow, dangerous competence that made him useful and made every quiet room around him an accusation.

"Do it," I said.

He nodded and turned away.

"Yarrow," I said.

He stopped.

"If the cast fails, you abort. No heroics. No trying to hold the old route because merchants panic at change."

"Understood," he said. "If it fails, I cut the lead carts and preserve the medicine wagon first."

He had already chosen the triage order.

I dismissed him and sat with that for a moment after he was gone.

Halvek saw where my thoughts had landed.

"You wanted him to stay young longer," he said.

"No," I said. "I wanted him not to become useful in exactly the ways I have."

"That has never been the same wish."

The first-bell cast took place from the old grain exchange roof because its signal frame still carried enough bronze to speak to the southern trenches if hit correctly. I cut the lattice open with a thumbnail until blood slicked the lower ribs and named the cost before the pain could make the naming feel optional.

"Two rings. One day of memory. South Trench false inspection order. East convoy redirected to lime road."

I struck.

The world opened sideways.

Bell-casting never felt like power in the ordinary sense. It felt like convincing structure to believe a lie for a few seconds longer than it should. Signal posts answered. Warden relays repeated the false command. Three checkpoints turned their inspections toward empty road while Yarrow's convoy masters took their carts down the lime cut under dust cover and black-market prayer flags.

I held the shape too long because from the roof I could see one wagon lagging, axle trouble on the descent, and because the impulse to optimize is just greed with better rhetoric.

The backlash took my breath, then my footing.

When I opened my eyes again I was in the forge office with sunlight already wrong for my body clock and Halvek's neat priest-script waiting beside the bed.

Second blank. One day.

Convoys through. One axle lost. No medicine loss. Five guards injured. You argued with Nara about escort fees and forgot the argument midway through having it.

Below that, my own hand in jagged ink I did not remember forming:

The pages are changing shape when I'm not looking at them.

That one unsettled me more than the missing day.

I went back three entries and found it wasn't entirely paranoia. The handwriting after bell work tilted differently, sharper and more compressed, with less wasted curve in the letters. The words themselves were colder. Cleaner. Operational success. Acceptable loss. Route holds. No mention of fear, almost none of grief. As if some clerk had taken over my pulse whenever memory got shaved close.

The mirror above the wash stand gave me the answer before reason did.

My reflection was already reading the page before my eyes reached the line.

Its mouth moved.

No sound at first. Then, faintly, as if from the far side of polished stone:

You kept the medicine.

I turned the mirror to the wall hard enough to crack the backing.

The clinic smelled of boiled thread, lamp oil, and summer infection. Heat makes wounds arrogant. Nara had six cots filled with men who would have survived in winter and now might not because everything rotted faster.

She was wearing a looser coat than usual, sleeves rolled higher, the belt tied lower at the waist. I noticed because she moved around the nearest cot more carefully than I expected, conserving her turn rather than twisting at the middle.

She's become exhausted.

"Convoys made it," I said.

"I know," she answered. "The medicine arrived yesterday."

Yesterday.

I hated how simple that word had become. A territory I no longer had rights to.

"Did we argue?" I asked.

One of the younger orderlies, a girl from the bridge camps with burn scars down one arm, went very still at the nearby basin. Nara sent her away with a look before answering.

"About escort fees," she said. "You said merchants pay them. I said families pay merchants. Then you forgot the point and started explaining the convoy geometry again."

"I don't remember any of it."

"Yes."

It was not accusation. That would have been easier.

She tied off a dressing on the cot and finally faced me.

"Your face barely changes when I tell you that now," she said.

"What should it do?"

"Something."

I started to answer with practicality because practicality had become my last reliable shelter.

She cut across it.

"That is what I mean," she said. "Everything reaches you as a number first. Even this. Even you."

I could have told her about the reflection. About the wrong handwriting. About the way the missing days felt not absent but occupied. Instead I said the one thing that made defense sound like honesty.

"If I stop doing the math, people die."

"People die while you do it too."

That ended the useful part of the conversation.

By full summer, the bells had changed how everyone around me planned. Not because they loved the magic. Because magic that extends reach becomes administration faster than war ever does. Captains started bringing me problems that could have been solved with better discipline because they assumed I could flatten distance by paying with my skull. Quartermasters asked whether I could ring for crowd control during ration crush. Two district clerks suggested a scheduled weekly cast to stabilize market rumor.

I threatened both clerks with permanent latrine duty and then spent that night wondering whether they were wrong.

That was how corruption worked at scale. Not only by making harm easier. By making it sound efficient.

Yarrow grew into that season like a knife fitted to the hand that shouldn't be holding it.

He was no longer the narrow child from the alleys, all elbows and suspicious appetite. He had height now, not much but enough to change how people misjudged him. His voice had lost its uncertain lifts. He carried reports as if he were already sure the words inside them would alter other people's lives. Men twice his age took route corrections from him because he was usually right and because the penalties for proving him wrong had become statistical, not theatrical.

The part that frightened me was not that he could command.

It was how little command seemed to cost him in public.

The third cast came on a day that had already gone bad before morning meal.

Three courier teams were moving clinic alkali, grain tallies, and one packet of kin-route names out of the south districts when shrine wardens sealed the culvert lanes ahead of schedule. Two teams got clear. The third vanished into the old cistern maze under Glass Kiln Ward with four armed escorts and a child runner too fast to have been left behind.

The packet of names mattered because Lio believed three of them were blood-relations Sereth had not found yet.

The clinic alkali mattered because Nara had a fever ward starting.

The child mattered because he was a child, though I had already built enough machinery around myself to understand that this was the least persuasive reason in the room.

We found the trapped team by sound: two hammer taps from inside the cistern wall, then one, then two again. Old quarry code. Still alive.

Wardens were sealing the outer shafts and rigging smoke pots to drive them inward.

"Too deep for runners," Yarrow said. "Too many forks for local relay. If they lose light in there, they choke before the wardens ever see them."

He had a map unrolled before the sentence finished. Three egress points, all compromised. One old pump tower still standing over the network, bell frame intact but warped. Range wide, sightline bad, cast conditions filthy.

"Three rings," Halvek said after one glance. "Anything less and you won't reach every branch."

Silence held for exactly the time it takes a room to imagine a week being cut out of a man.

Nara set down the forceps she was cleaning.

"No," she said.

No one answered her because there are kinds of refusal war teaches people to step around rather than confront.

"If they smoke the cisterns," Yarrow said, eyes still on the map, "the packet burns, the alkali spoils, and the trapped team dies inside an hour."

"I know the arithmetic," Nara said.

She looked at me, not at the map.

"Do you?"

I did.

That was the problem.

We climbed the pump tower under a sky the color of old dishwater. The bronze bell there had split years earlier and been mended with iron staples that made the whole frame sound sick even before I touched it.

The city spread outward in summer haze, all my failures connected by roads.

Below, Yarrow was already placing teams at the three possible exits. He had done it without waiting for final instruction, assigning reserve rope crews, smoke blankets, and one shooter on the west parapet in case the wardens used the trapped couriers as a screen.

He looked up once. Not for permission. For timing.

I raised one hand.

Nara had followed us farther than I realized. She stood on the ladder platform below the bell frame, one hand on the rail, face emptied of everything except anger disciplined into usefulness.

"If you do three rings," she said, "write before and after. Make the before larger."

I almost asked why that mattered. Then I understood she was not talking about paper.

I cut the lattice open at three nodes this time. Pain flashed so bright the tower swayed black at the edges.

Halvek's voice came steady through it.

"Name the cost."

"Three rings," I said. My mouth was already numb. "One week of memory for full-branch relay, smoke confusion, and eastward draw. Open the cistern exits. Blind the wardens."

"Again."

"One week," I said, louder. "I know."

The first ring tore the air.

The second tore me.

By the third, I was no longer sure which parts of the city were outside my skull and which were being projected there by pain and need. Every culvert answered. Every metal line in the district sang back wrong. I pushed the command through anyway: heat on the west branch, phantom movement south, open pressure north, draw the wardens off the trapped team, let the living find the ladder light.

And under all of it, quiet as a confidant in a chapel, the mirror-voice said yes, this is the efficient shape.

Then nothing held.

I woke to seven pages of missing life.

The first proof was my face. Beard rougher. Eyes more hollow. Fresh yellowing bruise on my collarbone I did not remember earning.

The second proof was the ledger stack tied to my wrist with black thread so I would not wake without it.

Page one, in Halvek's hand:

Three-ring cast successful. Trapped team recovered. Child runner alive. One escort dead in egress crush. Packet preserved. Alkali preserved. Memory loss consistent with expected week-scale severance.

Page two, in my own altered script:

Do not ring again this month unless the city itself is ending.

Page three, in Nara's hand:

You woke twice during the week and knew no one for six breaths each time. Then you knew everyone too well and frightened the orderlies.

Page four:

Yarrow handled the south ration crush without you. Read his report before speaking to him.

Page five held Yarrow's report, precise as wire.

Ration riot at Glass Kiln. Cause: rumor of reduced soup allotment. Correction: rumor false. Action taken: split line by household mark, removed instigator, opened reserve barrel, posted two visible medics, one hidden shooter. Casualties: three crushed, one stabbed, instigator executed during attempted arson of barrel cart. Stability restored.

He had signed it with his full name.

Not Yarrow.

Yarrow Pike.

An officer's signature.

I read the page three times because I could not feel the week that had made it possible.

The mirror over the basin was turned to the wall. Smart. I turned it back anyway.

My reflection looked more rested than I felt.

Its mouth moved before mine.

He chose correctly.

"Did he?" I asked it.

Wrong decision. Once you answer the thing, it stops needing permission.

The reflection smiled with only one side of my face.

He chose like you.

I covered the glass with a cloth and went to find the boy I had made into a function.

Yarrow was in the records room teaching two older runners how to mark suspicion versus evidence on watch sheets. He looked up when I came in and read the week-loss on my face before I said anything.

"You missed seven days," he said. "Mostly fever and direction-setting."

Mostly.

"I read your Glass Kiln report," I said.

"Yes."

"You executed the instigator."

"He tried to light the barrel cart with thirty people packed around it."

"You didn't wait for a second opinion."

"There wasn't time."

He said it cleanly, no apology and no pride. That was what made it unbearable.

"Was there another option?" I asked.

He considered the question honestly. That, too, was my training in him.

"Probably," he said. "Not one that kept the food from catching if I guessed wrong."

I could not even correct him from memory. The week where my moral authority might have contested his had been traded for the operational outcome he had delivered.

"Mera says you kept trying to stand after the third ring," he added. "Halvek and Nara had to drag you off the tower."

"Did I tell you anything useful?"

"You told me if the city begins relying on you to solve distance with bells, start lying about what you can do."

That sounded like good policy. It also sounded like something the reflection might say.

"Did I mean it?" I asked.

Yarrow's expression changed just enough to show he had noticed the question was wrong.

"You wrote it twice," he said.

That was answer enough.

The week returned only in scraps after that.

A bruise memory: someone grabbing my shoulder while I tried to walk without instructions.

A lamp memory: Halvek reading cost figures while I argued with numbers I could not hold onto.

A warmth memory: sitting at the ledger crate late, the clinic finally quiet, Nara beside me going over supply rates in that low voice she used when exhaustion had sanded the edges off anger.

That one came back not as sequence but as sensation.

Her shoulder against mine first by accident, then by decision.

The weight of her head settling there when she fell asleep halfway through a sentence about fever wash and dried mushroom allotments.

I know it happened because the memory returned in the body before it returned in language. I can still place the exact ache in my right arm from not moving for what must have been a long time. I can still remember the measured rhythm of her breathing through the linen of my shirt. The room had smelled of lamp smoke, ink, and the bitterleaf tea she never sweetened for me.

When she woke, if memory can be trusted even that far, she did not apologize.

She straightened, took the ledger, and continued from the line she had fallen asleep on.

As if certain forms of closeness only become survivable when treated as already decided.

I found her on the roof two nights after the missing week, folding dried bandages in the last of the light. The summer dark had not fully come in yet. District Nine stretched under us in chimney smoke and meal-line bells.

"There's something I don't remember clearly," I said.

"That narrows nothing."

"Have we crossed something?"

She held the folded cloth a moment longer than needed.

When she answered, her voice was very tired.

"I stopped asking that question."

Then she went back to folding bandages as if the matter had the same status as weather: present, consequential, and not improved by speech.

I stood there longer than made sense.

It is possible to win a sentence and lose all the ground around it.

By month's end, the city had started planning around my absences as much as my presence. Halvek insisted on pre-cast instructions in sealed envelopes. Yarrow kept duplicate route sheets in three locations. Nara began writing patient and command notes in different inks so if I lost a day I could tell whether a page was about bodies or systems before I misused one for the other.

That detail hurt more than most of the magic did.

Not because it was cruel.

Because it was kind in the shape of surrender.

The Black Weight no longer waited for mirrors exclusively. Sometimes I would be halfway through an allocation argument and know, with sick certainty, exactly which efficient cruelty the reflection would recommend. Sometimes I took the advice before seeing the glass. Sometimes I only realized later that I had phrased an order in words that felt pre-polished, as if rehearsed by a second mouth.

Coherence, Halvek said, was maintained through record.

Nara said coherence was not the same thing as being intact.

Yarrow said almost nothing unless the silence itself had become inefficient.

We went on that way because going on is what systems do once they learn to feed on damage.

At the end of the month I sat alone in the forge office with the two ledgers open: operations on the left, ethical debt on the right. The left ledger showed measurable success. Meal lines stabilized in two wards. Three kin-routes remained viable. South-lane courier survival rose by a third. Bell interventions prevented two sweeps, one convoy seizure, and the cistern deaths.

The right ledger held names, missing dates, the executed instigator at Glass Kiln, one escort crushed in the cistern egress, one week of myself gone where no name could follow.

I wrote the final entry slowly so the hand would stay mine long enough to finish it.

Entry Nine.

High summer, roughly two years from exile.

Bell-casting now operational at district scale. Confirmed costs: two rings equals one day lost; three rings equals one week. Write before. Write after. Assume the missing time still acts through me.

Yarrow now leads field corrections without waiting for permission. This keeps people alive and should frighten me more than it has time to.

Nara says my face no longer changes when I hear what the bells remove. She is likely right.

Operational success and personal attrition.

I sanded the page, closed the book, and caught my reflection in the dark window before I could turn away.

It looked like a man who had learned to survive by becoming divisible.

Its mouth moved.

I did not wait to see if sound would follow.
