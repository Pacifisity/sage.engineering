My father taught me House power on a road map, not in a throne room.

He would spread route charts across the long table, tap trade lanes with two fingers, and make me recite what moved through each one: grain, salt, ore, soldiers, official seals, debt papers. He said a House that only studies bloodline dies the first time a bridge closes.

Most mornings started the same way.

He read levy summaries while I read route changes. Sereth came in from dawn rites with ash on her cuffs and corrected my district totals without looking up from her tea. Mother took reports in the east hall and only raised her voice when clerks brought numbers without signatures. Uncle Drov drifted between all of us, smoothing tempers and collecting what people forgot to file.

At midday Father would pull me from formal lessons to walk the store courts. He made me check grain bins, water seals, and wagon axles myself.

"You don't inherit control," he told me once, tapping a cracked wheel pin with his boot. "You audit it."

On storm nights, he sat with me in the map room after last bell while rain hit the upper glass. He spoke in that low road-rough voice and made me repeat one line until I got tired of hearing it.

"Know the route before you count the enemy."

He was not warm in public. He was consistent. In House Veyr, that counted as kindness.

Then he left for a northern tribunal and never came back.

No body. No official death record. No witness testimony strong enough for final rites.

At first everyone called it a delay. Then they called it weather. Then they stopped saying his name in full and switched to "the patriarch" like he was already an archive entry.

House Veyr kept moving anyway.

Servants still lit the brass saints. Ward clerks still stamped grain contracts. Uncle Drov still ran morning levy ledgers with the patience of a man who believed numbers outlived people. My mother, Matron Ys, took interim authority under emergency law. Sereth took over ritual chambers and public rites.

I kept waiting for the front doors to open and my father to walk in, coat wet from road rain, already asking for route briefs.

At seventeen, I still believed he would return in time to tell me what came next.

The House did not share that belief.

By the third month, legal clerks from the Cinder Throne were eating at our table twice a week. They carried narrow black folios and spoke about continuity, emergency inheritance, and internal risk containment. They never addressed me directly unless required by form.

Sereth started asking for my training schedules. Mother started asking who had night access to my wing and why my room assignments kept changing.

Neither question felt like concern.

One evening I found my father's traveling compass locked in Matron storage, tagged for reassignment with other "inactive office assets." I signed it out under my own name and put it in my coat.

Small theft. Small defiance.

That night, from the upper gallery, I watched my mother and uncle review sealed papers with a Throne clerk while Sereth stood at the window, hands clasped behind her back, listening more than speaking.

When I stepped into the light, all three stopped talking.

"You should rest," Mother said.

It sounded kind.

It sounded final.

Interim authority is supposed to preserve a House until real leadership returns.

In practice, it lets people rehearse replacement.

Mother kept every decision legal. That was her strength. Every transfer stamped. Every seizure signed. Every emergency policy read aloud with witnesses present. If anyone challenged her, she produced three papers and a court citation before they finished speaking.

Sereth handled optics.

She visited district shrines, led healing rites in public squares, and spoke with the calm of someone already carrying inherited power. Crowds liked her because she looked steady. Clerks liked her because she never improvised in front of them.

I handled what they allowed me to handle.

Route inspections. Minor ward checks. House guard drills at hours no one important attended.

When I asked for access to the Origin chamber, I was told my focus discipline still needed review. When I asked again, Uncle Drov called it procedural delay and advised patience.

Patience is easier to preach when you already have authority.

One rain-heavy afternoon, a Throne assessor requested a demonstration of heir readiness before renewal of our emergency autonomy permits. I expected a combat test or strategic hearing.

Instead they asked for a controlled channel cast in the family chamber.

I passed the cast. Then a ward anchor failed in the wall seam and blew out half the chalk ring.

No one was hurt, but the assessor wrote for ten straight minutes.

"Unstable under stress," he said, not unkindly. "Recommend supervision increase."

Sereth stepped in before I could answer.

"He has been under unusual pressure since our father's disappearance," she said. "We'll tighten controls."

At dinner that night she sat beside me for the first time in months.

"They're watching for weakness," she said quietly. "Don't give them one."

"Then stop feeding them mine," I said.

She looked straight ahead.

"I'm trying to keep this House alive."

That was the first time I noticed we were using the same words and talking about different things.

Later, Yara from kitchen service slipped me a folded note while clearing plates.

Two lines only:

Throne clerks filed your cast under internal hazard review.
Matron requested expedited emergency options.

I burned the note in my lamp and told myself it was precaution, not a verdict.

I still slept with my father's compass under my pillow.

The week before the eclipse, House Veyr locked its lower archives and doubled interior guard rotations.

Official reason: external sabotage risk.

Real reason: paperwork.

By then I knew enough to watch the right hands.

Mother signed emergency continuity forms at dawn and dusk. Uncle Drov cross-filed witness statements. Throne clerks came through private service doors instead of the public hall. Sereth moved between ritual chambers and legal offices with no escort, no interruptions.

I confronted Mother outside the ledger room.

"Are you declaring Father dead?"

"No," she said. "I am declaring this House functional."

"Without me?"

She adjusted one glove finger before answering.

"With safeguards."

The same day, two guard captains I trusted were reassigned to district patrol and replaced by men from Matron command rolls. My training staff changed. My key access changed. Even my meal schedule changed, always by legal notice, never by force.

That's how houses remove people when they don't want public blood.

By evening I was escorting a supply audit through the east corridor when I heard Sereth arguing with someone inside the surgery annex.

"There has to be another way," she said.

A male voice answered, low and clipped. The flat tone of Cinder Throne lawyers.

"Emergency ratification permits transfer in cases of strategic instability. You have the signatures."

I stepped away before the floor guard saw me listening.

I should have run that night.

Instead I kept trying to solve it from inside the rules.

At dinner, Sereth sat across from me and barely touched her food.

"Stay in your wing tonight," she said. "No corridor rounds."

"Order or warning?"

She didn't answer.

I slept in travel clothes with boots beside the bed and my father's compass in my coat pocket.

At second bell, the ash eclipse swallowed the house lamps and the corridor wards shifted to night lock.

Someone opened my door with a key only senior command should have held.

I was half out of bed before they crossed the threshold. A cloth hit my mouth. Bitter oil flooded my tongue. Someone drove me back into the mattress while another caught my wrists.

I never saw who carried me.

By the time I surfaced again, midnight had passed and the eclipse light had turned the surgery glass the color of dirty iron. Every brass idol in the chamber looked black. The ward candles burned low. Even the hall clock sounded slow, like someone dragging metal across stone.

I was seventeen and still thought family status could protect me.

I woke strapped to a slate table in the surgery chamber.

Iron cuffs pinned my wrists and ankles. A leather wedge held my jaw open. Sereth stood over me in a white ritual coat. Our mother, Matron Ys, stood beside a legal clerk from the Cinder Throne and three witnesses. Uncle Drov watched from the back and kept his hands folded like this was a tax hearing.

Sereth's hands shook as she set out marrow hooks.

"Don't fight," she said. "If this fails, we both die."

I tried to answer and could only choke on leather.

They painted my spine with prayer ink and started the extraction chant. The first hook went between vertebrae and lit my whole body with pain. I smelled clove oil, hot wax, blood, and burnt marrow. The Origin Sigil fought as she pulled. She pulled harder.

The clerk asked for legal grounds. Mother pressed her seal into wax and declared me unstable and expendable. The witnesses approved. That was enough to make theft into law.

When the sigil thread tore free, Sereth flinched like she'd touched live wire. They set the shard in her basin. They burned my transfer writ before the ink dried.

She looked at me once.

"I'll carry it better than you," she said.

I spat blood at her boots.

Before dawn, Mother signed a debt conversion order and sold me to a Debtbound convoy for thirty-two ashmarks.

They took my House ring. They crossed my name out in inheritance ledgers. By sunrise I was chained in a transport wagon with quarry thieves and deserters.

The lead warden read my intake slate like comedy.

"Former High House. No sigil. Bad spine. Good ritual stock."

His men laughed every time he said former.

I memorized their faces anyway.

When the wagon rolled out under the bridge arches, no one from my family came to watch. One old kitchen worker made a ward sign as we passed. Everyone else stayed busy with grain tallies and permit stamps.

Losing the sigil felt physical, like a missing tooth you keep touching with your tongue, except the gap was in my chest and spine.

The city didn't pause for it. Bells still rang. Markets still opened. Clerks still stamped bread permits while I became collateral on a slate.

That was the hour revenge turned from feeling into plan.

I listed what I still had: one torn shirt, one split lip, no core, and a clear memory of that room. Where each witness stood. Who looked away. Who didn't.

At the first toll post, I made two decisions.

No law written by my family meant anything to me now.

If power belonged to people willing to take it, I would learn the price better than they did.
