Debtbound caravans are not prisons. They are rolling balance sheets.

By sunrise after my sale, a quartermaster with a split thumbnail walked the wagon line and priced us like damaged tools.

"Back strength, eight ashmarks. Ritual blood, twelve. Fever, burn after sale. Missing fingers, discount."

He said it in that bored market voice clerks use when they don't think the goods understand language.

When he reached me, he looked down at the intake slate twice.

"Former Veyr," he read. "No sigil. Spinal extraction trauma."

He scratched a note and added, almost pleased, "Expensive risk."

Then he stamped PIT TRANSFER across my line in red wax.

I learned two things before first bell.

First, Debtbound transport law allows wardens to kill inventory if treatment costs exceed projected yield. The phrase on the slate is burn after sale, which sounds cleaner than the smell.

Second, people who have been stripped to collateral still bargain. They bargain with teeth, with lies, with prayers, with each other. A deserter with a burned ear offered me two days of ration broth for my blanket strip. A quarry thief traded me a rusted buckle for one clean sip of water. A woman with wire scars on both wrists sold directions to a border ditch she swore had no patrols for three cinders she did not have.

No one asked my name.

By noon the rain started. Industrial runoff from the upper canals pooled in the wagon ruts and turned them the color of old tea. Horses slipped. Chains snapped tight around ankles every time a wheel hit stone. Each jolt drove pain through the seam where Sereth had pulled my Origin from my spine.

Pain has different voices.

This one was bright and narrow and personal.

At every stop, wardens did what institutions call maintenance. They checked chain points, counted living inventory, and chalked body conditions against projected value. At the third halt a levy clerk lifted my chin with the tip of his stylus and wrote: vomiting, low stability, likely non-compliant.

He was right about two out of three.

At dusk they fed us grain water thin enough to see the bottom of the tin and a half-hand of salt crackers hard enough to break bad teeth. The deserter on my left dipped his cracker in the runoff ditch to soften it.

"Where are you from?" he asked through swollen lips.

"Here," I said.

He gave a short laugh that turned into a cough.

"Nobody from here says here."

I didn't answer.

He watched me anyway, the way hungry men watch locked bread.

"Pit caravans route through Cinder Gate," he said quietly. "Marrow pits after that. They don't keep men with bad backs long."

He tapped his own throat.

"They keep blood and hands."

I believed him. I had seen quarry books as a child when Father made me audit extraction percentages and injury spoilage rates. Those ledgers used three columns for people: intake, yield, disposal.

House Veyr called that logistical realism.

On the wagon floor, with chain grit under my fingernails and rainwater in my boots, it looked like murder with accounting marks.

By second night my extraction wound had opened through the bandage rag. Every rut lit my vision white for half a second. I started mapping guard behavior between pain spikes because pattern is cheaper than panic.

Guard one checked left wrist locks every hour and never looked at ankles.

Guard two carried keys on a loose ring and scratched his neck before every count.

Guard three drank from a flask at third watch and slurred cinder totals to the clerk.

The quartermaster walked all counts himself and never stepped within chain reach.

He also liked to lecture.

"Cinder Throne law is mercy," he announced to no one in particular while rain ran off his hood. "House criminals get labor terms instead of temple rope."

He kicked a dead man off the back axle before finishing the line.

"That is civilization."

Nobody laughed.

At third watch the convoy halted under a broken toll arch to reseat a wheel pin. Wardens staked the chain ring to an axle post and posted two men with pikes near the lantern stack. The rest moved upslope to argue about mud depth and horse rotation.

The deserter leaned close enough that I could smell fever on him.

"If you have a trick," he whispered, "do it before gate dawn."

I looked at the chain bite on my wrist. Iron had eaten skin to pink. Rain thinned the blood and ran it down my palm.

I had no core.

No formal channels.

No legal protections left to exploit.

I still had tongue, blood, and memory of how vows bite when spoken correctly.

Father had once called emergency oathcraft a butcher's discipline. Useful when roads fail, unclean when used often, and always more expensive than men believe.

"Never cast what you can't pay for," he told me in the map room, tapping a trade lane with two fingers. "And never name a price you won't survive."

I counted what survival required.

If I reached Cinder Gate in irons, I died in pits.

If I cast too large without a core, I died in transit.

If I cast too small, I burned one year and still died chained.

The math left one viable line.

Sixty seconds of controlled blood-fire.

One year of life.

One chance.

I picked a tin shard from the wagon seam, put it between my molars, and waited until the rain covered the sound of my breathing.

Then I bit hard inside my cheek until blood flooded my tongue and copper filled my throat.

Pain sharpened the world.

I pressed the shard under my tongue, carved a short line, and felt heat wake in my jaw before the words even formed.

"One year," I whispered into blood. "One minute. Chains first."

The vow answered immediately.

Not with light.

With age.

My knuckles stiffened. A crack ran through my right wrist like old timber settling. Something in my knees ached as if I had walked ten winters in one breath. Then heat climbed out of my chest and up my throat, iron-red and hungry.

First push failed.

Flame coughed and died against wet links.

Second push spat and blistered skin but did not take the metal.

Third push held.

I exhaled between my wrists. Chain rings glowed dull orange, then softened around the rivet seam. I twisted before they cooled, skinned half my palm, and came free on one side.

The deserter stared at my hands like they belonged to someone else.

"Move," I said.

He didn't.

People freeze in exactly the moment where hope becomes logistics.

The nearest alarm rune on the axle post flickered awake.

I swung the loose chain into guard one's throat and pulled until he dropped his pike. Guard two turned with a shout and drove steel through my left shoulder. I burned him off by reflex, a narrow ribbon of red that caught his sleeve, then beard, then oilcloth mantle. He ran three steps and fell against the lantern stack.

Lanterns toppled.

Tar cloth lit.

Horses screamed and snapped traces.

Fire on wet canvas smells wrong, sweet and rotten at once.

The quartermaster shouted count commands from upslope, still trying to manage the event as inventory loss. Someone screamed for water. Someone else screamed for keys. I cut a harness line, kicked a burning pole out from under the wheel frame, and shoved heat into axle grease until the hub burst like split marrow.

The deserter finally moved, but the wrong direction. He ran uphill and vanished into rain and crossfire. I never saw him again.

I crawled off the wagon under smoke and ditch water with my shoulder leaking through three fingers and my tongue swelling enough to make breathing noisy.

In the churned mud beside a dead warden, I found what the city would call fortune.

A coat that still held warmth.

A cheap knife.

Ten cinders in a stitched purse.

A folded warrant packet sealed in temple black.

I took all four.

Behind me, the convoy burned in patches. Not one clean blaze, but dozens of ugly little fires eating tarps, rope, ledger sacks, and flesh by degree. Wardens cut chains on wagons they could still salvage and left the rest to smoke.

I moved on hands and knees until brush swallowed the road light.

By dawn I reached tree line north of the route with mud in my mouth and my own blood dried black under both nails. The shoulder wound had started stiffening around splinters of cloth. Every fifty steps I had to stop and listen for dogs.

None came.

At first I thought I had outpaced them.

Then I unfolded the warrant packet and understood why.

My name was already there.

CAEL VEYR.

Kin-traitor designation pending ratification.

Consecrated fugitive under Cinder Throne transport statute.

Recapture priority: red.

Disposition on resistance: execution authorized.

Sereth had moved fast.

Or Mother had.

Or both.

I burned the top half of the warrant and kept the lower half with the law language. Knowing exactly what powers they granted themselves felt useful in the same way poison labels feel useful.

At midday I found a culvert wall where refugees had scratched route hints in nail marks and charcoal.

GLOAM MARKET.
QUARRY CONTRACTS.
PAY IN BONE.
NO CREDIT AT NIGHT.

The last line had been carved deeper than the rest.

I sat under the culvert and rewrapped my shoulder with strip cloth torn from the dead guard's lining. My hands shook while I tied the knot. Not fear. Post-cast tremor and blood loss, plus the one-year bite still moving through joints like cold sand.

This is the part people misunderstand about lifespan casting.

You do not wake with white hair and a wise face.

You feel subtraction.

A year missing from recovery.

A year missing from how quickly hands settle.

A year missing from how long you can stay angry before exhaustion sits down on your chest and refuses to leave.

I tested the edge of a minor concealment phrase and stopped before activation. Without a core, even tiny work drew pain into the wound seam. I would not get a second major cast today and remain vertical.

New math.

One year spent.

One shoulder puncture.

One opened extraction seam.

Ten cinders.

No safe shelter.

No allies.

A city that now paid five hundred ashmarks for my live capture.

I started walking south toward District Nine because debt surgeons ask fewer legal questions than temple clinics and because movement is harder to track than grief.

At the outer toll arch a clerk in a waxed hood shouted entry rates from under canvas:

"Pedestrian, two cinders. Freight, variable. Corpses half-rate if wrapped."

He said it the same way our quartermaster had said inventory values.

Different office. Same grammar.

I paid two cinders with a hand that did not quite stop shaking and passed under the arch without eye contact. No one noticed me until I stumbled on the tax stone and left a blood print the size of a coin.

Then everyone noticed.

A tea seller looked up, saw the guard coat on my back, and looked away too fast.

A lane clerk tapped two fingers against his ledger spine, a signal pattern I knew from House audit corridors: mark and follow.

I cut right into steam, left through tannery stink, then under a rope bridge where dyed runoff turned the canal surface green-black.

By the time horns sounded behind me, I had three turns between us and no reserve if they forced a sprint.

I made shelter in an abandoned kiln annex with a broken roof and one dry corner near stacked ash barrels.

There, by lamp stub and rain noise, I opened the dead guard's pocket notebook and tore out six blank pages.

Father used to say routes fail faster when the person running them refuses to write down losses.

So I wrote mine.

Cast Ledger. Entry One.

Tongue-vow blood-fire.

Declared cost: one year.

Duration held: approximately one minute.

Result: chains softened, two wardens dead, convoy disrupted, escape achieved.

Immediate bodily effects: joint stiffness, hand tremor, tongue laceration, shoulder puncture worsening under exertion.

Strategic consequence: Cinder Throne fugitive designation active. Execution authorized on resistance.

I sat with the page for a long time after ink dried.

Then I added one more line because I had learned enough law to know institutions are not the only systems that erase people.

Personal note: no one from House Veyr came to watch transport departure.

I folded the page and slid it into my coat lining beside the broken half of the warrant.

Outside, district bells marked last trade hour. Somewhere farther in, market voices rose and fell over cinder prices, permit fraud, and bread weights. The city had already metabolized what happened on that road.

The wagon burned.

Two wardens died.

A House son became transport loss.

Tomorrow it would be a line item in three separate books.

I lay back against ash barrels and watched rain leak through cracked roof beams in thin silver threads.

This was the first night I understood revenge could not be an emotion if I wanted it to survive contact with logistics.

Emotion burns hot and dies.

Plans travel.

At dawn I would enter Gloam Market with eight cinders, one failing shoulder, and a surgeon's name I had heard once in a transport whisper.

Nara Quill.

If she took coin, I could buy two days.

If she took favors, I could buy fewer.

If she refused me, I could die legally by noon.

I slept in my boots with the knife in my sleeve and the ledger page under my tongue like a second vow.
