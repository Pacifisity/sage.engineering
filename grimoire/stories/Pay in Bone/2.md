Debtbound caravans are not prisons. They are rolling auctions.

On day two, a quartermaster walked the wagon line with a slate and stylus, pricing us like freight.

"Strong back, eight ashmarks. Ritual blood, twelve. Missing fingers, discount. Fever, burn after sale."

When he reached me, he frowned.

"Former Veyr. Bad spine. Expensive risk."

He marked me for pit transfer and moved on.

We traveled under warden escort through rain and industrial runoff. At night they chained us around axle posts and fed us thin grain broth. A deserter beside me whispered that the next stop was the marrow pits outside Cinder Gate.

I would not survive those pits.

By day three my back was failing. Sereth's extraction scar reopened every time the wagon hit a rut. I started tracking guard habits anyway: who got bored, who checked locks properly, who carried keys loose.

At fourth watch I used the only rite I could still force without a core: a tongue-vow.

I bit my cheek until blood filled my mouth, cut a line in my tongue with a scrap of tin, and made the trade clear.

One year of life for one minute of controlled blood-fire.

First attempt failed. Second burned. Third held.

Heat ripped down my throat. I exhaled at my shackles and the chain links glowed, then softened. I freed one wrist, then the other.

"Move," I told the deserter next to me.

He froze.

The alarm rune started lighting. I hit the nearest guard with the loose chain, grabbed his throat, and kept pulling until he dropped. The second guard speared my shoulder before I burned him off me. Horses panicked. Lanterns fell. Tarps caught fire.

I cut harness lines, shoved flame under axle grease, and crawled into the ditch while the convoy burned behind me.

A dead guard's coat gave me ten cinders, a cheap knife, and a folded warrant list.

My name was already on it, marked recapture priority, execution on sight approved.

By dawn I made the tree line north of the road, bleeding through the coat sleeve. Small spells almost blacked me out. Without a core, even concealment felt like dragging wire through nerves.

I moved in short blocks: fifty steps, stop, listen, move.

Near noon I reached a culvert where refugees had scratched directions into stone.

GLOAM MARKET.
QUARRY CONTRACTS.
PAY IN BONE.

I laughed until I coughed blood.

The vow had worked, but my body felt older in a single night. Hands shaking. Joints stiff. Mouth raw where I cut the line.

Still, I was no longer cargo.

I washed in ditch water, wrapped the spear wound, and headed for the city edge where debt surgeons worked with fewer questions than temple clinics.

At the district toll arch, a clerk shouted rates.

"Pedestrian, two cinders. Freight, variable. Corpses, half-rate if wrapped."

No one noticed me until I stumbled and left blood on the tax stone.

Then everyone noticed.

Gloam Market opened before sunrise and started cheating people before first light.

By the time I crossed the district line, every lane was loud: ration flour, fake seals, stolen relic metal, lamp oil sold as medicine. Nothing had one price. Nothing stayed honest for long.

I was bleeding through a dead guard's coat and leaving drops when I walked.

A tea seller saw the blood and looked away. A clerk with a scarred chin signaled down the lane. I turned before he could follow and cut through a tannery gate where the steam hid scent.

Nara Quill's clinic sat in a backroom behind hide racks and sealed crates.

Her assistant tried to throw me out until he saw the extraction scar on my back.

"Get Nara," he said.

She arrived already gloved and put water on to boil without asking for my name.

"Who did this?"

"Family," I said.

She didn't waste time on sympathy. She cleaned the wound, reopened bad stitches, and threaded temporary sigil lines to keep the damaged channels from tearing wider.

It hurt enough that I nearly blacked out twice.

Halfway through, she asked the question that mattered.

"Who has your Origin now?"

"Sereth Veyr."

Nara's jaw tightened. "Then this city is in trouble."

When she finished, she pushed a fee slate at me.

Six ashmarks. One vial clotting agent. Two favors.

I put ten cinders on the table and the dead guard's knife beside them.

She took the cinders, pushed the knife back, and crossed out one favor.

"You still owe me one major favor and supplies," she said. "Three medicine crates were seized at Warden Yard Seventeen. Bring them back and I keep treating you. Fail, and find another surgeon before your spine gives out."

I nodded. We both understood this was a contract, not trust.

Outside, market bells hit third trade hour and warden sweeps started.

At brazier square, Sereth's face appeared in public flame script.

"Cael Veyr is a fugitive and murderer. Harboring him is treason. Reward for live capture."

Five hundred ashmarks lit up under her name.

The crowd went still.

A fish seller angled a stall mirror so I could see two men behind me in civilian coats and warden boots. I tipped the brazier stand, vanished into dye smoke, and ran until the shouting got distant.

Under an aqueduct, I checked Nara's stitches and the warrant list from the guard I killed. My name sat near the top.

No safehouse. No faction. One debt already due.

I burned the warrant from the corner and watched my own name curl black first.

By dusk I had one workable plan.

Steal Nara's crates from Warden Yard Seventeen.

Buy time with medicine.

Keep moving.

You don't notice memory loss when it starts.

You notice it when you reach for something familiar and find nothing.

I watched Warden Yard Seventeen all morning from a roof across the canal.

Two patrol shifts. One clerk cycle. One blind corner where lime carts entered. Nara's three medicine crates sat under red tarp near the rear gate.

I couldn't brute-force this. No core, bad stitches, low blood.

At dusk I moved through mirror alley behind the yard and cast a minor concealment veil.

With a core, that veil would cost a cut and focus phrase.

Without one, it cost memory.

I sliced my palm, drew a spiral on dirty glass, and whispered the anchor line.

The veil took.

Cold rolled through my head. I crossed the service gate while two wardens argued about ration fraud. I cut the tarp, grabbed the crates, and almost got clear before one guard looked straight at the space I was standing in.

I pushed the veil harder.

Pain hit late, sharp and wrong, like cloth tearing inside my skull.

I dragged the crates through a lime chute into tannery lane. The alarm horn blew behind me. My shoulder reopened before I reached Nara's back door.

Her assistant hauled the crates in. I hit the floor.

Nara checked the seals, then looked down at me.

"What did you spend?"

"Not much," I said.

Lie.

Hours later, on a cot behind the hide racks, I tried to remember my father's voice.

I had his face. I had the way his hands moved over a map.

I did not have his voice.

I sat up too fast and tore a stitch.

Nara came in, fixed the seam, and watched me longer than usual.

"If you're casting blind without a core," she said, "you need to track the losses. Not tomorrow. Now."

In the morning she handed me dressings and spoke more softly.

"This kind of loss isn't neat. It starts small, then spreads. Be honest with me if it gets worse."

I nodded and didn't promise.

That night I opened a second ledger beside the vengeance list.

Entry one: Father's voice, gone.

He cut my purse with a clean, practiced move, then looked annoyed when he found only bandage cloth and two cinders.

I caught his wrist before he could run.

Thin arm. Old bruises. Hunger rash at the neck. Twelve, maybe.

"Name," I said.

He tried to bite me.

"Name."

"Yarrow."

Market traffic kept moving like none of this existed.

I should have let him go.

Instead I bought two stale rye rolls and handed him one.

"Why?" he asked.

"Because you got close," I said.

I needed eyes wardens ignored, and he needed someone to pay him before someone else owned him.

By afternoon he had mapped routes I'd missed: three side alleys, a smuggler stair, and a drainage run under Warden Yard Eleven. Fast, sharp, and careful with details.

"Coin or protection?" he asked.

"Both if the work is clean."

He gave a short laugh. "Nothing is clean."

First courier run was at dusk. No names on the note. No repeated route.

He came back before full dark with Nara's reply hidden in his boot and someone else's blood on his sleeve.

"Patrol stopped me," he said. "I lied. One hit me. I ran."

His jaw was already swelling.

"If they corner you, drop the run," I said.

He shook his head. "Then the lane dies."

No drama in his voice. Just street math.

I took him to Nara.

She taped his ribs, cleaned the cut on his lip, then pulled me aside while he dozed against a crate.

"You are using him," she said.

"So is the city," I said. "At least with me he gets fed and paid."

"That's still not safety."

Outside, ward horns signaled a bounty sweep. New posters named me and listed known associates as accessories.

Nara folded one and put it in my hand.

"If he stays with you, he's marked."

I looked at Yarrow sleeping with one hand inside his coat like he expected theft even in his sleep.

"If he leaves," I said, "he doesn't make winter."

Nara didn't argue. She didn't agree either.

Over the next days he became essential: route checks, gate counts, courier drops, patrol pattern memory. He also saw the parts of me I didn't like seeing: threats, pressure, hard choices made fast.

On the fourth night he asked, "Are you trying to fix this place, or just survive it?"

I said, "Today, survive."

He nodded like that answer matched what he'd already guessed.

In my second ledger I wrote:

Entry two: Yarrow in network.

Then I added one more line.

Keep him alive.

The Salt Lantern looked respectable from the river side.

The cellar told the truth.

Tamsin Rook handled deals under the casks, away from public tables. The room smelled like lamp oil, brine, and polished metal. Hook racks lined the beams. Nothing in there suggested mercy.

I came in with Yarrow behind me.

Tamsin studied my coat, my posture, the way I protected my left side.

"House Veyr dropped you hard," she said.

"You have a route or not?" I asked.

She slid a slate forward.

One week off-grid passage. Two clean lanes. Forty ashmarks.

I put two cinders on the slate.

She tapped the second line.

"Alternate payment. One living body with equivalent debt value."

Yarrow shifted beside me. I kept my eyes on Tamsin.

"I can pay in information," I said. "Checkpoint timings. Patrol blind spots."

"Information turns stale," she said. "Collateral doesn't."

A minute later, her guards dragged in a bounty hunter bleeding through both hands.

I knew him from my convoy escape. He knew me too.

"Help me," he gasped. "I'll split the reward."

Tamsin looked from him to me.

"He's breathing," she said.

I had options on paper. In real time, I had one.

"Take him," I said.

Tamsin stamped wax, signed route clearance, and handed me a marked token.

No speech. No judgment. Just transaction.

Outside in rain, Yarrow finally spoke.

"Was he dead already?"

"No."

"Then now he is because of us."

"Because of me," I said.

We boarded her barge before dawn, hoods down, lanterns masked. The hull rode low with contraband grain and shrine brass. Ward towers swept the opposite bank with mirror lamps while we drifted under fog.

Yarrow kept the route token in his fist even when he slept.

At first light we reached a mud dock beyond direct warden authority. Tamsin's runner gave me a red-notched strip.

"Blood marker," he said. "When she calls, you answer."

I tucked it in my sleeve.

I had bought passage and time.

Both came with debt.
