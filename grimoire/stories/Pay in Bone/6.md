People get broken young and useful under older hands.

I wrote that at dawn and spent the rest of the day proving I meant it.

Late summer in District Nine has a particular rot to it. Heat sits low over the gutters, turns fish scales silver-black in the drains, and ferments every argument into knife work by noon. Coin moves faster than mercy. Names move faster than coin.

By second bell, three different runners told me the same thing.

Warden captains were posting fresh sweep schedules around the kiln lanes.

My courier cuts were about to close.

If I wanted Yarrow alive another month, I needed a protected route out of the district for people and medicine. That meant smugglers, and smugglers meant the Salt Lantern.

The Lantern sat under a collapsed countinghouse where the river wall bowed inward and never dried. Officially it was a tavern with bad stew and honest dice. Actually it was a contract pit with lantern smoke, prayer fraud, and enough armed neutrality to keep deals alive until dawn.

You entered through the kitchen ash hatch and descended three flights into heat that smelled of lamp oil, pickled brine, and old blood scrubbed with cheap soap.

Hanging jars of tongues lined the back beam above the main table. Some looked animal. Some didn't.

House cautionary theater, I assumed.

No one corrected me.

Tamsin Rook ran the room from a chair with one short leg and a ledger twice the width of her chest. Gray coat, clean nails, eyes like a debt collector pretending to be bored. She looked at me once and smiled with exactly one side of her mouth.

"Former Veyr," she said. "You look less expensive than your posters." 

"You look exactly as expensive as your reputation," I said.

"Flattery is for priests. Sit." 

I sat.

She slid a slate across the table.

PASSAGE TERMS. DUSK RUN. TWO NIGHTS COVER.

Price: forty ashmarks.

Or one breathing body suitable for Guild transfer.

I read it twice because sometimes seeing the words clearly delays becoming the kind of man who says yes.

"Forty ashmarks buys a bridge," I said.

"Then buy a bridge." She folded her hands. "You came for a river lane and uninspected manifests. That's not transport. That's immunity." 

Behind me, two men argued about counterfeit cinders while a shrine scribe weighed teeth on brass scales and called it arbitration. The Lantern's floor always sounded like low rain: bargains, threats, laughter without trust.

"What kind of body?" I asked.

Tamsin's expression did not shift.

"Breathing. Not contagious. No open gangrene. Hands intact." She tapped one line with her pen. "I am not buying a corpse. I have uses for live labor that don't involve sentiment."

"You trade people like carts."

"And you route children through kill lanes," she said. "Let's not perform outrage at each other. It wastes lantern oil." 

There it was.

Judgment, concise and accurate, delivered as market language.

I noticed it and didn't care enough to pretend otherwise.

"I can deliver by tomorrow," I said.

"By sunrise," she corrected. "Sweep windows shift after dawn."

"I need two routes, not one."

"Then bring a body worth the trouble." She closed the slate. "And if you're thinking about burning your way out of this negotiation, don't. The man pouring your tea is carrying a river dart under his sleeve." 

I didn't turn to look.

No point insulting professionals by verifying they were professionals.

On the way out I passed a basin near the stair where rainwater gathered from cracked stone. My reflection in it lagged half a breath behind me, then caught up.

When I moved on, the basin face kept watching for one step too long.

Growing Black Weight, I wrote in my head. Still-water response.

No voice. Not yet.

By midnight I had my breathing body.

He'd been tracking me since the Debtbound road, one of the auxiliary hunters paid by seizure bounty percentages instead of salary. We caught him in a dye alley with a hook line, a brick, and Yarrow's patience.

His name was Oren Pell.

I knew because he kept saying it between blood coughs like repetition might preserve legal personhood.

"Oren Pell," he wheezed. "Licensed recovery. Not warden." 

He had a puncture under the ribs from Yarrow's second strike and a lung already filling. Fever heat sat under his skin. Breath came wet. Even with care he would not make two days.

Yarrow held the knife steady and looked at me for orders.

"Do we finish it?" he asked.

I looked at Pell. Then at Yarrow.

If we killed him, clean and quick, we gained safety and lost leverage.

If we kept him alive long enough to trade, we bought routes with a man who could still feel every minute of it.

The right answer and the useful answer were no longer related.

"Bind the wound," I said.

Yarrow hesitated, then obeyed.

"Why?" he asked quietly while wrapping cloth.

"Because breathing has value tonight."

He did not ask anything else.

At the clinic, Nara took one look at Pell on the table and cursed me in a tone she usually saved for broken sterilizers and temple auditors.

"No," she said before I spoke. "Absolutely not." 

"Stabilize him twelve hours," I said. "I can pay."

"Pay who?" She pointed at Pell's chest where the breath rattle sat ugly and loud. "Him? Me? Your conscience?"

"Nara."

"Don't use my name like a favor." 

She checked Pell anyway, fingers quick at pulse and pupil.

"Punctured lung. Septic edge. He dies with care or without it," she said. "Difference is where and how much pain." 

"Then give him less pain. And give me time."

Her mouth thinned.

"For what?"

"Passage contract." 

"With whom?"

I did not answer fast enough.

"Tamsin," she guessed. "Of course." 

She turned away, pulled thread and wash from a shelf, then stopped and faced me again.

"If I keep him breathing, what do I buy?" she asked.

"Coin," I said. "Whatever you want." 

She laughed once, no amusement in it.

"I remember how you take tea now," she said. "Two bitterleaf drops, no sweet, wait till it cools before the first sip. That is how often you end up on my table." 

She set the needle tray down harder than needed.

"I don't need your coin, Cael. I need something you keep refusing to spend." 

"Name it."

"Three escort nights for my medicine carts through Ward Two choke points. No substitutions. No route excuses. You personally verify the lanes are clean before wheels move." 

"That's too much for one dying bounty hunter."

"Then let him die and find another surgeon." 

Yarrow looked between us, reading the room like a weather chart.

I could feel time narrowing around Pell's breathing.

I made the choice.

"Done," I said.

"Say it clearly." 

"Three escort nights. I clear and verify Ward Two lanes myself." 

She held my gaze one beat, then nodded and went to work.

She inserted a reed tube, drained blood from the lung, stitched where stitching still mattered, and gave Pell enough poppy wash to blunt the panic without blunting consciousness. He screamed once anyway when the pressure shifted and then bit through his own lip to keep quiet.

"You're buying him hours," she said while tying off thread. "Not miracles." 

"Hours are what I asked for." 

"No," she said. "Hours are what you're willing to call humane now." 

I had no counter that wasn't theater.

By predawn we moved Pell through a canvas cart with funeral markings and delivered him to the Lantern service ramp.

Tamsin's men checked his pulse, opened one eyelid, and made him squeeze fingers on command. He managed two.

"Transfer-grade," the handler said.

Tamsin signed the contract and pressed her ring seal into black wax.

"You look sick," she said to me, watching Pell being rolled inside. "Regret, or infection?"

"Route terms," I said.

"Ah. Regret." 

She handed me a folded map skin and two clay tokens stamped with Lantern marks.

"One river barge lane. One ash causeway lane. Two nights each, if you don't burn my crews." 

"I need longer." 

"Then do better business next time." 

She leaned back and studied my face the way Nara studied wound margins.

"Someone will mention a defrocked rite-brother to you soon," she said. "Name's Halvek. Talks like scripture and debt law had a child. If he offers structure, take it. Men in your condition collapse without architecture." 

First vague reference. Casual as weather.

I filed it away.

On the climb out, the stairwell had a copper mirror nailed to the wall so patrons could check for blood before they returned to street daylight.

I looked up and saw my own mouth shape words one breath early.

No sound. Just movement.

When I blinked, it matched again.

I stopped using that stair after.

The next two days were logistics and proof.

I walked Nara's cart lanes myself, marked sniper corners, paid two bribe booths, threatened one shrine collector, and moved twelve crates of clot wash and fever tincture through Ward Two without seizure.

At the third escort night she met me in the clinic back room with hot tea already poured.

Two bitterleaf drops. No sweet. Cooled exactly enough.

"I kept my side," I said.

"So far," she answered.

I tried to thank her.

She cut me off with a raised hand.

"Don't," she said, uncomfortable with softness even when it was true. "Save gratitude for a future where we can afford it." 

Her fingers brushed my scar seam while changing dressing, clinical and careful. Scar tissue had tightened down my side into a ridge that pulled when I breathed deep. Under lamp light the line looked less like a wound and more like bad stitching on a torn flag.

I was beginning to look like a man assembled from failed recoveries.

Yarrow came in with route sheets and waited until Nara left the room.

"Where did Pell go?" he asked.

"To work," I said.

He studied my face as if trying to decide whether that answer was operational or moral.

"Is he alive?"

"When I traded him, yes." 

He nodded and put the sheets down.

No lecture. No comfort.

Just data accepted.

That night rain hit hard enough to drown district bells. In a puddle outside the kiln alcove, I saw my reflection open its mouth before thunder, like it already knew the sound coming.

I stepped over it and kept walking.

At first light I wrote the ledger entry and did not edit the line that made me look worst.

Entry Five.

Late summer, month nine from exile.

Salt Lantern contract secured through live-transfer collateral (Oren Pell, licensed bounty auxiliary, critical condition).

Assets gained: two protected movement lanes, temporary manifest immunity, smuggler token access.

Secondary outcome: Nara extracted three escort nights through Ward Two in exchange for emergency stabilization. Obligation fulfilled.

Operational impact: courier survivability increased; clinic supply continuity stabilized.

Personal note: Tamsin now owns me. Or I own the bounty hunter's corpse. Same difference.

I sanded the ink, folded the page, and slid it under the others.

By noon, wardens posted a new notice at bridge markets.

All carts crossing Ashbridge would now be searched under emergency collapse prevention law.

The line at the notice board went silent when they read the clause out loud.

I watched faces do math and understood what came next.

If Ashbridge locked, three districts starved.

If I hit Ashbridge, people died.

There are moments where strategy arrives disguised as a threat and asks whether you're willing to become exactly what you said you hated.

I sent for Yarrow.

We left before dark.
