The catacombs under Saint Vellum were older than the city and meaner than it.

We used the lower tunnels as transit because wardens avoided them unless ordered. Too many collapses, too many stories about voices from sealed galleries. Good conditions for moving medicine, contraband, and people no registry should see.

That night we were running three crates of clotting tincture and one child fever pack toward Dock Nine triage when crossbow fire hit from both side branches at once.

Not random patrol.

Prepared kill box.

First bolt took our rear carrier through the neck before anyone drew breath. Second pinned a crate against stone and shattered half our vials. We pulled into alcove cover while dust rained from ceiling seams with each impact.

Yarrow was two paces from open line when I grabbed his coat and slammed him into the wall beside me.

"Stay low," I said.

"They're moving left tunnel," he whispered, voice steady by force.

He was right. Boots in side passage. More than one team. If they closed both exits, we either died in place or surrendered to a regime that counted us as reusable examples.

Halvek, bleeding from a graze over his eye, crawled to the map marks scratched into the floor from prior runs.

"There is a collapse seam here," he said, touching the right-hand support rib. "Trigger it and this corridor dies."

"With us in it?"

"Not if anchor holds long enough to push shock outward."

Anchor.

Living anchor.

We had one warden alive. Yarrow had dragged him in from the first exchange and broken his wrist to keep him quiet. He lay against the wall spitting blood and curses, too dazed to understand the math yet.

I understood it immediately and wished I didn't.

If we used him, the collapse would seal pursuit and give us the minute we needed to escape through the side vent. If we didn't, the teams behind us would close and we'd lose everyone in this corridor, including Yarrow.

The warden saw my face and went pale.

"No," he said. "No, no, no."

I knelt in front of him anyway.

"What is your name?" I asked.

He blinked, confused by the question. "Pell."

I almost stopped there.

Then another bolt hit stone inches from Yarrow's hand and snapped that hesitation in half.

I pinned Pell to the seam with iron stakes from the crate braces and opened the collapse rite through the scar lattice. He screamed for water, for mercy, for his mother, all three in one breath. The ceiling answered with a low stone groan.

"Move!" I shouted.

We ran the side vent as the main tunnel folded behind us. Stone thunder. Dust wave. Silence where pursuit had been.

Outside the vent mouth, I vomited until nothing came up but bile and blood. Yarrow stood three feet away, staring at my hands, not speaking.

We made triage by dawn with half supplies and no escort carrier.

Nara unpacked the surviving vials and then looked at me hard enough to split skin.

"What did you do?"

"Got us out," I said.

"That is not the same as answer."

I did not give her one.

For three nights after, I dreamed the same scene wrong. In the dream, Pell was always asking for water in my father's voice. I woke with my hand closed so tight around the cot frame I bent a nail.

Yarrow resumed route work without complaint. He also started cleaning blood from his boots before entering rooms, even when there was none visible.

In the ledger I wrote the line and stared at it until sunrise.

First living-anchor collapse.

Survival purchased with a named man.

When I closed the ledger, Yarrow was still awake by the door pretending to sort route slips.

"You didn't have another option," he said, not looking up.

"Maybe not," I said.

He nodded once, folded the papers into a neat stack, and left the room without asking anything else.

I told myself I was going back for reconnaissance.

That was partly true. The other part was punishment.

Ashbridge looked worse in daylight than in memory. The missing center span had turned the river crossing into a wound with camps built around it. Tarp roofs stitched from sailcloth. Fire pits fed by broken furniture. Children coughing gray phlegm into sleeves while adults argued over ration tokens worth less each week.

Trade had not adapted. It had rerouted through extortion.

Guild ferries now charged triple ashmarks per cart at upriver crossings. Warden checkpoints skimmed half the goods in "inspection fees." District Nine paid in hunger and called it temporary.

Yarrow and I moved the camp edges in plain labor clothes, counting supply flows, listening to queue anger. Every third conversation included my name. Not whispered now. Weighed.

At one tent line, a woman recognized me and did not scream. She stepped close enough that only we could hear.

"My son fell with that bridge," she said. "Tell me he died quick."

I had no answer she could live with.

"I'm sorry," I said.

She nodded once like she'd expected nothing better and walked away.

By dusk we had what we came for: map of refugee concentrations, warden collection points, and one vulnerable convoy route I could hit later if needed. We also had a problem.

A patrol unit had begun using mirror hounds to scan camp lanes for hidden couriers. They were crude constructs, fed by fear residue and blood traces left in crowded ground. If they keyed on our route marks, every medicine line through this camp would collapse within days.

I had one option: cloak our team trail by casting despair veil, a low Grim Cant technique that bends attention by feeding on ambient misery.

Nara would have called it predatory. She would have been right.

I cast anyway.

The veil swallowed us cleanly, but the cost hit strange. Not blood this time. Not lifespan. Perception.

For two hours after the cast, every scream in the camp sounded like it came from two places at once, one present and one remembered. I could not tell whether the crying child near the bridge stump was real until Yarrow touched my sleeve and pointed directly.

"There," he said. "Actual."

The distinction mattered more than I liked.

We exited the camp after nightfall and reached tannery safe lane without pursuit, mission technically successful. I should have felt relief.

Instead I carried camp dust in my throat and that woman's question in my ear.

At the clinic, Nara checked my pupils and frowned.

"What did you cast?"

"Veil."

"On what fuel?"

I did not answer quickly enough.

She set the lamp down harder than needed. "Do not use civilians as ambient battery and call yourself strategic."

"I used conditions," I said.

"Conditions were people," she said.

No argument left after that.

Two days later, camp gossip hardened into organized resentment. Refugees had started calling my runners tax-crows and throwing stones at known courier marks. Not enough to stop operations. Enough to signal fracture.

I added the line to the ledger and did not soften it.

Ashbridge revisit. Intelligence gained. Human terrain degraded.

The handwriting looked older than my age.

Morn Hal requested parley under the broken bridge at moonless hour, which was either courage or arrogance depending on who wrote history.

He came with two escorts, unhelmed, hands visible. I came with Yarrow on overwatch and Irix somewhere I couldn't see but could feel like pressure in the air. Neutral ground in this city means everyone has an angle and no one pretends otherwise.

Hal looked older than the last time I saw him at the collapse, left cheek marked by a fine scar from stone spray. He held no visible weapon.

"You look worse," he said.

"You look employed," I answered.

He gave a short laugh and produced a sealed document case from under his coat.

"Straight offer. You stop hitting levy routes and disappear from district optics for one quarter. In return, we clear your active kill writ and reroute your skills as deniable asset outside city limits."

"You want to hire me."

"I want to stop wasting good violence on bad theater."

He set the document on a broken support block between us.

I did not touch it.

"Why now?" I asked.

Hal's jaw tightened. "Because your war and our response are grinding civilians into paste, and neither side has enough discipline to admit it."

It was the first honest sentence I'd heard from a warden officer in months.

Still no.

"Amnesty from the people who sold my body?" I said. "Keep it."

He nodded as if he expected that.

"Then keep the letter anyway," he said. "You'll want leverage when your own allies decide you're more useful dead."

That got my attention.

He slid the case forward. I took it this time.

Inside: formal offer with signature chain, two seal impressions, and route annotations for three planned sweeps in districts where Nara's clinics operated. Not all of it looked official. Some of it looked like Hal writing me information under legal cover.

"Why give me this?" I asked.

He looked past me toward the camp lights under Ashbridge stump.

"Because systems rot. Men improvise."

Then he stepped back and signaled his escorts.

"Next time we meet," he said, "it may not be with papers."

"Next time bring better terms."

He almost smiled and walked into dark.

I waited three minutes before opening the case again. One page held a secondary note in shorthand barely visible under lamp black.

WATCH YOUR MEDIC LINES. GUILD HAS BOUGHT TEMPLE INSPECTORS.

I folded the papers and tucked them into an oilskin sleeve inside my coat.

Back at the tannery, Nara read the warning and turned white around the mouth.

"If this is real, two of our routes are burned by tomorrow."

"Then move them tonight," I said.

We did. Barely in time. Dawn raids hit both old paths and found nothing but decoy carts full of stone and ash.

Irix whistled when he heard.

"You got intelligence from a warden captain and still think trust is binary," he said. "Impressive."

"I trust leverage," I answered.

He raised both hands as if conceding theology.

That night I logged the encounter with unusual care.

Morn Hal parley. Recruitment offer refused. Blackmail and warning assets acquired.

I slept with the document case under my cot and woke twice to check it was still there.
