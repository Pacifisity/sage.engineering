By winter turn, Sereth's theft was eating her from inside.

We saw it first in patrol reports: allied cousins disappearing from protected estates, marrow-house lights burning at forbidden hours, temple surgeons requisitioning blood salts in quantities fit for plague, not court medicine.

Then came the rumor no one wanted to repeat aloud: she drank kin blood before council sessions to steady the Sigil.

Rumor became evidence when Morn's old contacts delivered chambermaid testimony and disposal logs from House kitchens. Bone buckets marked with family seals. Night-wash cloths burned in private furnaces. A physician transferred and then found dead two days later in a bell tower stairwell.

Sereth was starving.

I exploited every fracture.

We hit two cousin caravans she had tagged for extraction and relocated survivors to hidden wards. We spread forged orders that rerouted her marrow teams into empty chapels. We paid hill mercenaries to stage false signals near old kin estates, forcing her to overspend guards and expose convoy lanes.

It worked at the level of pressure.

It did not give me the kill.

Attempt one came at a rain council in Lower Veyr Hall. We bribed a lamp custodian, seeded the rafters with three shooters, and timed shot lines to the moment she stood for oath response. She arrived late, changed podium position without warning, and left two body doubles bleeding where she should have been. We killed actors and a page boy.

Attempt two came at a marrow rite caravan crossing the north bridge. I rode with strike teams under smuggler flags, breached escort wagons, and reached the inner carriage in under ninety seconds.

She was there.

Thinner. Eyes too bright. Veins dark at the neck where resonance strain had spread.

She smiled when she saw me.

"Still chasing doors," she said, and triggered a scatter rite with her own blood on the carriage rail.

The blast threw me across cobbles and shattered hearing on my left side for two days. By the time smoke cleared, she was gone through a prepared under-bridge chute I had not known existed.

I killed her rear guard and came home with nothing except three severed sigil gloves and a cracked rib.

Nara stitched me in silence, then said, "You are both dying of the same hunger now."

"Mine is control," I said.

"No," she said. "Yours is her."

At council, Yarrow presented nightly loss tables with professional calm. House alliances around Sereth were unraveling exactly as predicted, but our own routes were also fraying under constant retaliation. Tactical advantage, no decisive conversion. The war dragged because both sides were now too invested in continuity to risk true collapse.

Halvek warned that starvation might make Sereth unpredictable enough to do what she had always refused: burn her own city sectors for resonance feed.

"If she crosses that line," he said, "there is no politics left. Only extraction."

I answered with deployment orders, not fear.

In private, I wrote this:

She is weakening and still untouchable.

The mirror beside my desk answered with her voice, not mine:

You taught me untouchable.

I smashed the mirror and spent an hour picking glass from my palm.

The solstice storm rolled in with green lightning and east wind strong enough to bend bell towers.

Perfect weather for concealment rites.

We had three critical convoys moving that night: medicine from river marsh, powder from hill principalities, and winter grain from Salt Lantern reserves. Cinder Throne watchfires controlled ridge sightlines; if those fires stayed active, all three convoys would be tracked and cut before dawn.

Halvek offered the numbers without decoration.

"District-wide surveillance veil will blind their watchfires for one night," he said. "Cost estimate: six years of lifespan at your current strain profile, plus one anchored joy-memory as harmonic fuel."

"Why joy?"

"Because grief burns too hot and rage fractures too fast. Joy stabilizes coverage."

I laughed once at the cruelty of ritual design and said yes.

We cast from Nine Chimneys roof under rain that felt like needles. Morn held perimeter. Yarrow ran signal relays. Nara stood at the stairwell with emergency draughts and the look of someone watching a man choose his own amputation.

I opened the veil with blood linework across my forearms, then fed the lattice through all nine chimneys and out along district ward-strands. The sky dimmed from iron gray to deep green-black. One by one, distant watchfires blinked and went blind.

At the center of the rite, Halvek asked for the memory anchor.

I reached for laughter.

There was a moment in my chest, warm and precise, a sound of someone laughing with me in a courtyard at dusk. For one second I could almost place it.

Then the ritual took it.

The warmth vanished. The sound vanished. The person vanished.

Only the shape of absence remained.

The veil held for seven hours.

All three convoys crossed untracked.

At dawn, the storm broke and the watchfires came back. I came down from the roof shaking so hard Yarrow had to take my arm. White streaks had appeared in my hair above both temples. My knees locked on stairs. My hands looked ten years older than they had at dusk.

Nara checked pulse, pupils, and joint response, then swore under her breath.

"You aged yourself into your own father in one night," she said.

"Did the convoys make it?"

"Yes."

"Then record success."

She nearly struck me. Instead she handed me a cup and said, "One day you will have nothing left to spend except other people."

I wanted to tell her that day had come already.

In council, I announced operational win and issued winter allotment increases made possible by surviving grain. Officers cheered. Quartermasters bowed. People who had not watched the rooftop rite called it brilliance.

Later, alone, I tried to remember whose laughter I had paid.

Nothing.

I checked old pages, cross-referenced names, traced dates where joy might have fit. No recovery. The anchor was gone clean.

In my private ledger, chapter fifty closed with three lines:

Six years paid.
One laughter erased.
War continues.

Outside, the city celebrated full granaries for one night.

Inside, my joints ached like old wood and my reflection looked like a warning I had ignored for too long.

The white streaks at my temples turned into rumor before breakfast.

By noon the markets had a name for me: Ash-Crowned. Recruiters painted it on wall slates. Street preachers called it a sign. Half the city wanted blessing. The other half wanted proof I could still bleed.

I turned myth into policy.

The White Hair Edict had six clauses and one deliberate deception.

Clause one banned debt transfers for children under fourteen.
Clause two through five built registry rules, witness standards, and penalties for violators.
Clause six preserved adult collateral contracts under wartime law.

The title promised mercy. The text delivered triage.

I read it in Chimney Court to a crowd packed shoulder to shoulder. Cheer when I reached the child clause. Fury when I reached adult retention.

"You saved our sons to chain our wives," a dockworker shouted.

Wrong wording. Correct structure.

I signed anyway.

By nightfall, parents in two wards thanked me in tears while labor crews in three wards threatened stoppage. Recruitment rose where people saw protection. Desertion rose where people saw extraction. Both reactions were rational.

Nara watched the signing from the stair rail, arms folded, saying nothing. Yarrow logged crowd leaders and slogan patterns as if this were weather.

After the ceremony, a woman pushed her son forward and whispered thanks. Ten steps later, an old rigger held out his ration card and asked if his debt now ended only when his body failed.

"Yes," I said.

He laughed once. "Then your white hair is just early grave ash."

In the ledger, the edict read as partial humanitarian gain and manageable unrest.

In private, I wrote the simpler version:

I cut one chain and tightened another.

Nara stopped me in a storage corridor between grain sacks and morphine crates.

"Selective mercy is still violence," she said, and handed me a damp list of names.

Adults sold this week to cover debt penalties, militia quotas, and ration arrears.

Renn Tal, rope maker, transferred to quarry labor.
Mira Sol, widow, bonded twelve years to convoy kitchens.
Daren Pike, black-lung, sold by family to clear fines.

By the fourth name I told her to stop.

She kept going until I took the paper.

"You wrote the child clause and called it reform," she said. "It is procurement with better language."

I answered with numbers: child trafficking down, registration compliance up, food routes steadier.

She did not argue the numbers.

"Surgeons cut to heal," she said. "You cut to keep a machine fed."

Rain hammered the loading roof. For a moment neither of us spoke.

I started to reach for her hand and stopped halfway. She saw it, then looked past me toward the clinic door.

"Do you know what scares me?" she asked.

"What?"

"That you still feel every cost and sign anyway. If you were cold, I could leave. You are not cold. You are committed."

I said the only honest thing left.

"I don't know how to win without this."

"Then winning is the wrong objective," she said.

She walked away with the remaining names.

I stood in that corridor until the lantern burned low, holding a policy that had saved children and sold adults in the same week.

In public, I called it wartime compromise.

In private, I called it what it was:

a tenderness I could no longer afford and could not stop wanting.

I built Chimney Court because fear without process eventually turns inward.

The court sat in the old turbine hall: three benches, one witness rail, one clerk pit, armed exits at both doors. Cases were public. Sentences were cinders, labor terms, restitution, or oath service.

Week one handled petty theft and forged ration slips.

Week two brought district stewards.

Lio's books showed ghost households drawing food. Yarrow's route logs proved deliveries never arrived. Irix produced two warehouse hands who swore under seal they had moved diverted crates at night.

I staged the trial like civic theater on purpose.

Both stewards were convicted. One was sentenced to supervised labor accounting for life. The other to oath-bound quarry intake with backlash clauses for falsified records.

Three nights later, both disappeared.

No broken locks. No ransom. No body.

Petty corruption dropped by half in ten days. Petitions dropped with it. People complied faster and trusted less.

Nara called it obedience to uncertainty.

"You do not have legitimacy," she said. "You have fear with clerks."

She was right, and the metrics still improved.

By month end, court throughput was high, black-market knife cases were down, and enlistment rose in wards that now feared internal punishment more than enemy raids.

In council I called that stabilization.

In private I wrote the clearer line:

Order is improving faster than justice.

The leak was too precise to ignore.

Every time we moved medicine routes, Sereth's scouts appeared near the slow points within hours. Not enough force to seize cargo. Just enough to bleed us and prove they knew.

Irix proposed a trap: one fake route, six slightly different copies, six recipients.

We seeded Blackmill Road as a high-priority surgical convoy with unique coordinate errors in each copy.

Ambush arrived at the coordinate from copy three.

Copy three had gone to a quartermaster clerk, an outer-watch sergeant, and Healer Sera Vonn from Nara's clinic team.

We detained all three before dawn.

The clerk broke first and named a Guild broker. The sergeant denied until Yarrow laid timestamped relay slips in front of him. Sera stayed silent until Nara sat down across from her.

"Why?" Nara asked.

"They took my brother," Sera said. "They sent fingers with his rings."

No one spoke for a while after that.

Irix recommended immediate execution. Nara asked for exile and witness posting. I postponed sentence one night and stared at route maps until morning, knowing delay would cost credibility and speed would cost whatever was left of us.

In the end the facts were simple: the leaks were real, the motive was coercion, and the dead on our roads did not care why the map was sold.

I entered the docket for sentencing at noon and wrote one private note beneath it:

Grief is now a weapon class.
