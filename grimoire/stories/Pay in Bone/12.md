Crowds make vows out of anything spoken loudly enough.

Halvek told me that before I let the dockworkers put my name into a chant.

I listened in the technical sense. I heard every word. I even understood him. Understanding and obedience had been living separate lives inside me for a long time.

Late summer had thinned toward autumn by then. The heat no longer sat on the district like a hand over the mouth, but it left a residue in the walls. Tithe Docks smelled of brine rot, tar, lamp smoke, and old embalming oil baked into planks from the canal fires months earlier. Even clean wind came in dirty there.

My hair had started showing gray at the temples.

Nara mentioned it once while changing the lattice dressing under my ribs.

"You look older every time you come in," she said.

"I am older every time I come in."

"You know what I mean."

I did. I ignored it with the discipline of a man keeping one more ledger from himself.

The Docks had become the place where every contradiction in the city came to ask for a signature. River crews wanted escort guarantees without fees. Labor gangs wanted ration priority without registry marks. Dock wives wanted their bonded husbands back from quarry shifts. Convoy masters wanted stable law from the same war machine they swore they still despised.

Everyone wanted relief.

No one wanted the cost column attached.

It started with a tax riot small enough to be dismissed by anyone not standing inside it.

Three tally clerks. One broken soup barrel. Two boys fighting over a dropped sack of barley while their mother bled from the mouth where a guard's baton had found her. By dusk the riot had become a prayer meeting, then a labor assembly, then a crowd deep enough that the front rows could not hear the back and the back rows still believed that meant hope.

They gathered under the dead cranes at Tithe Docks where rusted chain arms hung over the river like unfinished executions. Lanterns swung from hooks driven into old cargo beams. Someone had painted my name on sailcloth and nailed it over a customs arch. Someone else had painted below it:

BURN THE DEBTS OR BURN WITH THEM.

Yarrow read the exits before he read the faces. Two roofline shooters placed by us, one by someone else. Eight men in dock leathers carrying cudgels under fish cloaks. A shrine watcher pretending to beg by the east piling. He gave me all of it in under fifteen seconds and then asked the more dangerous question.

"Do you want the crowd hot or quiet?"

He meant managed by force or managed by promise.

"Quiet if possible," I said.

He nodded and vanished into the bodies, taking three runners with him and leaving the space around the platform just porous enough to feel uncontrolled and just structured enough to stop a crush.

Halvek stood beside the timber post with his coat buttoned wrong and one hand resting on the cane he refused to admit was now necessary after temple damage to the hip.

"If they ask for absolutes, do not answer in absolutes," he said.

"If I answer in fractions, they stop listening."

"Then perhaps they should. Crowds translate certainty into liturgy faster than priests do."

He was right.

Then the chants started.

Not war chants. Worse.

Administrative chants.

Open the ledgers.

Break the bonds.

Bread without chains.

You can fight armies. You can negotiate with smugglers. A crowd asking you to become the form of justice it already invented is harder to survive than either.

Dockmaster Pell Ren climbed the platform first, all scarred wrists and old rope-burn hands. I knew him by reputation: labor captain, strike organizer, survived three regime lashings and still spoke like a man doing the listener a favor by using short words.

He pointed not at me but at the crowd when he talked.

"They'll work for you," he said. "Stevedores, net crews, coal-haulers, boilermen, tally women, bridge mudders, half the drydock gangs if I tell them the terms are real."

"What terms?"

He smiled without softness.

"Cancel the bone promissories on dock households. Wipe quarry transfer penalties for families working ration shifts. End child-seizure clauses in arrears collections. Give us written passage rights to move food without temple inspection delays."

He might as well have asked for a new city.

The crowd understood enough law to know the scale of what was being demanded. That was why it had weight.

"I can't erase ledgers I don't hold yet," I said.

Murmurs. Anger immediately, because hunger has no patience for procedural truths.

Pell Ren did not let the crowd tip either way.

"Can you take them?" he asked.

That was the real question beneath everything.

Not Are you just?

Are you capable?

The Black Weight answered first, though not in a mirror this time. Just a pressure behind the eyes, that almost-clean sense of solution arriving stripped of reluctance.

Say yes now. Build the obligation later.

Halvek heard the pause and stepped closer.

"Careful," he said under his breath. "Promises to a crowd bind wider than you mean them to."

I should have let the silence stay. I should have answered with logistics, contingencies, committees, any of the cowardly accurate things that preserve optionality.

Instead I looked out at the dockwives, levy-scarred laborers, half-starved children asleep against rope coils, and men with quarry numbers burnt into the backs of their hands. I thought of Ashbridge still half broken. I thought of ration lines. I thought of how much easier it had become to sell obedience when you could at least offer a shape to the future.

"I will open the debt ledgers," I said.

The crowd moved like a single lung.

That should have frightened me enough to stop.

"I will break the bonds I can reach," I added, and heard the mistake while making it. "And I will take the rest."

The chant came back with my own words inside it.

Open the ledgers.

Take the rest.

Halvek closed his eyes as if listening to a funeral bell.

By the time I left the platform I had gained four labor captains, three smuggling crews willing to move under my marks, two former tally clerks who knew the archive indexing habits of the wardens, and an obligation large enough to structure the next six months of war.

On the walk back through the dock lanes, Halvek said, "Congratulations. You have converted aspiration into magical liability."

"You make everything sound diseased."

"Only the things you insist on living inside."

The first proof of what I had done arrived before dawn.

Five separate neighborhoods sent petitions asking when their promissories would be nullified. Not reviewed. Not negotiated. Nullified. One grain broker tried to pay militia levy in expectation of future debt relief and then claimed my speech as legal authority when his books were seized anyway. Three men in the river ward carved LEDGER BREAKER into a customs arch and beat a warden clerk nearly blind for refusing to endorse their household waivers.

Promises do not wait for the machinery that might one day honor them.

They collect interest immediately.

Uncle Drov arrived in the same week, not in person but more insultingly: by column, warrant, and seal.

Three outer nodes went dark in two days. Chapel relay at Gutter Spine. Ash store at North Kiln. The old east infirmary depot beyond the glass lanes. All taken under emergency annexation doctrine by House Veyr territorial stewards acting in concert with Cinder Throne authority. The warrants were publicly posted in triplicate, which was Drov's preferred style of cruelty. If he could make theft look administrative, he considered the work elegant.

The documents named me what the law now needed me to be.

Ritual destabilizer.

Usurping heir.

Unauthorized ration sovereign.

That last one bothered me most because it was accurate enough to sting.

Morn Hal spread the maps on the command table at Nine Chimneys and marked the losses with chalk that broke twice in his hand.

"This is not harassment," he said. "It is annexation pacing. Drov means to reduce you by functions. First depots. Then clinics. Then road law. He will force you to defend geography instead of initiative."

"Can we retake North Kiln?" I asked.

"Yes," he said. "And lose Gutter Spine in the same afternoon if that is what he wants."

The room smelled of cooling forge slag, wet canvas, and too many men waiting for me to decide which wound to call survivable.

Irix leaned against the wall with his arms folded and said, "Hit none of the seized nodes first. Hit the wagons between them. Make annexation expensive before you make it reversible."

Morn nodded once, disliking agreement but not pretending otherwise.

That became the week's work: not glorious recapture, not speeches, just sabotage, misdirection, and attrition calibrated to slow Drov's legal war enough that it had to reveal its teeth. We burned two supply wagons outside Gutter Spine, poisoned one horse line with soap bloom so it looked like plague at first inspection, bribed a chapel steward to misfile annexation writ copies for three days, and redirected medical stock through Tamsin's older river lanes under funeral manifests.

The city called it resistance.

It felt like accounting in motion.

Nara felt the strain first because everyone did through her.

The clinic had grown from a back room into a living accusation. Six beds became twelve. Twelve became sixteen if you counted the pallet spaces under the shelves. She started refusing fighters whose wounds she judged self-inflicted through stupidity. She increased guard rotation without asking me. She moved the child cots farther from the main operating tables and posted two of Yarrow's quietest runners at the back stair.

She also stopped lifting crate loads herself.

Not theatrically. Not enough for a stranger to notice on first pass. She simply redirected weight with the ruthless practicality of someone redoing a route map. She crouched instead of bending. Sat down between procedures more often. Ate dry biscuit in short, angry bites and glared at anyone who commented on it.

I noticed all of it and misnamed it as exhaustion because I still preferred errors that let me postpone understanding.

The intelligence on Sereth arrived in fragments: intercepted shrine traffic, panicked testimony from kin branches we reached first, Lio's readings from old rite law, and one captured courier whose jaw Yarrow broke before the man decided honesty was cheaper than silence.

The pattern was simple once the noise came off.

She was hungry.

Not metaphorically. Structurally.

Lio Ferr laid the bloodline tablets out in the old counting room with the reverence of a man who trusts information more than people and is correct more often than not.

"Stolen Origin power doesn't seat cleanly," he said, tapping one family lattice with an ink-black nail. "It can be stabilized. It cannot be made native. She needs resonance input from line-adjacent blood at intervals, especially under sustained high-load casting."

"Intervals of what length?" I asked.

"Depends on strain. If she's been doing district-scale work, shorter now. Much shorter if your disruptions have forced compensatory casting."

Halvek added, "Think of it as a theft the body must keep re-explaining to itself. Every explanation requires fuel."

The mirror at the far wall gave me the next thought before I formed it fully.

Take the family away.

Not kill them. That would have been simpler and less useful.

Hide them. Move them. Protect them badly enough that they still hated me for the method and well enough that Sereth couldn't drink from them again.

Lio saw where my expression changed.

"Yes," he said. "That is the asymmetry available to us. Protect the kin she has not reached. Move the branches she uses as reserve anchors. Starve the ritual without ever standing in the ritual room."

So that became the season's other machine.

Kin extraction.

Not noble rescue. Not family reconciliation. Extraction, because some of them spat at my feet, some demanded written guarantees I could not lawfully issue, and one old aunt struck me across the mouth with a candlestick and called me my mother's second mistake.

She came with us anyway.

The first coordinated pull was a branch house near the old dye yards: one elder-line widow, two cousins too frightened to lie well, and a bookkeeper nephew who tried to report us while we were cutting the back gate. Yarrow intercepted him at the stair and had him disarmed, blindfolded, and walking before I reached the landing.

"He knew the rear chapel code," Yarrow said. "If we left him unbound he would have tripped the signal bell inside thirty seconds."

"Did he fight?" I asked.

"Enough to confirm the decision."

That answer had too much of me in it.

We moved the branch through soap works, under tannery drains, then across the half-repaired bridge on labor permits forged by a dock clerk who had taken my crowd promise as private sacrament. The widow stared at the broken center span the whole time and said, "This is where cities learn who they belong to."

"And who is that?" I asked.

"Whoever can keep them fed after they are frightened," she said.

She understood politics better than most generals I had met.

Three more branch pulls followed in nine days. One clean, one costly, one ruined.

The ruined one was at a chapel safehouse on Rain Street where Sereth's people had arrived two hours ahead of us under forged medical authorization. They took back two blood-relations and left one boy hanged from the stair rail with legal parchment nailed to his chest declaring him restored to legitimate line custody.

The parchment was drier than the body.

Yarrow cut him down without a word.

I read the writ twice because rage loves paperwork when it can prove intent.

At the bottom, beneath the House seal, Sereth had written one line in her own hand:

You are thinning me, brother. That does not mean you are safe.

That night I asked for a casualty tally and got, instead, a message from Morn: Drov's columns had paused rather than pushed. Watching, not bleeding. Which meant the pressure had shifted. Which meant Sereth herself was driving priority now.

Hungry engines make mistakes.
