Ashbridge wasn't pretty, but it fed three districts.

Grain from the south lanes. Charcoal from the kilns. Fish from river quarter.

Control the bridge, control food.

When Yarrow and I reached the approach, the lane was already locked.

Pike lines. Infantry shields. Crossbow nests in both tower mouths. A legal crier reading confiscation law while my name sat on the seizure order in temple script.

Warden-Captain Morn Hal waited at centerline in black plate, helmet under his arm.

"Still standing like a House son," he called. "Shame about the rest."

I signaled Yarrow behind stone cover.

"Open the lane," I said. "No one has to die here."

"This bridge is worth more than you," Hal said.

Bribery failed. Threats failed.

I chose force.

Ashbridge support sigils were old and badly patched. I felt weak seams in the stone with my palm. If I overloaded them wrong, I would die with the bridge.

Hal saw me kneel and gave the order.

Arrows hit first. One runner went down behind us. Yarrow yelled once, then went quiet.

I cut my palm, fed blood into the seam, and pushed the channel past safe output.

The lattice cracked.

The collapse was ugly.

Center span dropped. Outer rail snapped. A tax cart tipped over the edge with clerks still strapped in. Wardens fell with the stone. Others jumped and broke on lower piers.

Hal kept his feet through the first shock and came at me. The second shock split the tower stair and dropped him out of sight.

Then the screaming started.

We withdrew through dust and smoke while emergency bells rang across district lines.

No triumph. Just ringing ears, reopened wounds, and Yarrow covered in gray bridge dust.

At the tannery, Nara took one look and asked, "What did you do?"

"Closed a route," I said.

"You closed food access for three districts," she said. "That isn't a route. That's a population."

That night repair levies went up before full casualty counts were posted.

By morning I was the bridge-breaker in every warden bulletin.

I sat in Nara's backroom while she stitched my hand and did not argue with the label.

By morning, bread prices had doubled.

Before Ashbridge fell, a loaf in District Nine cost four cinders. After the collapse and levy order, it hit eight at dawn, twelve by afternoon, and sold out before nightfall.

Guild stores closed "for inventory." Wardens guarded granaries. Ration lines wrapped entire blocks.

I walked those lines in borrowed cloth with my hood low.

Outside one soup chapel, a mother offered a bracelet for half-ration access. The clerk laughed. She shoved him. He called wardens. They beat her in front of her children.

I could have stepped in and started a fight we would lose.

I kept moving.

By dusk the city had two versions of me. In one, I hit the regime where it hurt. In the other, I broke the bridge and starved neighborhoods.

Both versions were partly true.

Yarrow came back from market runs with data on his arm in charcoal.

Grain prices. Patrol intervals. Black-market permit fees.

"They're selling the same permit twice," he said. "Window first, alley second."

"Who runs the alley?"

"Wardens and Guild together. Different coats, same people."

Nara heard him and set a basin down hard enough to crack it.

She'd been working nonstop through knife wounds, hunger collapse, and fever.

"Carry medicine to District Nine," she told me. "Then water. Then whatever's left."

I did.

At one alley crossing, a boy asked me if I was the one who broke the bridge. I told him to keep moving and stay near the chapel line. He didn't move until I handed him my water skin.

Late that night I met Halvek behind a ruined chapel wall. He smelled like cheap wine and spoke like an audit report.

"You already made a dark choice at Ashbridge," he said. "Now decide whether you want to admit it."

I left before he could keep talking.

On the way back, another hungry kid pulled a kitchen knife on me for bread. I took the knife, gave him half my ration, and pointed him toward Nara's line.

By dawn, soot slogans covered district walls.

BRIDGES FALL. TAXES DON'T.

That afternoon the Throne announced emergency grain powers, suspended appeals, and authorized stockpile seizure for "public order."

Public order looked like truncheons and empty bowls.

That night, Yarrow sat across from me while I wrote and asked if we could still call this helping.

"Some days," I said.

He waited for more. I had none.

I logged the result of Ashbridge in my ledger and did not soften the wording.

Tactical win. Civilian loss.

I found Brother Halvek asleep behind a collapsed chapel wall, using a wine jug as a pillow and a relic hammer as a blanket weight.

Temple hunters were sweeping expelled clergy that week. Halvek stayed alive by being useful to people who never wrote his name down.

When I kicked his boot, he opened one eye.

"If this is confession, get in line," he said.

"I need to cast without killing myself," I said.

That got his attention.

He listened while I explained the extraction damage, the tongue-vow, and the unstable pain channels. He asked practical questions. Where the pain started. How long it lasted. Which side failed first.

"You're forcing power through bad wiring," he said. "You need vow structure."

"Teach me."

"Hide me from temple hunters, and I will."

I trusted the deal, not the man.

I moved him to an unused bell room above the tannery. Nara objected immediately.

"You are not bringing drunk oathcraft into my clinic," she said.

Halvek gave her a formal bow that did not fit his clothes.

"I only drink when no one pays me to work," he said.

Nara looked at me. "If this kills someone, that's on you."

"It already is," I said.

Training started that night.

Halvek's core rule was simple: a vow isn't words, it's consequences. Weak vow, weak focus. Strong vow, stable output and brutal backlash if broken.

"Pick a vow you'll be tempted to violate," he said. "Otherwise it's theater."

I cut my palm and wrote on a cracked altar slab.

I will not kneel to House Veyr.

The bond took immediately. My breathing steadied. Hand tremor dropped. Channel control tightened for the first time since extraction.

We tested it on a patrol shaking down triage carts. I used a short cast and dropped the lead officer without opening a full blood-fire flare.

Clean output. No instant collapse.

By evening, temple auditors flagged the cast signature as forbidden binding and raised my bounty tier.

Halvek read the notice and almost smiled.

"Now they take you seriously," he said.

Back at the tannery, Yarrow asked what a vow was.

"A promise that bites back if you break it," I told him.

He nodded like he understood exactly what that meant.

After he left, Nara stood in the doorway with her arms folded.

"You told him the clean version," she said.

"He's twelve."

"He's listening to everything," she said. "If you're going to build him into your network, don't let him confuse pain with purpose."

I wanted to argue. I didn't.

Halvek looked over my channels and said, "You need an anchor lattice or you'll burn out by accident."

He laid out needles, ink, and heated salt in a storage room above the tannery vats. The walls sweated steam. The floor had quick ward lines in case I seized.

Nara refused to assist.

She also refused to leave.

She stood at the door with a clotting kit and watched every move Halvek made.

He mapped the lattice path across my ribs and old extraction scars.

"This will hurt," he said.

"It already hurts," I said.

"This is different. This changes where pain goes."

The first needle went in. By the fourth, my hands shook. By the seventh, I tasted blood and metal.

Nara pressed cloth to my shoulder and said, "If he passes out, we stop."

"If I pass out, finish it," I said.

She gave me a look that said we would argue later if I lived.

The full lattice took over an hour. At the end Halvek sealed the center point with blood.

My vision blurred, then snapped clear.

For the first time since the extraction, my channels felt aligned enough to trust for one short operation.

The test came fast.

A warden patrol grabbed one of Nara's medicine carts near canal gate and started a legal seizure under emergency levy powers.

We reached the lane with minutes left.

I cast through the new lattice.

Pain hit hard but directional, not random. First pulse dropped three wardens. The lead officer stayed up and rushed me with a hookblade. Second pulse broke his wrist and put him on stone.

We got the carts back.

Then I folded in alley runoff and vomited blood until my sight narrowed.

Nara dragged me home and resealed a split seam in the lattice.

"You got control," she said. "Now your body sends the invoice."

"Can I still run lanes tomorrow?"

She looked up from the stitching and said, "You can stand if you rest. You can fight if you ignore me. Pick one."

I stood the next morning.

By morning, people in free districts had a new saying for power sold as easy.

"A clean sigil is a liar."

I heard it from a bread seller while buying water.

He wasn't wrong.

That night I logged the result.

Scar lattice active. Control improved. Cost rising.
