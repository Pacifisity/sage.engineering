Gloam Market opened before dawn and started cheating people while the bells were still arguing over first light.

I entered through District Nine's south toll with eight cinders in my pocket, a punctured shoulder wrapped in dead-guard cloth, and a coat that still smelled faintly of burned horse oil.

The lane clerk at the gate took my last entry coin, dropped it into a split brass bowl, and never looked up from his slate.

"Pedestrian fee paid," he said. "Injury tax waived if you die off main road."

He said it like weather.

Inside the district, everything moved faster than pain.

Soup stalls thinned broth with chalk and sold it as bone stock. Fishmongers painted old scales with lamp grease to fake freshness. Shrine clerks sold stamped blessing scraps by weight. Three boys in temple aprons hawked counterfeit permit tabs that would pass one checkpoint and fail the next.

The smells came in layers.

Rotted citrus from spoiled convoy sacks. Hot tallow from candle gutters. Lye steam from tanning vats. Iron and wet brick where runoff pooled in cracked stone seams.

In House Veyr we called places like this unstable trade nodes.

On foot, with blood drying in my sleeve, it looked like a city feeding itself with a knife.

I kept moving.

A crier at brazier square read out levy changes in temple cadence while two wardens posted updated warrants to a board covered in older names.

"Emergency transport statutes remain active," the crier said. "Harboring debt fugitives constitutes material aid to legal disorder."

He looked at nobody and everybody at once.

A woman selling boiled root glanced at my shoulder wrap, then at the warrant board, then away.

That look had a price.

People in this district did not betray for principle. They betrayed for grain, debt erasure, and one week without inspections.

I cut into tannery lanes where steam made faces hard to track and watchers harder to trust. Half the doors were false fronts. Real clinics hid behind hide racks, lime pits, and storage rooms that smelled like old blood and new soap.

I asked for Nara Quill at three wrong doors first.

At the fourth, a narrow apprentice with burn scars on both wrists blocked the threshold and looked me over the way quartermasters look over freight.

"Payment first," he said.

"I have cinders," I said.

"Everyone has cinders."

He reached to shove the door closed. My coat shifted. He saw the extraction seam through the collar split and froze.

Then he turned over his shoulder and called, louder than needed, "Nara. You need to see this one."

She came through a curtain of hanging hide strips, already gloved, hair tied back in a cloth that had once been white.

She did not ask my name.

She did not ask my House.

She looked at the wound edges, the way I was standing to protect one side, the dried blood pattern under the collar, and she said, "Back room. Table. Now."

That was the first kindness she gave me.

Not softness.

Accuracy.

Her clinic was a long room carved out of an old storage bay behind the tannery vats. Two lamps. Three cots. One brick basin. A ledger desk with ink stains deep enough to have seasons. The air tasted of boiled water, clove wash, and hide acid.

She cut my shoulder wrap away, then sliced through the shirt seam at my back and stopped breathing for half a beat when she saw the full extraction line.

"Who did this?" she asked.

"Family."

"Name."

"Sereth Veyr took the core. Matron Ys signed the transfer."

Nara set her jaw and reached for a kettle hook.

"If that's true," she said, "the city is about to get uglier than usual."

She worked fast and talked only when necessary.

"Hold still."

"Breathe now."

"If you pass out, I keep sewing."

The puncture in my shoulder had dragged dirt and cloth threads into muscle. The extraction seam across my spine had reopened where wagon shocks kept splitting half-healed tissue. She reopened both injuries on purpose, flushed them with hot salt, then threaded temporary sigil sutures to keep my damaged channels from fraying further.

The first stitch hurt.

The tenth changed vocabulary.

By the twentieth I could taste copper in my teeth from biting through my own cheek to stay awake.

Nara never offered pity.

She offered options.

"I can close this so you walk tonight," she said. "Or I can close it so you cast next week. Not both."

"Tonight," I said.

She gave a single nod and changed thread.

"Then don't cast unless you decide what memory you're willing to lose when your body asks for collateral."

I turned my head enough to see her profile.

"You think I'll lose memory before blood?"

"Without a core?" she said. "You'll lose whatever is cheapest for the spell and most expensive for the person."

She finished the last seam, pressed clot cloth over my shoulder, and tied off the sutures with a knot I recognized from convoy medics.

Practical. Fast to cut if the wound turns bad.

Then she walked to the ledger desk, opened a slate, and wrote down my debt out loud where I could hear each number land.

"Base treatment, four ashmarks equivalent. Emergency channel threading, two ashmarks. Clot wash, one vial replacement. Total due now: six ashmarks."

I laughed once, then regretted it.

"I have eight cinders," I said. "Not ashmarks."

"Then you're short," she said.

I put the cinders on her desk anyway. She counted them without touching my eyes.

"Eight cinders buys first closure and two days of dressings," she said. "Not future treatment."

She turned the slate and added another line.

"Outstanding balance may be paid by route work, retrieval, escort, or clean coin."

"Retrieval of what?"

She slid a second page across the table.

Three crate marks.

Warden Yard Seventeen.

Seizure stamp in red.

"My medicine consignment," she said. "Confiscated under emergency levy because the ward captain decided triage fluids count as strategic stock."

She tapped the page with her pen.

"Bring those crates back and I keep treating your spine. Fail, and you'll be hunting someone else to sew channels you already burned through."

I looked at the seizure stamp. Official text. Temple countersign. Legal theft dressed as policy.

"Why trust me with this?" I asked.

Nara's expression didn't move.

"I don't trust you."

She closed the ledger.

"I trust incentives."

From the front lane, market bells hit second trade hour. Voices rose, then dipped in the strange synchronized hush that only happens when public notice crews arrive.

Nara's apprentice pulled back the curtain and said, "Brazier square wants all residents out. Warden flare board's active."

Nara glanced at me once.

"Sit if you want to live."

I stood anyway.

The square was packed by the time I reached the edge crowd. A projection brazier burned green-white over the crier platform. House sigils spun across the smoke in formal sequence: Throne authority, Veyr crest, legal ratification mark.

Then Sereth's face formed in the fire.

Sharper than memory.

Calmer than truth.

She wore rite white and spoke in that measured public tone she used when we were children and she wanted clerks to believe she had never been afraid of anything.

"Cael Veyr has committed treason against House and Throne," the projection said. "He is designated kin-traitor and consecrated fugitive. Harboring him is punishable by seizure, labor sentence, and property cancellation."

The script flared brighter.

"Reward for live capture: five hundred ashmarks."

A half-second later, crowd sound changed shape.

Conversation became arithmetic.

I kept my head down and started backing through the fish lane when a polished stall mirror caught two men moving against traffic in matching civilian coats and warden boots.

One tapped his sleeve once. Signal.

Track and close.

I kicked a brazier stand into a dye cart, took a faceful of blue smoke, and cut left through a rope alley before the watchers reached the spill.

Someone shouted.

Someone else shouted my family name like an accusation and a prayer at the same time.

I lost them at tannery sluice stairs and made it back to Nara's door with my shoulder bleeding through fresh stitching.

Her apprentice swore when he saw the blood line.

Nara did not swear.

She took one look and said, "Table. Again."

While she reclosed the split seam, she asked no questions about the chase.

Afterward she wrapped the shoulder tighter and set a small bottle beside my hand.

"Two drops in boiled water for fever," she said. "Any more and you stop breathing."

She pointed at my fingers.

"Tremor's worse."

"It'll settle."

"Or it won't."

She pulled a folded permit slip from under her ledger and handed it to me.

"Back lane sleep pass. Good for one night if patrols ask why you're in this district after curfew."

I read the seal twice.

Clinic authority. Provisional.

Signed with her name.

That was the second kindness.

Still not softness.

Risk.

"Why?" I asked.

"Because if you bleed out in the gutter, I don't get my crates back."

She held my gaze one beat longer than necessary.

"And because you're not the first person your family fed to paperwork."

By dark, curfew bells had started and Wardens were sweeping outer lanes in pairs. I did not use the pass. Paper helps until it doesn't.

Instead I moved through roof shadows and watched Yard Seventeen from an abandoned kiln ridge.

Nara's three crates sat under a red tarp near the rear wall, guarded by routine more than skill.

Two gate wardens.

One ledger clerk on rotating count.

One blind corridor where lime carts entered after dusk.

I stayed there long enough for the pattern to repeat three full cycles.

No cast. No move.

Just observation.

Father's old line lived in my head like a nail hammered clean through timber.

Know the route before you count the enemy.

At midnight I retreated to the kiln annex where I'd slept the night before. Rain had eased into mist. The district sounded different after curfew: fewer voices, more boots, a bell here and there marking confiscations nobody would appeal.

I pulled out the cast ledger page from my coat lining and added a second entry beneath the one-year vow cost.

Entry Two.

Location: Gloam Market, District Nine.

Medical status: temporary closure only; channel seam unstable; repeat maintenance required.

Debt status: six ashmarks due reduced to route debt and retrieval obligation.

Operational obligation: recover three medical crates seized at Warden Yard Seventeen.

Strategic pressure update: public projection confirms five hundred ashmark live-capture bounty under Sereth's authority.

I paused, then added one personal line because the ledger was already becoming the only place where truth did not need witnesses.

Personal note: Surgeon ignored my name until she needed to price my survival.

I let the ink dry and listened to rain hit broken tile.

This chapter of my life should have been simple.

Escape convoy.

Find help.

Run.

Instead it was already logistics, obligations, and scarce people making precise bargains in a city that called those bargains mercy when signed by the right office.

Near second watch I heard boots in the lane outside the annex and flattened against the kiln wall with knife in hand. Three wardens passed within arm's length, talking about reward percentages.

"Five hundred split six ways is still winter money," one said.

"If we find him first," another answered.

They moved on.

I waited until their footfalls drowned under canal noise, then sat back down and looked at the route sketch I'd scratched into soot: wall seam, tarp line, clerk cycle, exit points.

No clean entry.

No clean exit.

No clean anything.

At dawn I would either steal Nara's crates and buy treatment I could not survive long without, or fail and start searching for another surgeon while hunted under legal kill authority.

I folded the sketch into my ledger, slid both under my coat, and lay down on warm ash with my boots still on.

Before sleep took me, I made one small promise out loud because spoken words hold better than silent plans.

"No cast without naming the cost."

The kiln gave no answer.

The city did.

At first light, temple bells rang seizure hour.
