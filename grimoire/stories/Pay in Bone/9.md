The page looked calm.

My hand wasn't.

Deep winter never breaks cleanly. It rots into thaw. Snowmelt where there had been no snow, just old frost trapped in mortar seams, runoff that carried soot into doorways and made every alley smell like wet ash and sewage.

The famine eased by degree, which is the cruelest kind of easing. Not enough to call relief. Just enough to make people work again while still hungry.

Bread returned to some stalls at nine cinders, then eight on better mornings. Soup lines shortened by half a street and felt merciful for it. The dead stopped arriving in piles and started arriving one at a time, thin enough to look almost administrative.

Wardens used the thaw to harden policy.

Medical carts were searched under contamination law.

Temple inspectors started asking for shrine stamps on wound kits.

Defrocked priests, unlicensed oath-workers, and "informal ritual technicians" were posted for apprehension with bounties low enough to tempt mean men and high enough to tempt hungry ones.

That was how Brother Halvek came to me.

Not with revelation.

With vomit on his sleeve and three temple hunters half a street behind him.

I found him in the shell of a collapsed chapel south of Saint Vellum, wedged between a broken altar and a rain barrel that had collected enough green water to grow skin. He still wore fragments of formal vestment under a commoner's coat: black threadwork at the cuffs, oath-script burnt into the inner lining, ash-white collar gone gray with use.

He also smelled like sacramental wine, old blood, and someone who had been awake too long without earning the wakefulness.

"If you're here to rob me," he said when I stepped inside, "take the cup and leave the theology. The cup is silver. The theology's spoiled." 

"Temple hunters are coming," I said.

He looked toward the doorway, sighed, and pushed himself upright using the altar edge.

"Then I'd prefer not to die in this parish," he said. "It has bad acoustics." 

Two hunters came through the side arch with pole-hooks and legal bands tied at the wrist, the sort of men who let paperwork do the moral labor before they swing steel.

Yarrow dropped the first from the choir loft with a brick to the temple. I took the second at the knee and finished it before he found breath to call out. Halvek watched both deaths with the expression of a man revising a sermon mid-sentence.

"You travel strangely for charity," he said.

"You talk too much for a fugitive priest." 

"Defrocked," he corrected. "Priest suggests institutional shelter. I have the opposite." 

We moved him through kiln lanes under sackcloth and tannery stink while Yarrow cleaned blood off the brick in canal water and did not comment on the efficiency of it.

At the clinic, Nara took one look at Halvek's split lip, temple ink scars, and the wound in his side where a hook had nearly found liver, and said, "No sermons on my table." 

"I only preach when paid," Halvek said.

"Then you're overdressed for your market." 

He laughed at that, then winced when she probed the wound.

While she stitched him, he watched me sort route lists and casualty notes at the desk and said, almost mildly, "You're doing it backward." 

"Doing what backward?"

"Trying to build a command structure with no oath architecture and no stable casting frame. Winter let you survive on improvisation. Spring won't." 

Nara tied off one stitch with unnecessary force.

"Don't recruit from my table," she said.

"I never recruit," Halvek answered. "I diagnose. Recruitment is what desperate men hear when diagnosis sounds useful." 

He stayed three nights because he couldn't stand straight after the hook wound and because I hadn't decided whether he was asset or liability. By the end of the second, he'd corrected two of my route redundancies, identified one of Yarrow's checkpoint lists as too pattern-stable to survive a competent watcher, and named the real problem with my magic in six words.

"You have power. You lack structure." 

He said it while turning one of my ledger pages with bloodless priest fingers, careful not to smudge the ink.

"You paid with memory, lifespan, pain, and other people's bodies," he said. "You documented the costs well enough. What you never built was an internal law for where the power goes afterward." 

"I don't have an Origin core."

"No," Halvek said. "You have a ruin. Ruins can still carry load if you brace them properly." 

He asked me the question no one else had thought to ask cleanly.

"Do you know how much life you have left to spend?"

I gave him the obvious answer.

"Enough."

"Men who say enough about finite resources die with surprised faces." 

That same night he had me sit inside a chalk ring cut with salt and lampblack and check my lifespan buffer the old way: breath cadence, pulse count, sigil response to blood heat, the occult arithmetic House children with intact cores get taught as caution and then spend their adulthood ignoring.

Without the Origin, the reading came blurred. Still usable.

Starting natural buffer, had I gone unwounded and uncast, somewhere in the range of eighty to ninety years after youth.

Current practical remainder, after blood-fire, bridge expenditure, and the smaller hidden drains of emergency work: lower than that by enough to feel in endurance if I was honest and enough to deny if I wasn't.

Halvek did the figures twice on scrap and pushed the page to me.

"You can keep paying this way for a while," he said. "Not indefinitely. Not if you mean to govern anything larger than three rooms and a rumor." 

I folded the page and put it in my coat beside the older ledger notes.

Finite. Documented. Real.

That changed less than it should have.

Two days later, Halvek took me to the ruined choir crypt under Saint Vellum and told me I needed a vow before I needed another tactic.

"A real vow," he said. "Not the mouth-cut emergency kind. Something that narrows you on purpose before the city narrows you by attrition." 

"Why?"

"Because men without cores are too easy to disperse. Pain, fear, persuasion, nostalgia. A binding turns at least one part of you into law." 

The crypt smelled of wet stone, lamp smoke, and old incense that had seeped so far into the walls it woke when the air shifted. He placed a cracked altar slab on two brick stacks, scraped away the mold skin with his sleeve, and set out the materials like a butcher laying out clean knives.

Salt.

Ash.

Black thread.

One silver nail bent from temple salvage.

"Name something the war can't be allowed to buy from you," he said.

I thought first of Yarrow, because Nara would've wanted that answer.

Then of Nara herself, because I had already started building the habit of measuring a room by whether she was inside it.

Then of House Veyr.

Not as family. As gravity.

The structure still pulling at every choice I made, whether by revenge, fear, shame, or the old training that taught me power was legitimate only when stamped by blood.

"I will never kneel to House Veyr again," I said.

Halvek nodded once.

"Good. Specific. Expensive if broken." 

He had me cut the center of my palm and let the blood strike stone. Not drip. Strike. Then speak the vow again with the silver nail pinned over the blood line so the oath anchored to pain and metal both.

"I will never kneel to House Veyr again." 

The crypt answered by tightening around my joints.

Not metaphorically.

Physically.

An immediate lock in the knees, hips, and lower spine, as if the body was rehearsing the punishment for future betrayal before I had even stood up.

Halvek pressed two fingers to the blood on the slab and closed the ritual with a phrase too old for liturgy and too practical for prayer.

"Now if you break it," he said, "the body remembers what you promised before your mind can justify the exception." 

"How bad?"

"Bad enough that you'll think twice. Worse if you don't." 

He said it like weather.

That was when I started trusting him.

Not because he offered power.

Because he described consequences without salesmanship.

The vow stabilized one axis. It did nothing for the broader problem of casting without a core.

For that, Halvek wanted fire.

Nara wanted him thrown out of the clinic.

"He's proposing to solve structural damage with more structural damage," she said after hearing the first outline. "That's not medicine. That's masonry done on meat." 

"Accurate," Halvek said.

"I wasn't complimenting you."

He spread his notes anyway.

Scar lattice.

Alternative channel architecture burned across the ribs and flank where the body could learn to route power through pain rather than lineage resonance.

Stability through suffering made deliberate.

"It will let him cast without tearing every path open fresh each time," Halvek said. "It will also hurt continuously and worsen under load. That is the trade." 

Nara looked at me, not him.

"And if it fails?"

Halvek answered because he had no talent for comforting lies.

"Hemorrhage. Shock. Nerve damage. Death if the burn pattern collapses inward." 

"You left out madness," I said.

"That's because the madness is already here." 

He said it while looking directly at the basin on the shelf where my reflection had been arriving a fraction early for weeks.

Nara heard the truth under the cruelty and hated him for both.

"Get out," she said.

He did.

I followed him to the back corridor anyway.

"Will it work?" I asked.

"Yes," he said. "And no. It will work as a bridge works when built from cracked stone under too much weight. It will hold until it doesn't." 

That was enough.

We burned the lattice in the quarry chapel two nights later because the clinic smelled too much like survival for what came next.

Halvek marked the pattern with charcoal first: branching sigil geometry from lower ribs across the side seam and upward toward sternum, each node positioned to intersect old pain lines from bridge backlash and extraction trauma.

Nara refused to leave.

"If he dies," she said, "I want it to happen in front of someone who knows how to stop it if stopping stays possible." 

Halvek gave her the iron with no argument.

The first brand touch felt like being unmade with handwriting.

No scream at first because pain that complete arrives ahead of sound.

Then sound caught up.

I bit through the leather strap and tasted old tannin, blood, and the copper edge of panic I was too tired to dignify by naming.

Halvek called pattern points as Nara burned them.

"Lower branch."

"Hold." 

"Center node. Deeper." 

"He's fading." 

"Then hurt him harder or we lose the line." 

Somewhere halfway through, the world narrowed to five things.

The smell of my own skin scorching.

Nara's sleeve against my shoulder keeping me from rolling off the table.

Halvek's voice counting like a clerk of pain.

The bell in my ears.

The conviction that if I opened my eyes I would see the reflection standing where my body should be.

When it ended, I vomited black into a basin until my ribs seized and the fresh burns leaked through bandage cloth in steady, humiliating heat.

"Congratulations," Halvek said quietly. "You now have a structure worth fearing." 

I tried to answer and could not get enough air around the laugh.

The fever took me that night.

Three days, according to the ledger later.

To me it arrived as fragments.

Nara's hand forcing water at my mouth.

Halvek changing soaked cloth with priestly detachment that somehow felt cleaner than pity.

Yarrow at the doorway, reading me casualty updates because apparently I kept asking whether the routes still held.

At some point I dictated notes and did not remember doing it.

When I surfaced properly, my own journal had three pages in Nara's hand.

Pulse irregular. Fever high.

No seizure.

Patient incoherent but operational language intact.

Then, later, one line written smaller than the rest:

He is building something that will swallow him if it doesn't kill him first.

I found her in the next room boiling thread.

"You wrote in my ledger," I said.

"You were too busy hallucinating doctrine at the ceiling." 

"You stayed." 

She didn't look up.

"Someone had to keep you from dying in an interesting way." 

That was not softness.

It was still more than obligation.

I almost thanked her and remembered Chapter 6 in time to stop.

She saved me the effort anyway.

"Don't," she said.

Then, after a long pause: "You're building something that will swallow you." 

I knew she had written it. I knew because the sentence sat in the ledger with her pressure on the pen strokes and because it was the kind of truth she only said when she was too tired to make it gentler.

"It may be the only thing keeping people alive," I said.

"Those are not opposite statements." 

That stayed with me longer than the fever.

Halvek tested the lattice on a patrol two weeks later, when my bandages were mostly dry and my side felt like a live animal had been sewn under the skin.

Three wardens were sweeping a tannery culvert line and had one of my runners pinned behind a slag cart. Too close for bell work. Too messy for blood-fire unless I wanted to pay years to kill men who barely understood the command chain above them.

"Pain channel," Halvek said from the alley mouth. "Short burst. Let the lattice carry the load." 

"Cost?"

He didn't smile.

"The pain itself. Plus whatever you tear doing it if your body gets ambitious." 

I drew breath, felt the burn lines wake under the skin, and named the trade anyway because not naming cost is how power starts lying.

"Pain only. Single shock. Close range." 

Then I let the lattice bite.

It felt like nails being pried under every fresh scar at once.

The shock hit outward in a dirty invisible wave. Not elegant. Not bright. Just force carried through agony. Two wardens dropped immediately. The third staggered, turned, and Yarrow put a knife into the soft place under his jaw before I could move.

The runner lived.

I doubled over and coughed black into the gutter.

Halvek crouched beside me, clinical as always.

"Good," he said. "That means it took." 

"That means I'm bleeding internally." 

"Also true." 

Back at the clinic, Nara cut the reopened seam cloth away, saw the fresh blood in it, and closed her eyes once.

"Sustainable?" I asked.

She rinsed the wound, tied off one leak point, and answered without looking at me.

"For a while." 

That was her verdict on most of us by then.

For a while.

The thaw had brought dock delegations by then, because scarcity makes administrators out of whoever can keep two routes running and three fires lit.

Cask Bren came first, not Old Scale from the clinic bed but dock-labor Bren, barrel-shouldered, one ear half gone, practical enough to negotiate during a burial week if that's when numbers lined up.

He met me in the lower weigh house with two stevedore representatives and a ledger of labor injuries under one arm.

"You keep saving lanes one at a time," he said. "We're past one at a time." 

He wanted formal partnership.

Dock labor in exchange for ration guarantees, escort priority, and voice in allocation tables.

Not allegiance. Administration.

I heard the shift immediately.

Fugitive no longer.

System operator.

I told him I'd answer after reviewing stores, routes, and headcount.

He shrugged.

"Review fast. Hunger doesn't wait for governance to invent itself." 

That line could have come from my father, and for once the missing voice hurt less than the recognition.

That night I logged the chapter's numbers carefully.

Entry Eight.

Late winter into spring, month nineteen from exile.

Defrocked asset acquired: Brother Halvek, oathcraft specialist, hunted under temple irregularity statutes.

Vow bound: never kneel to House Veyr again.

Observed effect: immediate stabilizing focus; future breach projected to produce severe bodily backlash.

Scar lattice installed across ribs and side seam.

Immediate costs: fever, hemorrhage risk, continuous pain baseline, vomiting black clots.

Test cast successful at close range. Runner preserved. Three wardens neutralized.

Operational outcome: non-core tactical casting now possible without direct lifespan expenditure for each short burst.

Projected long-term cost: pain dependency, structural attrition, cumulative bodily failure.

Then I wrote the line Halvek had effectively sold me with honesty and Nara had priced with exhaustion.

Personal note: Brother Halvek promises I can fight without my core. The cost is all of me.

Outside, meltwater ran black through the kiln gutters.

Inside the washbasin, my reflection stayed exactly where it should have been for almost a full minute.

That frightened me more than when it moved.
