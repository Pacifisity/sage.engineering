Morn Hal chose the Glassfields because fire loves mirrors.

At noon her pyromancers lit the first line, and sunlight turned into blades. Towers shattered. Carts burned. Refugee crews who had come to salvage glass were caught in open ground before my militia could form ranks.

I made the call that won me the field and ruined it.

Instead of pulling civilians out, I sent fighters to hold the south cluster and deny Morn Hal the ridge approach. Tactically sound. Morally rotten.

In the third hour she changed the board. Her casters dropped the remaining towers in a chained collapse, and the entire basin became a storm of glass.

We took cover under beams and wagon frames.

The refugees did not know how.

When the air cleared, the dead were everywhere. Cuts too many to bind. Lungs full of glitter dust. Eyes ruined by flying shards. Morn Hal used the chaos to withdraw, satisfied with denial if she could not take possession.

The battle report called it a defended position.

Nara called it what it was.

"You turned a district into a wound," she told me, setting a jar of blood-flecked sputum on her table. "This is from people who never touched your lines."

By the second week, wind picked up the powdered glass and carried it into homes and market lanes. Coughing turned to bleeding. Masks became ration items. Children learned weather signs before they learned sums.

I funded clinics, filter cloth, transport, and grave crews.

None of it repaired soil, lungs, or trust.

Halvek read the casualty totals beside me that night. "You are past accident," he said quietly. "You made a choice and now you are managing its consequences like inventory."

He was right.

I still signed the next operations sheet before dawn.

Three weeks after Glassfields, the children started coughing blood.

Most had never been near the battle. Dust had crossed wards on dry wind, slipped through shutters, and settled in lungs too small to fight it. Nara's clinic filled with parents holding cloth to mouths and waiting for miracles no one could offer.

"Supportive care only," Nara said, voice flat with exhaustion. "Masks. Rinses. Rest. Some will heal enough. Some won't."

I released emergency funds the same hour. Extra cots, boiled water teams, courier lanes for medicine, cloth workshops for masks.

The system got faster. The suffering did not get smaller.

Sem was eight when she died of infection after weeks of bleeding coughs. I stood in the doorway while Nara worked, because leaving felt like cowardice and staying felt like a sentence I had earned.

When Sem stopped breathing, the room went quiet in that hard way quiet does after a fight is lost.

Nara wrote her name with careful letters.

I wrote her as a casualty linked to environmental exposure.

Both entries were true. Only one was human.

Halvek found me later with the ledger open.

"This is where people split," he said. "They either let the cost change them, or they file it and move on."

I filed it and moved on.

By month's end, mask lines were normal, triage became routine, and glass weather was posted beside market prices. A disaster had entered daily life.

I told myself adaptation was resilience.

Sometimes, in the hour before dawn, I heard children coughing from three streets away and knew it was also evidence.

Lio brought the theory; Halvek brought the warning.

"Stolen power anchored by blood can be destabilized by blood," Lio said, laying old lineage charts across my table. "Seven true kin-names, spoken in proper sequence at your ancestral grave-circle. It will force backlash through Sereth's bindings."

The catch was obvious: we had only five names.

Two branches of Veyr lineage had been erased from public records decades ago. We searched temple ledgers, monastery rolls, and sealed family archives bought with money and threats in equal measure.

I tried to remember the missing names myself and hit the same wall every time. Bell-casting had burned holes where family memory should have been. Faces remained. Voices did not.

Lio attempted sympathetic reconstruction from places I had once met those relatives: well water, hearth ash, my blood, and prayer-scraped stone. We recovered emotional echoes, not words.

At last, in a disputed succession register hidden behind damaged bindings, we found one of the erased names: Vel Caith Veyr.

I expected recognition. I felt nothing.

That frightened me more than the gaps.

We left with six names and a plan to reach the Veyr crypts, now under warden watch. Six could wound Sereth's hold. Seven might break it.

I logged objective progress and supply needs, then added one private note I did not show the captains:

I can recover facts about my bloodline without recovering any attachment to it.

Whether that made me safer or emptier was unclear. Either way, we moved forward.

The ossuary was older than city maps and colder than any crypt should be.

Its custodian traded in memory. For relics and coin, she brought us seven clay jars marked with Veyr seals. Inside each were milk teeth etched with kin-contract script: names carved into enamel, inheritance written in bite marks.

We opened the first jars and confirmed key lines quickly, including Vel Caith Veyr.

Then we hit the break.

One socket was empty. The tooth carrying a critical name had been removed long ago.

When I touched that jar, a ward snapped shut around my mind.

I saw the Veyr dead rising in burial ranks while Sereth stood among them, naming blood faster and cleaner than I could. In the vision, every name answered her.

I woke on stone with blood in my mouth and Lio gripping my shoulders.

"Preservation curse," he said. "It weaponizes fear around missing records."

The problem was that fear matched probability. Sereth had kept closer ties to family structures than I had. She might already hold what we were missing.

We spent two more days testing traces in the archive. Nothing.

Final count: six confirmed names, one void.

"Enough for a weakening rite," Lio said. "Not enough for a full collapse."

I accepted it.

Since the ward, memory had become harder to trust. Old gaps from bell-casting now filled with false fragments. Sometimes I pictured a relative and saw Sereth's face instead. Halvek called it cognitive fracture and gave the only cure I would not take: stop using the bell, stop touching cursed records.

I chose progress over stability.

By week's end we had infiltration routes, six names, and a ritual that might tilt the war without ending it.

I recorded it as operational advancement.

Privately, I wrote the part I could not say aloud: I am still deciding this with clear eyes, and that may be the worst sign of all.

The warning at Nine Chimneys was a clock made of bones.

Two of my lieutenants, cleaned and arranged in a ring by the gate, each long bone set like an hour mark. No trap, no guards, no haste. Just precision.

Irix studied it and said the name before sunset: Karel Dune.

Retired bounty hunter, rumored butcher of a provincial court, expensive enough that only major houses or private syndicates could hire him. If he was working my territory, someone with deep pockets wanted pressure without fingerprints.

I changed routines that night. New sleeping rooms every dusk. Rotating guard ladders. False movement reports seeded through merchant channels.

Three weeks later Karel walked straight into my command tent in daylight.

He did not draw steel. He sat and treated the meeting like an audit.

"You understood the message," he said. "Most people either posture or panic. You adjusted."

"Who sent you?"

"A client with fear and money. Beyond that, irrelevant." He watched my shaking hand as if timing it. "You are still dangerous. You are also coming apart."

I told him he was wrong.

He listed the evidence anyway: bell-casting memory gaps, cursed-archive bleed, split attention, degraded sleep, over-reliance on momentum. "If you keep this pace," he said, "collapse is a schedule, not a possibility."

"Then kill me and finish your contract."

"Not yet," he said. "You may self-destruct faster than I can stage it."

When he left, Halvek stepped from behind the inner curtain.

"You just received independent confirmation," he said. "What will you do with it?"

I gave the answer he already knew.

"Continue the Sereth operation."

He closed his ledger with a sound like a latch.

That night I entered the meeting as data: contact with Karel Dune, risk horizon shortened, operations unchanged.

The entry was clean. The choice was not.

I chose blood over leverage.

If Sereth's stolen power was fed through Veyr family channels, then cutting those channels could weaken her faster than any siege. We identified three junior cousins tied to ritual traffic and struck their funeral convoy before it reached the second ward gate.

Irix ran the operation. Three teams, six exits, no speeches.

It worked.

By evening, three Veyrs were dead and every House branch that mattered had my name underlined.

The response was immediate: formal blood vendetta, mercenary contracts in hill country, convoy raids, garrison probes. Not one war, but twenty small ones that never quite ended.

Halvek met me over the dispatch table and did not lower his voice.

"This is not strategy anymore," he said. "This is rupture."

"They were feeding Sereth."

"Some were trying to leave that system. You killed possibilities along with targets."

Nara gave me a different version in the clinic, hands red from work.

"You can still step away," she said. "Renounce, disappear, break the center point."

"If I step away, they come for everyone tied to me. You. Yarrow. The dock crews."

She held my gaze for a long time. "That was true the moment you made us your shield."

By month's end we had stabilized into attrition: too violent for peace, too costly for decisive victory. Merchants bled coin on escorts. Villages learned to hide food caches. Patrol logs doubled. Funeral logs tripled.

Karel appeared once at twilight, watching my map wall.

"You built yourself a war that cannot be won clean," he said. "Impressive engineering."

I told him to leave.

He smiled like a craftsman seeing good work in a broken machine.

In my ledger I wrote: vendetta active, equilibrium hostile, timeline shortening.

Under it, for myself only: I have become a problem that reproduces when cut.

After ten days without real sleep, the mirrors started answering back.

At first it was useful. A route correction in a washbasin reflection that prevented an ambush. A supply warning in polished steel that let us move grain before a raid. The advice was good enough that I listened.

Then I started speaking aloud to surfaces during staff meetings.

Irix called it what it was.

"You are splitting your own analysis into voices and mistaking the split for revelation," he said. "Accuracy does not make it healthy."

Threll said the officers were watching and pretending not to.

So I adapted the building instead of myself.

We removed mirrors from planning rooms. Covered bright metal. Moved councils to matte-walled cellars. I still consulted reflections in private, then returned with decisions already made.

Halvek watched the renovations with open grief.

"You are architecting around your breakdown," he said. "That means you have accepted it as infrastructure."

He was right, and the worst part was that output improved.

Yarrow's network ran cleaner with the mirror prompts integrated into timing windows. Convoys arrived with fewer losses. Patrol loops tightened. On paper, command performance rose.

Inside my head, coherence fell.

The voices differentiated over time. One counted stores and labor. One tracked military risk. One understood wards and ritual load. They argued, and I adjudicated, and some nights I could not tell which voice was mine before the argument began.

By month's end, the command system no longer required a whole person at its center. It required a signature, a schedule, and enough fragments to keep the machine fed.

I logged the month as operational efficiency gains under stress conditions.

Then I sat in a dark room with a covered mirror and waited for instructions I told myself were still my own.
