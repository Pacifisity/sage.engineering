A clean sigil is a liar.

That was Halvek's line, delivered over a washbasin while I was spitting last night's clotting into pink water and trying not to tear the lattice by breathing too deep.

"Anything that channels cleanly is hiding the payment somewhere else," he said. "Usually in someone weaker."

Summer had come down hard on District Nine by then. Not the hot, loose summer I remembered from House courtyards with shaded cisterns and servants who could afford to complain about dust. This was kiln-heat summer. Market-stink summer. Summer that turned the mortar soft at noon and left the poor sleeping on roofs because rooms held death too well.

The thaw damage never got repaired.

Ashbridge still stood broken in the middle, rebuilt in pieces with chainwalks, timber scaffolds, and toll platforms ugly enough to prove they were temporary and permanent both. The famine no longer looked like empty shelves. It looked like systems. Meal points. Queue chalk. Barrel counts. Escort schedules. Armed boys with route ledgers deciding who crossed which lane before dark.

That was the season where I stopped being able to tell, by instinct alone, whether I was running a resistance or a government with worse lighting.

The lattice made the distinction less important.

It hurt all the time now.

Not dramatically. Dramatic pain is almost merciful because it gives the body one thing to believe in. This was constant nail-pressure under the ribs, a live wire dragged under scar tissue from flank to sternum. When I laughed, it bit. When I turned too quickly, it flashed white. When I cast through it, the whole side of me felt like a door being forced in against rotten hinges.

Halvek called that stability.

Nara called it slow murder with bookkeeping.

Both were correct.

By midsummer I was running six standing efforts at once: clinic escort, grain diversion, south-lane courier relay, salvage crews at the bridge piers, shrine interception, and the watch lists Yarrow kept on men who smiled too often when they lied. The city had adapted to the harm I had done it. Black markets threaded around the broken bridge. Families learned which ration clerks could be bribed with soap instead of coin. Smugglers sold river fish caught upstream of the oil sheen at three times honest price and still called it mercy.

Every adaptation made me more necessary.

That was the worst part.

The day I learned to ring two bells without vomiting immediately, Nara refused wine at the supply table and I barely noticed.

We were in the tannery cellar with the noon heat pressing down through warped boards. Tamsin had sent a crate of stolen clinic spirits as a peace offering or a taunt. Cask Bren had sent mold ledger figures and a request for more armed escorts on the kiln road. Yarrow stood by the back wall with two folded reports and the look he wore now when he had already made half the decisions and only needed me to approve the unpleasant quarter of them.

Nara uncorked the spirits, sniffed once, and pushed the bottle toward Halvek.

"Not drinking?" I asked, eyes still on the route slate.

"Not at midday," she said.

That should have been enough to register because she drank at midday whenever the wound count justified it, which was most days.

I let the detail pass.

Yarrow set the first report on the table.

"Two scouts cut off at Saint Vellum bell row," he said. "Mera and Seben. Warden sweep closed both stairs. They still have the tower roof if nobody panics."

"How long?"

"Half an hour before the wardens bring a rope team. Less if someone decides to be clever with fire."

Halvek looked up from the spirits bottle.

"You can open that lane with bells," he said.

Nara's head came around sharply.

"He can lose more of himself with bells," she said.

"That too." Halvek never softened facts to improve their reception. "But range is what he has and time is what the scouts do not."

I had been practicing on scrap wards and dead signal lines for three weeks. One ring for a district veil or a narrow directional pulse. Two for broader command reach. Three for things no one sane should use twice in the same season.

We had the numbers written plainly now.

One ring: one hour, maybe a little more, gone from memory like a room bricked over.

Two rings: a day.

Three rings: a week, sometimes torn loose so raggedly the edges bled into the next one.

Cost math had improved my decision-making only in the sense that naming a crime more precisely does not stop it being a crime.

"Nearest bell?" I asked.

Yarrow answered at once.

"Kiln watchtower. Bronze salvage, hung three nights ago. I already sent Renn to clear the ladder. If you decide no, he'll stand down."

That was how he spoke now. Not asking permission to prepare the field. Only asking whether I wanted the final harm done.

"Go," I said.

He was moving before the word finished.

I caught Nara watching him leave.

"You taught him to do that," she said quietly.

She meant the speed. She meant the lack of visible hesitation. She meant more than that.

I had no time to answer honestly and no interest in answering dishonestly.

The kiln watchtower had once carried a fire bell large enough to mark shift changes across four wards. Now it carried a patchwork bronze throat pieced from salvaged chapel metal, river mooring bells, and one cracked funeral tongue Halvek insisted improved the resonance.

"It improves the honesty," he said.

I bled into the casting groove cut around the striker post, enough to wake the lattice but not enough to make my hands weak. The city spread below in heat haze and dirty light: broken scaffolds at Ashbridge, long ration lines by the soot kitchens, white shrine flags near the southern road where the wardens had started calling their checkpoints mercy stations.

Saint Vellum's bell row stood beyond all of it, a cluster of old towers and connected roofs with wardens moving at the base like beetles around a carcass.

Two rings meant a day.

I said it aloud because vows begin in clarity and so should ruin.

"Two bells," I said. "One day of memory for an open lane and a blind sweep."

Halvek put the striker in my hand.

"Make it exact," he said.

"I don't know how exact the loss will be."

"No," he said. "But you know what you're authorizing. That is what matters."

The first strike went through my teeth.

The second went through my spine.

Sound lifted out over the ward roofs not like noise but like pressure, a sheet of hammered air that folded alley echoes together and sent every loose pane shivering. Through the lattice I pushed the instruction shape Halvek had drilled into me: blind the west stair, magnify heat shimmer along the north roofline, pull the eye left while the bodies run right.

Pain answered cleanly.

That was the danger of the lattice. It made suffering feel obedient.

For one impossible second I saw the entire route at once. Wardens on the north stair looking the wrong way. Mera dragging Seben by the collar because his leg had gone bad. Yarrow's reserve pair already on the opposite roof because he had predicted my answer before I gave it. A gull lifting off a parapet at exactly the moment the false shimmer broke and made one rifleman flinch.

Then the bell stopped being outside me.

The city tipped.

I remember getting one hand on the post before my knees went.

I remember Halvek taking the striker before I dropped it and saying, from very far away, "Document immediately if you can still form sequence."

Then there was a clean place where the day should have been.

I came back in my own bed at the tannery with my boots still on and the taste of metal dried at the back of my throat. Afternoon light had shifted wrong. The room felt used by someone else.

My ledger sat open on the crate beside me.

Across the top of the page, in my own hand, written harder than usual:

Do not trust the blank feeling. You paid for it.

Below that:

Operation successful. Mera recovered. Seben recovered with leg fracture splinted. Renn arrow-wounded, nonfatal. You asked Nara the same question three times after. Stop asking it now.

There were burn marks at the bottom edge of the page, as if I had held the paper too close to a lamp without noticing.

The missing day had texture only through residue. Different shirt. A clean bandage at my side. A cup of bitterleaf gone cold near the bed. One line in smaller script that was not mine:

You kept trying to remember whether I had already told you to rest. I had.

Nara's hand.

I found her in the clinic rear room shaving a dressing roll into narrower strips.

"Did I ask you something yesterday?" I said.

She did not look up.

"Several things."

"Which one mattered?"

"None of them. That was the problem."

I waited. She kept cutting cloth with the quick, controlled movements that meant anger held in functional shape.

"You asked whether Mera got out," she said. "You asked whether Yarrow had returned. You asked if the bell had worked. Then you asked if we had already had this conversation. Then you asked again."

"Did I say anything else?"

That made her stop.

"You said the room felt borrowed," she answered. "You said you could hear the missing day scraping at the walls."

That sounded like me. Worse, it sounded accurate.

"I wrote it down," I said.

"Halvek says that is how you stay coherent."

"Do you disagree?"

She finally looked at me then, eyes flat with sleeplessness.

"I think coherence is a low ambition for a man trying to govern districts."

She handed me the fresh dressings and brushed past me, close enough that the back of her hand touched my wrist. There was nothing accidental in the contact and nothing openly claimed either. That had become our way of speaking when words would have forced us to admit more than either of us had planned for.

By evening, the city had already converted the rescue into myth.

The bell-witch on the kiln tower.

The dead scouts who walked down light.

The Veyr cripple who could reorder sight itself.

None of those versions mentioned that I had traded a day for two lives and spent the next empty hours asking the same questions like a head wound with handwriting.

I wrote the cost anyway.

If I stopped writing it, I would become the kind of man I had been raised among.
