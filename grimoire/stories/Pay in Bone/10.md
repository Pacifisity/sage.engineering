The wardens closed the harbor at dawn and called it purification.

They came in white coats marked with temple ash and a sunburst sigil that had never once fed a hungry child. Sanctified wardens, the kind the councils deployed when they wanted war to look holy. They blocked the river mouths with chain booms, cut off medicine barges, and posted bell-priests at each pier to bless the siege as if starvation could be consecrated.

By noon, Nara's runners were out of gauze, tincture, and clean blades.

"If the next barge doesn't break through," she told me in the tannery cellar, "I start choosing who receives pain dullers and who screams through amputation."

I left before she could keep speaking. I already knew what she was about to say: that this was still my fault, even if I had not lit the first torch. Every road now curved toward my choices.

Irix and I mapped the docks from roofline to bilge. Tithe Docks had old embalming canals under the plankways, built in plague years to carry preservative oil for mass dead processing. The city had forgotten they existed. I had not.

"If we open them and spark," Irix said, tracing routes in charcoal, "we trap wardens between fire gutters and tide walls."

"And my river crews?"

He looked at me for one long beat. "Half, maybe more, if wind turns wrong."

The mirror-voice in a window behind him whispered yes before I answered.

At dusk I gave the order.

Yarrow moved with demolition teams under the pier lines, setting charges to crack the old canal caps. He did not flinch when two men vanished to crossbow fire beside him. Morn Hal's defected officers held the south docks with borrowed pikes and oilskin shields while my boat crews rowed straight into chain booms and hacked links under arrow rain.

When the caps blew, embalming oil rose like black breath through every seam in the docks.

I cast from the watchtower with both hands, feeding spark-lines down soaked planks. Fire raced outward in branching veins, turning each pier into a furnace trench. Sanctified wardens burned where they stood, then leapt into water already slick with flame. The tide itself caught. Men died screaming sermons.

So did mine.

Half my river crews never reached shore. Some burned in boats. Some drowned under burning tar. One captain kept rowing even after his sleeve caught, trying to clear wounded men before his own skin failed. I watched him go under still gripping an oar.

By midnight, the siege was broken. Medicine barges entered under smoke cover. Nara's cellar got its blades and cloth and pain syrup. The wardens withdrew to higher streets, leaving charred dead at low tide.

I walked the piers alone after first bell. Burned rope, boiled fish, and fat smoke clung to everything. I found Yarrow sitting on a crate, washing soot from his knife with dock water that reflected no stars.

"We won," he said.

"Yes."

"Then why do you look like we lost?"

Because we had. Because victory that requires burning your own crews into the tide is not victory, only arithmetic with better propaganda. Because everyone still breathing would remember who lit the canals.

"Because this is what winning costs now," I said.

He nodded like I had confirmed weather.

At dawn, Nara sent casualty totals and supply intake lists. I sent her names of the dead boat captains and ordered funeral ash stipends for each family. It was bureaucratic mercy. It changed nothing except the neatness of the ledger.

By evening, rumors were already moving through market lanes: the warlock turned water into fire; the warlock can burn tides; the warlock saved the docks; the warlock killed his own.

All of them were true.

The dock fires should have been enough to dominate the season. They weren't. Once the water turned toxic, every pressure point in the city started failing at once, and the war stopped arriving in sequence.

Three days after the dock fires, the fish floated belly-up in black water.

Oil sheens spread from pier to pier, and every net came up slick with poison. Protein prices tripled by second market bell, then doubled again when people realized this was not a one-day contamination. By night, ration lines became knife lanes. Men stabbed over dried eel strips. Women traded wedding metal for bone broth. Children learned to stand behind stronger bodies and wait for collapse.

I convened quartermasters, Guild runners, and two dock syndicates in the Nine Chimneys records hall.

"We ration by banner," I said. "Households register under ward flags. No flag, no allocation."

"That is forced loyalty," one syndicate clerk said.

"That is inventory control," I answered.

The second order hurt worse.

"Food barges receive armed escort only if they pay escort fees in cinders. No exceptions."

The hall went quiet. Everyone understood what that meant: I was converting famine into revenue and revenue into control. I told myself it was logistics. Nara called it what it was.

"You are charging starving people for the privilege of not being robbed," she said later, in the clinic corridor.

"I am charging merchants so they keep running routes."

"And merchants pass cost to families. Do not hide behind distance in the chain."

She was right. I signed the order anyway.

By week two, every person in lower wards carried a ration card stamped with ash-black dye and my sigil. The card tracked grain allotments, soup access, dried mushroom quotas, and salt shares. It also tracked behavior: riot marks, theft allegations, curfew breaches. Food became obedience made visible.

Yarrow administered one checkpoint with perfect efficiency. He denied a ration extension to a widow because her card lacked the required witness seal. I watched him do it without expression. Later, he asked if he had done right.

"You followed protocol," I said.

He nodded. "Then why did she look at me like I killed someone?"

Because sometimes procedure is murder done in clean ink.

I did not say that.

The mirror-voices helped optimize routes. One told me to move barley stores upriver before a guild strike. Another warned that temple alms kitchens were being infiltrated by warden informants tracking my ration network. The information was accurate. The source remained a fracture I could not safely explain.

At the end of the month, mortality dropped in three wards because ration regularity improved. In two wards, it rose because access rules excluded unregistered families, migrants, and anyone who refused banner enrollment.

People lived because of me and hated me for the terms.

Again: all true.

Nara posted a handwritten notice outside her clinic that made my captains furious:

FOOD SHOULD NOT REQUIRE FEALTY.

I left it up.

It changed nothing in policy. It changed something in me, though not enough. I started keeping two ledgers: one for logistics outcomes, one for ethical debts. The first showed stability gains. The second showed names.

The second ledger got heavier faster.

The regime fracture followed famine faster than I expected.

Glassfields had already made Morn Hal inconvenient to her own side. The harbor disaster finished the work. By the time her column came east, she was arriving less as an enemy commander than as proof that the state was beginning to cannibalize its competent officers.

Morn Hal arrived under white cloth, with half a battalion and no flags.

I met them at the east road kill-box with three hundred riflemen hidden in slag berms and two fire teams ready to erase the convoy if the defection turned out to be theater. Morn rode at the front in stripped armor: no rank braid, no sanctified insignia, no temple mark. Regime courts had scapegoated Morn for the Glassfields fallout and dock losses, stripped command, and posted a warrant for ritual negligence.

When a state punishes competence to protect pride, professionals defect.

"I brought maps," Morn said, dismounting. "Shrine armories, reserve chapel stores, hidden bell towers, and emergency temple vault routes. I brought forty-six officers and two hundred ninety rank soldiers."

"Why here?" I asked.

"Because your enemies are liars and your methods are honest about being monstrous."

It was the best recruitment pitch I had heard in years.

I accepted the defection publicly in the quarter yard. Bread, salt, oath witness, official seal. Then I accepted it privately by assigning Yarrow as shadow auditor.

"Track Morn's people," I told him. "Where they sleep. Who they meet. What orders they question. Record everything."

"Do I report suspicion or only evidence?" he asked.

"Both. Mark which is which."

He vanished into the new battalion like smoke.

Morn's maps paid immediate dividends. We struck two hidden shrine armories in one week and pulled enough powder and shot to arm three additional ward brigades. We seized a bell tower network that had been feeding warden sightlines into my convoy routes. For once, betrayal bought measurable survival.

It also bought paranoia.

My old officers resented Morn's arrival. They had bled through years of retreat and scarcity, and now a former enemy arrived with rank-level intelligence and instant access to command. There were tavern fights, bunkhouse stabbings, two duel challenges, and one sabotage attempt on Morn's supply wagons.

I crushed all of it with public punishment and private leverage from Lio's ledgers. Cohesion returned. Trust did not.

Nara cornered me after hearing I'd assigned Yarrow as internal spy.

"You made a child your secret police," she said.

"He's not a child."

"That sentence is the problem."

I had no answer that wasn't another confession.

By month end, Morn's battalion had integrated enough to function in mixed formations. Their officers were disciplined, their doctrine useful, their loyalties uncertain. Yarrow's reports arrived nightly: terse, precise, often damning.

No direct treachery. Plenty of conditional commitment.

Morn knew I was watching. One night, in the command forge, Morn handed me a folded map and said, "You are right not to trust me. Keep doing it. People who trust too early die first."

I put the map with the others and marked the moment in my private book.

Defection had increased our military capacity.

It had also institutionalized suspicion as policy.

That was the trade now: every gain came with a poison clause.

Halvek chose that exact moment to tell the truth about the theft.

If he had confessed months earlier, I might have killed him and called it justice. After Morn's maps started paying out, utility had already trained me to pause one breath longer than revenge wanted.

Halvek confessed in the quarry chapel with rain hitting broken slate overhead.

"Before the theft," he said, "Matron Ys consulted me on ritual legality."

I stared at him and did not understand for a full second. Then the words arranged themselves.

"You advised my mother," I said.

"On whether transfer rites could be made technically lawful under old temple exception."

My hand was around his throat before I decided to move.

I drove him against the altar stone and lifted until his boots scraped. The mirror-voices screamed approval from wet wall tiles. One of them said finish it. One said ask first. One said both.

Halvek clawed at my wrist and managed three words.

"I can... fix..."

I let him drop.

He coughed blood into his sleeve and sat against the altar base, breathing like each inhale had legal terms attached.

"You do not get forgiveness," he said. "You get utility. I can give you a method to crack stolen-Origin stability."

I wanted to kill him anyway. I wanted to break his jaw and leave him for quarry dogs. I wanted one clean act that did not require calculation.

Instead I listened.

He described the fracture model: stolen Origin power binds through a triad of blood resonance, vow continuity, and harmonic anchor. Break two points at once and the user destabilizes rapidly. We had already been attacking blood resonance through kin isolation. The missing piece was harmonic anchor disruption at ritual distance.

"You need a sympathetic lattice keyed to the thief's cast pattern," Halvek said. "Not her blood. Her rhythm. How she breathes before release. How she loads pain into spell structure."

"You can map it?"

"Yes. I helped codify the legal frame that made her theft possible. I know the architecture."

That sentence almost made me hit him again.

I kept him alive because his betrayal was useful.

That was the moment I despised myself most clearly.

Nara found us before dawn. Halvek still shaking, me covered in slate dust and chapel water.

"What happened?" she asked.

"Truth," I said.

She looked at Halvek, then at my hands, then at the altar with blood drops drying in little black suns.

"You both smell like cowardice," she said, and left.

Within a week, Halvek and I built the first draft of the harmonic counter-lattice. It required recordings of Sereth's cast signatures from prior engagements, bell-resonance overlays from captured chapel towers, and an anchor object from House ritual stock. We used a broken votive rod taken from my family chapel before the war. It hummed when aligned.

Yarrow watched all of it in silence.

On the fourth night he asked, "If Halvek helped the theft, why is he alive?"

"Because he can help end it."

"So guilt buys immunity if useful enough?"

"No," I said.

He waited. I corrected myself.

"Yes. Sometimes."

He nodded like he had expected that answer and was disappointed only by how long it took me to say it.

The lattice worked in testing against sample wards. Against Sereth, we still needed proximity and timing we did not yet have. But for the first time in months, the path was more than rage and attrition. It was technical, narrow, and maybe real.

In my private ledger I wrote one line and underlined it twice:

I spared a traitor because revenge requires engineers.

Then I shut the book before I had to keep writing.
