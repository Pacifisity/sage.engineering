I was born in Hollow Spire when it still had music on festival nights.

By the time I came back, House Veyr had turned it into intake infrastructure.

Recruitment prison is the polite term. In practice it was a processing block where debtors, levy dodgers, and political nuisances were sorted into labor contracts, forced labor, or ritual extraction programs no clerk would say aloud. Iron cages in old guild halls. Family separation by productivity score. Children tagged for "future yield assessment."

I read the intake boards from a rooftop and felt my teeth hurt from clenching.

Yarrow looked over my shoulder and whispered, "We break it tonight, right?"

"We break records first," I said. "Cells second."

If you free bodies without burning paperwork, the system reclaims them in a week.

We started with the archive hall.

Irix neutralized two tower sentries without noise. Halvek jammed the alarm bell with a wax wedge and prayer wire. I cut through the side door rune using the lattice pain channel and almost blacked out when the feedback hit my sternum like a hammer. We got inside anyway.

Shelves. Rows and rows of intake slates. Names reduced to quotas. Work indices. Punishment notes. Family links in red ink for collateral chaining.

I took the first torch myself.

The paper fire ran hungry and fast. Smoke curled under rafters while Yarrow and two dock fighters smashed lock cabinets with sledge bars. By the time wardens breached the front hall, the intake books were black lace and floating ash.

Then we hit the cell blocks.

Breaking a prison in theory looks like heroism. In practice it is chaos with screaming.

Some detainees ran immediately. Some froze and refused to move without children they could not find. One man attacked us with a broken stool leg because he thought we were transfer officers in disguise. A woman with a split lip spat in my face and said, "Another warlord. Different coat."

She still followed when we opened the outer gate.

Street fighting bled into dawn. We held intersections long enough to move three hundred people out through the ash lane and into temporary shelter points at old textile halls. We also lost twelve fighters, including two who had joined me at Tithe Docks and never saw thirty.

By noon, Hollow Spire was technically "liberated" and practically starving.

Freed people needed food, medicine, shelter, and security in a district where I had just burned the only records system controlling ration distribution. Guild stores closed. Warden response columns formed at the perimeter. House Veyr posted emergency replacement authority through proxy captains.

Victory lasted six hours before logistics arrived.

Yarrow stood beside the pump with ash in his hair and asked, "Did we free them or recruit them?"

"Both," I said.

He didn't argue. He just started helping the ex-clerks sort ration names into usable lines.

At the assembly square I stood on a broken pump and told the crowd the truth I could not make kind.

"I can get you out," I said. "Or I can keep you here and draft a defense line. Either way, idle mouths die first. If you stay, you work and you fight."

Murmurs turned to shouts. One man called me tyrant. Another called me savior. A woman from the cell block front stepped through both arguments, spat near my boot, and said, "Pick one, warlock."

"I pick survival," I said.

That is how conscription began.

By nightfall we had intake camps reversed into war bands: former prisoners on perimeter watch, former debt clerks repurposed to ration accounting, former porters carrying sandbags to choke alleys against expected warden push.

Nara arrived after dark, saw the setup, and closed her eyes briefly.

"You did it," she said. "You really built a state out of triage."

"I built a chance," I said.

"No," she said softly. "You built obligation."

She was right. Obligation is heavier than banners.

I ended the day in a burned office that had once processed my own district as numbers. I wrote the line in the ledger while cinders still drifted through the window frame.

Hollow Spire intake complex destroyed.
Population freed and drafted.
Liberation now requires governance under fire.

I underlined governance until the nib snapped.

Nara's clinic ran out of bandages before it ran out of patients.

Three connected cellars under the tannery held everything: surgery tables, fever cots, supply bins, and a floor drain that carried blood into the waste channel by bucket load. The place smelled like lye, copper, and sleeplessness.

"You promised supply runs," Nara said without looking up from a burn case.

"The cart was intercepted."

"Then plan better," she said. "These wraps are third-use. He'll die slowly because we have cloth and no clean cloth."

I offered another escort run. She shook her head.

"You keep spending fighters to get supplies for fighters you spent. That's a loop, Cael."

Upstairs, Halvek gave the same warning in his own words.

"Your clinic has a purpose deficit," he said. "Nara thinks you're saving people only to spend them again."

"She's not wrong," I said.

He told me to give her full medical authority and stop overruling triage decisions.

I did.

That changed more than I expected.

When we captured a burned warden who might have had useful intel, Nara looked at me and waited. I told her to make the call. She declined treatment. He died two days later.

She didn't thank me. She didn't need to.

Around the same week, the Black Weight behind my eyes got worse.

It started as peripheral shimmer. Then shape. Then presence.

"Avoid mirrors," Halvek said. "Reflection feeds it."

"What is it?"

"A tax. Sereth left you flawed infrastructure. It runs, but it watches while it runs."

I stopped looking at polished metal and still felt watched.

By month end, the clinic had three additional surgeons and better flow discipline. Survival rates improved in simple cases and worsened in severe ones because Nara was taking harder patients. She called it honest medicine.

The district called it Warlock Mercy.

I let them keep the phrase.

One evening Nara pulled Yarrow into a tendon repair because she needed steady hands.

He held the line without shaking while blood covered his sleeves.

"You have good hands," she told him.

He looked at me before answering her.

"Should I keep helping?"

"If you want to," I said.

He nodded once and stayed.

That was the day I stopped pretending he was outside this war.

At midnight I updated casualty logs and then wrote a second list: names saved, names lost. My hand shook harder on the names.

Halvek found me staring at the page.

"Keep that tremor," he said. "When it goes, you go with it."

I told him humanity was inefficient.

"Yes," he said. "But it's the only brake you've got."

The bolt came from a roof I'd already cleared.

Yarrow and I were moving through Ash District, reading mark-stones for trap notes and gang borders, when the shot missed me by less than a meter.

Before I could locate the archer, Irix dropped him from a higher line with a knife throw that looked casual and wasn't.

"Your perimeter is sloppy," he said as he landed. "Two more on the north corner."

I sent half my escort north and took the lane with the rest. Forty seconds later, the corner was ours and the shooters were either dead or gone.

Afterward, Irix gave me a clean summary.

Five active contracts on my head this week. Two House-backed. One warden channel. Two private buyers.

"Why tell me?" I asked.

"Because I'd rather work the problem than sell the head," he said.

I offered a week contract. He accepted on conditional terms: no oath, no loyalty lock, free exit if command ethics crossed his line.

Over the next days he watched everything.

Route discipline. Escort spacing. How Yarrow deferred. How Halvek advised. How often I ignored both.

On day three he caught me staring into a rain barrel when the Black Weight shifted behind my reflection.

"That's not borrowed power," he said. "Borrowed returns. This behaves like infection."

He wasn't wrong.

At week end he gave me his assessment in plain language.

"You're not building a gang. You're building infrastructure."

"Same thing with better uniforms," I said.

"No," he said. "Gangs collapse with leaders. Infrastructure keeps running. Even when the builder is dead or worse."

He asked for permanent terms: fixed revenue share, operational autonomy, no vow, explicit right to leave.

I agreed.

Halvek cornered me after.

"You're collecting people not bound by debt," he said.

"That's the point."

"No," he said. "That's the risk. They are your conscience only as long as they can walk away."

That night I wrote the contract terms into the ledger.

No oath. No debt chain. Right of defection.

For the first time in weeks, that felt safer than loyalty.

Lio Ferr arrived with three ledgers and ash on his sleeves.

He had spent two days in temple basements translating old Veyr records.

"Stolen Origin work doesn't stabilize on pride," he said. "It stabilizes on blood."

He showed me two centuries of family rite logs. Every case where someone carried an Origin fragment without clean inheritance required recurring kin donations. Quarterly in some generations. Monthly in others. Always escalating.

Sereth had my sigil.

Which meant she needed Veyr blood to keep it from eating her.

Lio's second ledger listed recent disappearances from distant branch lines: river traders, minor clerks, two teenage cousins with no registered House ties. Quiet removals, regular cadence.

Maintenance.

"How many still reachable?" I asked.

"Forty-three," he said. "Scattered and mostly unaware."

I started a hidden census.

Locate every living Veyr branch relative. Move them under protection before Sereth's collectors reached them. If she couldn't feed the stolen sigil, she'd weaken over time.

Halvek read the same list and called it what it was.

"You're turning relatives into strategic inventory," he said.

"She already did."

"Yes," he said. "Now you are too."

Over four weeks, Irix and our contacts moved twenty-six relatives into safe locations. The oldest negotiated relocation like a merchant contract. The youngest came because their parents were told the truth in one sentence:

Your blood is now a target.

The other seventeen stayed out of reach, hidden, skeptical, or too far behind bad roads.

Sereth's response came fast.

More raids. Higher bounties. Marrow hounds pushed farther into our perimeter lanes.

She had felt the shortage.

That night I stared at the census and felt the Black Weight shift behind my eyes, as if it approved the logic.

I wrote one line in the ledger before sleep.

Countermeasure established. Family secured in part. Cost ongoing.

The break-in worked.

The aftermath didn't.

We hit the Guild grain vault on Merchant's Rise before dawn. Irix and I took point. Four guards dropped quietly. Lock failed on the third pressure cycle. We loaded forty-three sacks and moved them into lower-ward distribution points.

Nara had planned for controlled lines.

Instead, word spread faster than carts moved.

Hundreds hit the first lane. Then more. Queue discipline collapsed into crush. Two stabbings, one trampling death, and a wave of panic that turned food into a weapon before breakfast.

"Stop distribution," Nara ordered when she found me. "We're feeding a riot."

We shut it down, reset by district lists, and resumed in controlled batches by noon.

By sunset, the Guild answered.

Three well systems in the lower wards were poisoned with caustic runoff.

Nara's cellars flooded with patients. Split lips, internal burns, dehydration collapse. She looked at me over a table full of survivors and said, "This is retaliation for your raid, and we're the ones paying it."

For six days we ran clean-water convoys under armed escort.

Forty-nine dead. Nineteen critical. More with permanent injury.

We captured six paid poisoners and made the punishment public. It stabilized fear, not trust.

Irix reviewed the numbers at the end of the week.

"Food security improved," he said. "Territory support increased. Casualties severe but within historic range."

"And?" I asked.

"People don't think you saved them," he said. "They think your help is dangerous."

He was right.

In the ledger I recorded the operation as both win and warning.

Vault raid successful.

Civilian confidence unstable.

Infrastructure gap exposed.
