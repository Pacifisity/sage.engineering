I sent for Yarrow.

We left before dark.

Early autumn came in with iron rain and wind that smelled like wet rope and old grain. The kind of weather that turns carts over and excuses delays until somebody with a seal calls those delays sabotage.

Ashbridge carried a third of District Nine's food in bad weeks and half in worse ones. One stone span over black water with timber scaffolds bolted to older arches, patched six times in eight years, prayed over more than repaired.

If wardens locked that crossing under emergency search law, market shelves emptied inside ten days.

Morn Hal knew that.

I knew that.

By moonrise we watched the bridge from a tannery roofline with rain stinging our faces and route chalk turning to paste on my palm.

Yarrow lay beside me, still enough to disappear in shadow, counting rotations under his breath.

"Front line, twelve," he whispered. "Rear cart check, six. Clerk table by center post. They doubled pikes since noon." 

"Any blind lane?"

"Only if we make one." 

At first bell, Hal's proclamation crew arrived with braziers and legal slates.

The crier read it in temple cadence:

All crossings subject to seizure search under collapse-prevention statute.

Unregistered manifests impounded.

Persons aiding kin-traitor Cael Veyr prosecuted as material collaborators.

Then Hal stepped forward himself, rain-dark cloak, no helmet, voice clear enough to carry over wagon noise.

"No confiscation for compliant carts," he said. "No harm for lawful passage. Cooperate and move." 

He sounded almost reasonable.

That was why people obeyed long enough to get trapped.

I sent a runner with a wrapped bribe packet and terms before dawn.

Ten ashmarks, route parity, and one-night lane quiet for clinic carts.

Hal sent the runner back with the packet unopened and a short note in clean block script.

Not for coin.

I sent fear next.

Two sabotage teams hit outlying levy sheds, burned one manifest stack, and left Hal's patrol sergeant alive with my warning carved into the table plank beside him:

OPEN THE BRIDGE OR LOSE THE ROAD.

By sunrise Hal had replaced both sheds, reinforced the center span with extra pikes, and posted three additional clerk tables.

No panic. No overreaction.

Just discipline.

That made him dangerous.

By afternoon queue length stretched from the toll arch to the fish stairs. Carts stood axle-to-axle in rain. Flour sacks soaked through canvas. A mother argued with a clerk over inspection fees until a warden tapped his pike shaft and ended the argument for her.

This was the shape of policy before famine becomes visible.

Delay first.

Loss after.

Yarrow returned from the south supports at second watch with blood on his sleeve and no change in breathing.

"Scout came under the bridge," he said. "Saw my marks." 

"And?"

"He's in the river." 

Second kill.

No tremor in his voice.

I nodded once, like this was just field maintenance, and hated how easy that nod came.

At dusk, I tried words one last time.

I walked onto the bridge under a white strip flag and empty hands. Hal met me at the center check table with two wardens and three levy clerks pretending not to listen.

Rain ran off his jawline. He looked tired and completely in command.

"You have one minute," he said.

"Open a no-search window for medicine carts and grain under fifty sacks," I said. "Night cycle only. You keep face, districts keep food." 

"And you keep your movement lanes."

"Yes." 

He studied me for a long beat.

"You are asking me to legalize selective smuggling under my own seal while half this city thinks I'm too soft already." 

"I'm asking you not to turn inspection into siege." 

Hal looked over the queues, then back to me.

"You burned my sheds and threatened my road captains this morning," he said. "Do not arrive at dusk wearing statesman language like a clean coat."

He wasn't wrong.

"Then call it what it is," I said. "A deal between men who know what hunger does to crowds." 

"I know exactly what hunger does," Hal answered. "I also know what happens when I cut exceptions for one armed operator with a bounty on his head." 

He leaned closer, voice lower.

"Your war is not the only fire here, Cael."

Then he stepped back and raised his hand.

"Minute's over."

Bribery failed.

Fear failed.

Negotiation failed.

Force was what remained.

I left the bridge and told myself I would disable the crossing, not destroy it.

Hold traffic for one day.

Break inspection architecture.

Force renegotiation.

No mass casualties.

Plans sound humane before physics gets a vote.

Ashbridge's center truss rested on three older sigil plates embedded in the stone ribs below deck. House engineers used them decades ago to stabilize flood surge loads. Half the ward-lines were cracked now, patched with cheap brass and prayer wax.

I could see the fatigue in them even through rain.

My cast design was narrow:

Pain-channel surge into the center ward seam.

Overload one support.

Drop the inspection platform.

Keep main stone arches intact.

No free casting.

I bit my cheek for blood, drew a quick anchor line across my palm with Yarrow's nail knife, and said the cost out loud under my breath so the magic could not pretend I hadn't agreed.

"Two years. One controlled breach. Center platform only." 

The payment took immediately.

Age does not arrive as a wrinkle. It arrives as subtraction.

Knees stiffening mid-step. Grip strength thinning by one impossible degree. Breath shortening where lungs used to answer cleanly.

Then power came up through the scar seam in my side and down my arm like heated wire.

I hit the first sigil node from beneath the span and felt it give.

Good.

Second node resisted, then cracked too hard.

Bad.

The third node was supposed to stay dormant.

Instead, rainwater bridged a broken brass line across the rib and fed the surge sideways into legacy supports no one had mapped in years.

I knew it had gone wrong before the stone moved.

The bridge made a sound like a wet tree tearing under winter weight.

People looked up.

Somebody shouted.

Then the center span dropped.

Not clean.

Not surgical.

A violent half-collapse that took deck, check tables, and a full cart line with it.

The inspection platform sheared first. Clerk slates flew like birds. Pikes hit stone and vanished.

Two grain carts tipped nose-first into the gap.

One porter jumped and almost made it.

A mother reached for two children on the rear cart as the axle snapped under them.

Everything became noise.

Stone thunder.

Wood scream.

Horses breaking harness.

River impact, heavy and final.

By the time dust mixed with rain and settled enough to count, the dead were already decided.

Two children.

Three tax clerks.

Five wardens.

One porter.

Eleven total.

Numbers are a coward's way to hold grief at distance.

I used them anyway.

Hal survived because he had shifted two meters left at the last second to shove one clerk toward the rail. He went down hard, came up blooded, and started pulling survivors with his own hands before his officers did.

Competence under disaster.

I watched from the lower embankment with blood in my mouth and my cast hand shaking hard enough to blur my knife edge.

"Move," Yarrow said, grabbing my sleeve. "They're sweeping banks." 

I did not move.

Not until I saw the second child's red scarf disappear under black water.

Then I moved too fast, slipped on limestone slick, and went to one knee with pain lancing up my spine like someone was finishing Sereth's surgery all over again.

By the time we reached kiln safe lane, my pulse was wrong and my hands would not stop trembling.

Two years, I'd declared.

The cast felt like more.

Not in lifespan math.

In consequence.

Nara found me on her table before dawn, shirt cut open, side seam bleeding where channel strain had reopened scar tissue.

She cleaned the wound in silence at first. Water ran pink into the basin. Her hands stayed steady.

Outside, bells were already tolling death count.

When she finally spoke, it was quiet enough that I almost wished she had shouted.

"Did you mean to kill them?"

I stared at the ceiling beams and answered the only thing that was true.

"I meant to break a checkpoint." 

She tied off the stitch line and her fingers stayed at my ribs one second longer than procedure required.

"You broke a food artery," she said.

No accusation in her tone.

Assessment.

Same as always.

Worse than accusation.

Yarrow sat on the bench by the door with his wet boots in his hands and said nothing while she worked. His sleeve still carried someone else's blood from earlier.

When Nara left to check flood casualties, he finally asked,

"Was this the plan?"

"No," I said.

"Will we say that out loud?"

I thought about queue lines, dead children, Hal hauling survivors from broken stone, and what public truth does to command when command is already mostly rumor.

"We'll say the bridge had to close," I said.

Yarrow's jaw tightened once.

He nodded.

He was learning.

Later that morning I walked back to the collapse edge in civilian coat and hood up. Refugees were already setting tarp camps on both banks. Guild men were marking temporary ferry points with chalk. Wardens were posting emergency ration caps before the bodies were all identified.

Crisis metabolism.

Faster than mourning.

In a rain puddle beside the broken stone, my reflection looked at the gap and almost smiled.

When I turned away, I could still feel it watching.

Black Weight seemed satisfied with the scale.

At second watch I opened the ledger and wrote with a hand that kept slipping on the pen.

Entry Six.

Early autumn, month eleven from exile.

Ashbridge operation failed design parameters.

Intent: disable inspection platform and force route renegotiation.

Result: center-span partial collapse; trade artery severed.

Confirmed dead: 2 children, 3 tax clerks, 5 wardens, 1 porter.

Strategic outcome: Warden checkpoint neutralized; immediate transport halt across three districts.

Projected secondary outcome: grain shock within one week, famine pressure increase, expanded levy enforcement.

Personal cost: declared 2 years lifespan; severe channel backlash; scar seam reopened.

I sat with the page until ink dried and added the line the chapter had earned.

Personal note: I intended to block a bridge. I created a choke point for famine.

By evening, bread queues were already doubling at kiln markets.

By night, people were saying my name like a prayer and a curse in the same breath.

By dawn, the first emergency grain tax notices went up.
