Temple bells rang seizure hour at first light, and District Nine answered by hiding anything worth taking.

Shop shutters came down in sequence. Clinic doors moved from open to half-open to barred. Street altars lost their donation bowls. Men with clean coats and dirty ledgers stopped standing in obvious places and started standing where traffic narrowed.

The city did not panic.

It optimized.

I watched Warden Yard Seventeen from a kiln roof through cracked green glass and counted the same cycle for a fourth day.

Two gate wardens at the front, one with a bad knee who favored left turns.

One clerk team rotating every two hours through the rear inventory lane.

Lime carts entering at dusk, unloading for nine minutes, then clearing to canal side.

Nara's three medicine crates under red tarp against the west wall, marked with her clinic seal and seizure wax over the top.

The first two days I had told myself patience was strategy.

By day four, patience was another word for infection.

My shoulder seam was holding, but the extraction line at my spine was hot under the bandage and throbbing in time with my pulse. Nara's temporary sutures were doing what temporary sutures do: delaying consequence, not deleting it.

At noon I went back to her clinic through steam lanes and tannery runoff and found her cleaning an amputation saw in boiled lye.

She did not look up when I came in.

"If you're here for clean dressings," she said, "sit. If you're here for miracles, leave."

"I have crate timing," I said.

That made her glance up.

"And?"

"No clean entry without casting."

She dried the saw, set it down, and took my chart slate from the desk corner.

"Then pick what you lose before the spell picks for you," she said.

"Blood I can afford."

"You keep saying that like blood and memory aren't neighbors." She tapped the extraction seam note with a fingernail. "You're running damaged channels without a core. If you force concealment too hard, something else pays."

I knew she was right. I did not have a better option.

She stepped closer, cut the old dressing free, and checked the seam line with two quick fingers.

"You need rest," she said.

"I need those crates."

"You need both."

"I only have one." 

She held still for one long second, then rewrapped my shoulder with tighter cloth and tied off the knot against my collarbone.

"If you cast," she said quietly, "name the cost out loud before you start. Do not improvise halfway through because panic makes you generous with your own life."

She pressed a small mirror shard into my palm.

"Concealment needs a reflective anchor. Glass is cleaner than canal water."

I looked at the shard. It held a warped slice of my face: one eye too low, mouth split by old blood at the lip.

"You keep these for patients?" I asked.

"I keep them for stitches in bad light," she said. "Don't romanticize it."

When I moved to leave, she caught my sleeve.

"If this fails, don't come back here with wardens on your heels," she said. "I can't protect everyone in this room and you in the same minute."

That was the hard edge.

Then she added, almost under her breath, "If it works, knock three times, pause, then once. I don't open for panic knocks anymore."

That was the part she didn't bill.

Dusk came wet and metallic. Rain pushed smoke low over the lanes and turned every lamp halo into a blurred yellow wound.

I crossed two roofs, dropped into a drainage run behind the yard wall, and waited under the lime chute until the cart team rolled out and the rear lane went blind for exactly the nine minutes I'd measured.

Three crates.

One narrow lane.

Two patrol loops converging at minute seven.

No time for a brute lift and run.

I cut my palm with the guard knife, drew a spiral on the mirror shard with blood, and held it close enough to see my own eye tremor.

"Cost declaration," I whispered. "Memory for concealment. Keep body function. Keep movement."

The words sounded formal and ridiculous in rain and gutter stink.

They still mattered.

I spoke the anchor line and pressed power through damaged channels.

The veil took on the first pulse.

Not invisibility.

A misalignment.

Eyes sliding past where I stood, minds preferring the doorframe shadow, attention finding easier things to look at.

I moved.

The first crate dragged heavier than its size suggested, sloshing medicinal spirit against wood slats. I got it to the chute mouth, shoved it through, went back for the second.

Boots at the lane mouth.

A clerk voice.

"Rear tally now. Captain wants—"

I flattened against the wall and held breath until spots flashed at the edge of vision.

They looked straight toward me and did not see me.

The second crate cleared.

The third stuck halfway through the chute where old mortar had collapsed.

I pushed.

Nothing.

I pushed harder and felt channel heat spike under my ribs.

The veil wavered.

A warden turned his head.

Panic is loud inside bone.

I fed the cast again without fresh declaration.

That was the mistake.

Pain did not hit first.

Absence did.

One instant I knew exactly what I was doing.

Next instant I was kneeling in lime slurry with blood on both hands, the third crate beside me, and no memory of the previous few breaths except a white hiss at the back of my skull.

Alarm rune flared red on the rear post.

I grabbed the crate, shoved it through the chute blind, and followed it into the drainage run as bells started climbing toward full alarm.

I don't remember crossing the next two alleys.

I remember arriving at Nara's back door with all three crates stacked against the step and my shoulder seam reopened, hot blood running down my ribs inside the shirt.

Three knocks.

Pause.

One knock.

The door opened.

Her apprentice hauled in the first crate, then swore when he saw the blood.

Nara came through the curtain already gloved, took one look at my face, and said, "Table. Now."

I sat. She cut cloth away and resealed the split stitch line while I watched steam bead on the ceiling and tried to keep my breathing even.

"What did you spend?" she asked.

"Memory," I said.

"How much?"

"I don't know yet."

She finished the seam, tied the knot, and held pressure over the fresh line.

"Then test it," she said.

"Now?"

"Now." Her voice stayed flat. "Before your brain lies to you about what stayed intact."

I closed my eyes and reached for easy things first.

My name.

Still there.

Sereth's face in projection fire.

Still there.

The route pattern at Yard Seventeen.

Still there, mostly, with one clean gap where minute seven should have been.

Nara watched me and waited.

"Something's missing," I said.

"Be specific."

I tried for my father's voice.

Not a story.

Not a lesson phrase.

Just the sound of it.

For a second I thought I had it: low, road-rough, patient.

Then the sound collapsed into static and cracked-lamp hum.

I opened my eyes too fast.

"I can't hear him," I said.

Nara didn't move.

"Hear who?"

"My father." The words came out smaller than I meant. "I have his face. I have his hands over a map. I have his boots on stone. I don't have his voice."

Her jaw tightened once, then released.

"That's the bill," she said. "You paid it."

I laughed once without meaning to and wiped blood off my lip with the back of my wrist.

"One year for fire," I said. "A voice for a lane shift."

"Don't bargain with the scale right now," she said. "Just record it before shock edits the memory."

She pushed my ledger and ink toward me.

"Write while it's raw."

My hand shook hard enough that the first line blotted.

Entry Three.

Operation: Warden Yard Seventeen retrieval.

Result: Three clinic crates recovered.

Immediate bodily cost: shoulder seam reopened; channel burn along extraction line.

Immediate strategic cost: alarm escalation, rear-yard pattern likely compromised for future use.

Payment event: memory extraction during concealment reinforcement.

Loss identified: father's speaking voice no longer accessible.

I stared at the last line until the letters blurred.

When I looked up, Nara was watching me in that same unsentimental way she watched wounds.

Assessment first. Feelings later, if there was time.

"Sleep in the back room tonight," she said.

"I can move."

"You can collapse while moving." She took the ledger, blew the ink dry, and set it beside the cot. "Back room."

I didn't argue.

Her back room was a narrow space behind stacked linen crates and a shelf of dried herbs. One cot, one cracked wash basin, one wall mirror clouded by age and steam etching.

I lay down with boots on and knife under the blanket edge and told myself I'd stay awake long enough to map next routes.

Instead I woke sometime past midnight with the mirror in my line of sight and the room too quiet.

For a few seconds I thought I was still in House Veyr surgery because the light was wrong and my back hurt in the old pattern.

Then I saw my reflection.

It was me.

Almost.

Face shape right. Bruise line right. Mouth split right.

But the mouth moved half a beat before I did, as if finishing words I had not spoken yet.

I held still.

The reflection held still.

Then, very slowly, it mouthed something soundless.

I could not hear it.

I sat up, reached for the mirror shard Nara had given me, and looked from shard to wall glass.

Two reflections.

Two delays.

Neither matched my breathing.

Pain crawled down my spine like cold wire. Not wound pain. Recognition pain.

I knew enough about compromised channels to name what this was and not enough to stop it.

Black Weight starts small.

Not with madness.

With tiny mismatches your tired brain can still explain away.

A missing minute.

A missing voice.

A reflection that seems to move first if you haven't slept.

I could have called for Nara.

I didn't.

Some truths get harder to carry once someone else confirms them out loud.

At dawn she found me awake on the cot edge with the ledger open and my fingers pressed hard enough into the paper margin to leave crescent marks.

She set a cup of boiled bitterleaf beside me.

"Fever's down," she said. "Tremor's up."

I nodded.

"I saw something in the mirror," I said.

She didn't ask what.

"Then stop staring at mirrors," she said.

"That simple?"

"No." She cinched fresh dressings at my shoulder and tied the knot clean. "But simple rules keep people alive when complex rules fail."

From the front lane came the morning noise of District Nine recalculating after another night of seizures.

New prices shouted over old debt.

Two wardens arguing about patrol reassignment.

A child crying because a ration stamp had been marked invalid.

Nara pulled one of her recovered crates open and checked the glass vials inside one by one.

"You kept your side of the deal," she said.

"For now," I said.

"For now is all this city sells."

She closed the crate and wrote a new figure in her ledger.

Outstanding debt reduced.

Maintenance schedule required.

I watched the ink dry and understood this was the next shape of my life.

Not revenge as one clean strike.

Revenge as logistics, paid in pieces.

Later that day, in a lane mirror clouded by dye steam, I caught my reflection watching me before I looked directly at it.

I kept walking.

That night I added one more line beneath Entry Three.

Operational note: avoid reflective surfaces when possible.

Personal note: I can still remember what Father taught. I cannot hear him teaching it.

The two facts sat side by side on the page and refused to reconcile.

By second watch, I had route sketches for two new courier lines and one quiet corner of market where watchers changed slower than bells.

By third watch, I had learned the city had a new idiom for people like me.

Pay in bone.

By dawn, I met the boy who tried to cut my purse.
