Tithe Docks smelled like fish rot, rope tar, and revolt.

By the week after the lattice ritual, the bread riots had become organized. Not ideology. Logistics. Dock laborers, warehouse women, and debt-haulers started setting ration lines by gang, then family, then whoever had enough people to keep a queue from turning into a knife circle. Warden patrols showed up mostly to collect levy slips and confiscate sacks for "emergency redistribution."

Which meant theft with paperwork.

I climbed a crane spine above the docks and watched the morning cycle with Yarrow at my shoulder. Two guild clerks took debt payments at stall nine. A Warden sergeant took twenty percent from each cart at gate two. One old woman with three grandchildren paid twice and still left with half a sack of worm grain.

"If we cancel the debt books here," Yarrow said, "people will follow us."

He said it like weather, not hope.

He was right. He was also twelve.

At noon a labor foreman named Cask Bren called a crowd under the rust bell and offered me terms in front of two hundred starving people.

"You void bone debt notes for dock families," he said. "We close the wharf to warden boats and move cargo only under your route marks."

It was the first governance deal anyone had put in my hands. Not a heist, not an ambush, not survival. Administration with knives behind it.

Halvek pulled me aside before I answered.

"Careful," he said. "Promises to crowds behave like vows. Not cleanly, but close enough to punish pride."

"I need the docks."

"Then bargain in constraints, not absolutes."

I went back to the bell and made the mistake anyway.

"Bring me your debt notes by dusk," I said. "I will burn every debt tied to dock labor families."

Cheering hit like surf. Men who called me bridge-breaker yesterday now called me captain. Women I had never met touched my sleeves and wept into rope-burned hands. For an hour I let it feel like momentum instead of liability.

By sunset, three things happened.

First, the debt pile was five times larger than expected, because half the district ran their debt through dock proxies once rumor spread. Second, a guild broker posted armed collectors at four lane exits and bought two local enforcers who had agreed to stand with us that morning. Third, Nara found me in the tannery and dropped a ledger in front of me hard enough to shake the table.

"You promised what you cannot fund," she said. "If you burn everything now, they starve in six days when merchants shut grain entirely. If you delay, they call you a liar by dawn."

I stared at the pages. Names, balances, blood-signatures, children listed as loan collateral. Systematic cruelty in tidy rows.

"Then we take the archive," I said. "Not just this pile. All of it."

Nara rubbed both hands over her face. "That is a war operation, not a speech correction."

"Good."

Outside, the docks started chanting my name again, slower now, suspiciously. Expectation has a different sound than devotion.

Yarrow watched the crowd from behind the bell post and kept one hand on the courier satchel like it was a shield.

Before midnight, I met Cask Bren in a net shed and rewrote the deal.

"I can void some debt tonight," I said. "The rest after we seize the ward ledgers."

He spat into the sand. "You promised all."

"And now I'm telling you the price of all."

He looked at the men behind me, at Yarrow with a courier satchel and no fear left in his posture, and then nodded once.

"You have two days," he said. "After that, the docks choose someone else to believe."

That night I burned a ceremonial stack of debt notes at the wharf bell and fed the flames with enough signatures to buy us temporary faith. The crowd roared. Soot stuck to my teeth.

"Are we helping them or buying time?" Yarrow asked when the first cheer wave faded.

"Both," I said.

He nodded, but he did not look convinced.

When the cheering died, I could still hear Halvek's warning under it.

Promises to crowds behave like vows.

I went back to the tannery and started planning the archive strike with blood still drying under my nails.

Warden Archive House Three had no windows on the ground floor and no pity in its filing system.

The building sat behind a low wall of sanctified brick near the old rail quarter, guarded less like a fortress and more like a tax church: disciplined shifts, clear lines, too much confidence in procedure. Debt is easiest to protect when people think paperwork is neutral.

We hit it on storm night.

Nara drew the floor plan from memory of earlier seizure hearings. Yarrow mapped patrol loops from two days of gutter recon. Halvek prepared a silence knot for the outer bell rope. I prepared blood and accepted, in advance, that this cast would cost more than bone-fire.

Entry route was through soot vents above the records hall. The vents were narrower than Nara remembered. I had to exhale to pass the first bend and tore the lattice seam on a rusted edge before my boots even hit tile.

Inside, rows of shelves held the city hostage by ink: debt notes, collateral rolls, child claims, labor transfer orders, penalty appendices stamped with temple seals. People think cages are iron. Most are paper.

Yarrow moved fast between aisles, marking bundles with wax chalk. Nara found the dock family ledgers and handed them to me without speaking. Her face did all the speaking needed.

Then the alarm rune lit on the far door.

A junior clerk had seen our lamp shutter reflection in a polished file plate and reached for the bell lever. Halvek's knot should have delayed the pull. Should have.

I did not have time for should.

I cut my forearm and cast freeze across the bell chain, forcing the metal to seize in place. The spell took, but not cleanly. Without core stability, the channel pulled from lifespan to complete itself. I felt the transaction in my knees first, then in my lungs: three months gone in one breath.

The clerk screamed anyway. We moved anyway.

We loaded what we could carry, then set fire to what we could not. Wet ledgers smoke black and slow, thick enough to blind hallways and trigger panic before flame reaches rafters. Wardens rushed the front doors into their own records room inferno while we exited through the lime chute Nara had marked in charcoal two hours earlier.

Rain hit us outside like thrown nails.

By dawn the dock square was full. I stood on a cargo crate with burnt ledgers at my feet and read names aloud while Yarrow fed debt notes to the brazier. Men cried. Women screamed blessings and threats in the same breath. One old porter kissed my blood-streaked hand and told me his grandson might live the winter now.

I let him believe that.

Because then came the bill.

By noon, Cinder Throne issued emergency seizure authority to every warden captain inside three districts. Medical carts were searched. Temple granaries were locked to civilian access. New patrol powers allowed house entry without warrant in "debt instability zones," which now meant all of Tithe Docks and half the lanes around it.

Nara counted inventory and swore so quietly I barely heard her.

"They'll choke medicine first," she said. "Punishment by infection."

She was right. Two of her suppliers vanished by evening. A third was found in the canal with his wrists wired to a debt stamp block.

At sunset, the dock bell rang again, and this time no one cheered.

Cask Bren stood under it with ten foremen and one question.

"We burned the records," he said. "Why are wardens still collecting?"

"Because law here doesn't protect paper," I said. "It protects power."

He looked at me for a long moment, then nodded like a man accepting weather he hates.

"Then next time," he said, "we burn men too."

I did not answer. I did not have to. Everyone there heard what came next in the silence.

That night, I added two lines to the ledger and watched the ink spread in rainwater drips from my sleeve.

Ledger strike successful.

Retaliation phase begun.

I left a third line unwritten for a long time.

Yarrow asked before sleep if old debts were really gone now.

"Some are," I said.

"And the rest?"

I looked at the wet ink and told him the only honest thing I had.

"The rest changed shape."

The bounty arrived with etiquette.

A runner in polished boots delivered the notice to three hundred public boards by noon, each copy stamped with Guild Chains seal wax and Cinder Throne co-signature. Reward tiers. Proof standards. Pardon clauses. A full legal menu for hunting me as employment.

Alive paid best.

By dusk, strangers started measuring my lanes.

You can spot a professional hunter by what they do not ask. They do not ask your name. They ask which rooftops hold weight, which bridges ring under sprint boots, which backroom surgeons take cash over faith. They buy silence first, maps second.

I made counter-moves fast. No route used twice. No sleep in the same district two nights running. Message chains split across three couriers, one always false. Yarrow handled decoys with the competence of someone too young to have acquired it.

"Three new faces at fish stair," he reported. "One missing two fingers, one monk haircut with crossbow callus, one woman pretending drunk but counting exits."

I circled the locations on the map.

Nara tightened my lattice dressings and said what both of us knew.

"You cannot outrun market logic forever. Someone will sell your night for their winter."

She was right. Someone did.

The first attempt came from a roofline near Brass Alley. Bolt from sixty paces, poison-waxed head. I only survived because the shot hit the wall where my neck had been half a second earlier when a gutter tile snapped under my heel and made me shift instinctively. Luck, not skill.

By the time I reached cover, the shooter was already gone.

The second attempt came cleaner.

I was crossing a laundry bridge over canal lane with Yarrow two steps behind when three crossbows rose from opposite roofs at once. Triangulated, disciplined, no warning calls. Perfect setup.

Then all three shooters dropped.

One bolt through each throat, fired from a higher angle I had not clocked.

A figure in dark cord cloth landed on the bridge rail, knife out, eyes amused.

"You're expensive," he said. "I wanted to meet the merchandise before I sold it."

Irix Thorn.

He introduced himself with a shallow bow as if we were at a banquet and not standing over bodies on wet boards.

"Guild Chains retained me," he said. "But your mortality profile improved after observation."

"You mean you changed your price."

He smiled. "I mean I like complicated work."

I should have killed him there. Instead I took his offer: temporary collaboration under mutual surveillance. He brought hunter methods, target sketches, and names of two brokers who had posted secondary bounties in cinders for anyone willing to bring my left hand as proof.

In exchange, he wanted freedom to leave whenever contract geometry shifted.

"You trust him?" Yarrow asked later.

"No," I said. "I budget him."

"That sounds like trust with paperwork," Yarrow said.

"It sounds like staying alive," I answered.

For three days, Irix proved useful and infuriating in equal measure. He disarmed one trap I missed, insulted Halvek's theology, and recited casualty math like poetry while cleaning his blades.

"Paranoia is expensive," he told me. "But cheaper than funerals."

By the end of the week, every safehouse we touched had to be burned, vacated, or rotated. Not because they were all compromised. Because eventually one would be.

I slept in stairwells, roof gutters, and one abandoned mint cellar where old coin dies still hung from chains like teeth.

On the seventh night, Nara handed me a folded notice with my own face on it and a new annotation in guild shorthand.

TARGET ADAPTING. EXPECT ESCORTS.

I laughed once without humor.

"Good," I said. "Let them adapt."

But when the lamps went out, I stayed awake with knife in hand and counted doors until morning.
