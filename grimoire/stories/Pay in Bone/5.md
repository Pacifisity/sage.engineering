Red Quarry was where old regimes buried things that still needed to work.

The outer pit held broken statues, collapsed ward engines, and condemned oath stones. The inner galleries held people: refugees, deserters, and debt-fugitives hidden behind slate curtains and false rockfall. Halvek used it as a training ground because no sound escaped the walls cleanly.

He brought me there before dawn with one instruction.

"Today you hold a ward until I tell you to stop."

"Against what?"

"Against failure."

He drew the circle in quarry grit mixed with my blood and furnace ash. Anchor points at cardinal cuts. Relief channels toward a drainage trench in case I convulsed. Nara stood outside the ring with water and clotting packs, already angry.

"This is not training," she said. "This is attrition."

Halvek did not argue. "Exactly."

I took position at center and opened the lattice.

The ward's purpose was simple: hide the inner galleries from sweep mages passing along the ridge road. If the shield dropped, the refugee pockets behind us would light up on sanctified mirrors like lantern targets.

For the first hour, it was manageable. Controlled pain, steady breath, defined output.

By hour three, the quarry wind changed and started carrying stone grit into my eyes and mouth. Each blink scratched. Each inhale stung. I kept the line anyway.

By hour five, my left hand started trembling hard enough to blur the circle marks when I re-traced them with blood. Nara tried to step in with a stabilizer injection. Halvek blocked her.

"If he can't hold this without sedation," he said, "he dies in the first siege that matters."

"He might die now," she snapped.

I wanted to tell both of them to shut up. I needed the breath for the ward.

At hour six, ridge scouts signaled incoming.

Not sweep mages. Warden skirmishers.

The shield had to widen to cover the approach tunnel where noncombatants were moving deeper into the quarry. Widening a blood ward while exhausted is how you shred channels permanently. I did it anyway.

Pain surged down my left arm like molten wire and never fully left. The ward held. Skirmishers passed above without locking target. Refugees stayed unseen.

When Halvek finally cut the circle, I collapsed face-first into grit and vomit. I could not close my left hand around the water skin Nara tried to give me. Fingers stuttered, then failed.

She looked at my hand and went very still.

"Tremor damage," she said. "Could settle. Could stay."

It stayed.

By evening I could still cast, but fine control in that hand was gone in waves. Cup lifting, thread tying, writing on narrow lines, all unreliable. Small losses that multiply under stress.

Halvek called it acceptable.

"You kept two hundred people alive," he said. "Your hand paid. Good trade."

Nara turned on him so fast I thought she might hit him.

"He is not a ledger line," she said.

Halvek didn't answer. He just looked back at the quarry wall like it had settled the argument for him.

I almost struck him and missed because of the tremor.

That night I practiced gripping a knife until my forearm cramped, then switched to the right hand and practiced again. Yarrow sat nearby pretending to clean boots while watching every failure.

"Looks bad," he said finally.

"It is bad," I said.

"You still alive though."

I looked at the shaking fingers and nodded.

"For now."

In the ledger, the entry took three attempts to write legibly.

Quarry ward successful. Left-hand tremor permanent under load.

I underlined permanent twice, each line a little crooked.

Sereth chose market hour for maximum humiliation.

At third bell, every public brazier in three districts flared with synchronized sigil light. Trade stalled mid-argument. Children stopped running. Even drunks shut up when House-grade ritual takes over civic fire.

Her face formed in flame above the square scales, calm and immaculate in white and black, my stolen power flickering in her pupils like held lightning.

"Cael Veyr," she said, voice carried by guild amplifiers, "is a plague vector and kin-traitor under confirmed temple review. His actions have destabilized food routes, debt law, and sanctified order. Deliver his heart and receive ten thousand ashmarks and pardon slips for all listed offenses."

Ten thousand.

Not a bounty. A declaration of war.

People around me looked at one another with the quick math of hunger and opportunity. One man reached subtly for his knife, then thought better when Yarrow stepped into view with a courier satchel and a stare older than his face.

I left the square before the broadcast ended and started moving assets within the hour. Safehouse rotations. Route burns. False sightings planted through fish markets and chapel queues. Irix called it "survival theater" and did his part with irritating competence.

By sunset, we intercepted Sereth's herald caravan on the ash road north of Cinder Arch.

Three wagons, silk covers, six escort wardens, one flame-amplifier mast for repeating bounty declarations in out-district markets. The escorts expected bandits. They got planning.

Yarrow collapsed the lead horse with a spike line across the roadbed. Irix dropped the rear driver from ridge shadow. I took the center wagon head-on and cut the mast cable before the herald could trigger alarm flare.

Fight lasted ninety seconds and felt longer.

Two escorts died immediately. One crawled into ditch fog and bled out cursing House Veyr and my mother by name. The herald, a narrow-shouldered man with perfect diction and no stomach for blood, tried to buy his life by offering route intelligence and a forged pass list.

"I only read what they give me," he said. "I don't make policy."

"Neither did the clerk on Ashbridge," I said.

He cried when I cut his tongue.

We took the caravan seals, burned the mast, and left the wagons stripped. At first light, I had the severed tongues nailed over the old Veyr district gate with wax tablets pinned beneath each one:

YOUR WORDS COST HUNGER.
NOW THEY COST TONGUE.

It was theatrical. Cruel. Effective.

By noon, every House courier in district traveled with doubled guard and half the confidence. Sereth answered by increasing patrol stipends and quietly deputizing guild collectors as temporary wardens.

Nara saw the gate display and refused to speak to me for a full day.

When she finally did, her voice was ice.

"You are becoming easy to fear," she said. "Hard to follow."

"Fear moves faster than faith," I answered.

"So does rot," she said, and walked away.

That night, Yarrow asked if we had "won" against the broadcast.

I thought of the ten-thousand reward still hanging over my head, of new deputized collectors, of how quickly cruelty had become language both sides understood.

"No," I said. "We just changed what the next day costs."

He nodded and wrote route notes by lamplight without another word.

After midnight I sat alone with the ledger and realized my hand was steady while writing one kind of line and shaking on another.

Steady on operations. Shaking on names.

I left the page half-finished and watched dawn come in through soot-streaked glass.

Language turned on me faster than law.

A week after the herald ambush, I heard the phrase first from a butcher arguing over knife rent.

"Pay in bone like Veyr's stray," he spat when the lender doubled terms.

By evening, children were saying it in game chants. By morning, clerks muttered it when new levy notices arrived. In three days, my surname had become a unit of hidden cost.

That should have been useful. Myth is leverage.

It was also uncontrollable.

I tried to shape the rumor stream with planted versions: Cael frees debt children; Cael spares medics; Cael targets only officials. Some of it was true some days. None of it stayed true once it entered market mouths.

A clean sigil is a liar, someone had scrawled beside my wanted sketch.

They were learning too quickly.

Irix mocked my frustration and taught me the hunter's rule about reputation.

"You don't control story," he said. "You choose which story costs your enemies more to believe."

So I chose one.

For six nights we seeded the same message through drink stalls, shrine queues, and ration lines: House Veyr's collectors were marking families for collateral seizure under emergency clauses. Some of those clauses existed. Some didn't. The panic was real either way.

Result came hard. Families started hiding children before tax rounds. Dock workers refused random identity checks. One collector was found nailed to his own ledger door with his stamp chain wrapped around his throat.

The regime answered with curfew expansions and public whipping for "rumor sabotage." A woman was flogged for repeating a line I had planted. She never knew my name. I knew hers after Yarrow brought me the list.

"Do we stop?" he asked.

I looked at the sheet. Forty-two detained. Nine whipped. Two dead in holding.

"No," I said, and hated the clarity of it.

That night, I walked old market lanes in disguise and listened to my own legend spoken by strangers.

Some called me debtbreaker.
Some called me child-using butcher.
Some called me necessary.

All of them were right often enough to be dangerous.

At a tea stall near fish arch, two old women argued over whether I was House revenge or district revenge wearing a person suit. One of them looked straight at me, not recognizing me, and said, "Doesn't matter what he is. Matters what breaks after he passes."

I paid for both their tea and left before they could ask why.

Back at the tannery, Halvek reviewed the updated rumor effects map and tapped the district where enforcement had thinned.

"Mythic pressure works," he said.

"At what burn rate?" Nara asked from the doorway.

No one answered.

The map did not show things that mattered most: Yarrow flinching at loud bells now, Nara sleeping at the clinic table with scalpel still in hand, me hearing my name in mirror reflections before anyone spoke.

By dawn, the idiom had mutated again.

Count your ash twice, then hide the bones.

I wrote it in the ledger margin and circled it. Not as victory. As warning.

Because once people start speaking you as proverb, they stop asking whether you are still human.
