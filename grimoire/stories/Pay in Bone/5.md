By dawn, I met the boy who tried to cut my purse.

He hit me at the third fish lane, where steam from the gut vats made everyone look half-drowned and honest hands looked busy enough to trust.

He came in close on my bad side, two fingers and a nail-knife, quick enough that I only felt the light tug at my coat seam before I saw him.

Small frame. Bare feet gone gray from ash dust. Hair cut with something blunt. Eyes older than a twelve-year-old should carry.

Around twelve, I guessed. In this district, that meant almost nothing.

I caught his wrist by reflex and pinned it against the scales post. He twisted like wire, tried to bite, then froze when he saw how close my knife already sat under his jaw.

"Give me one reason," I said.

He kept his breathing steady. Good sign. Or bad.

"Because you don't look like a man who kills for ten cinders," he said.

I almost laughed.

Ten cinders had started half the blood I've seen.

I pulled the stitched purse from his sleeve and opened it. Three chalk tokens. A rusted button. My own coin. Two fishhooks. No food.

"Name," I said.

"Yarrow."

"Family?"

He shrugged one shoulder.

"Depends who's asking."

That was the first time I saw what he was: not innocent, not hardened, not either thing cleanly. Just adapted. Fast enough to stay fed most days.

I let go of his wrist and stepped back.

He didn't run.

"You should run," I said.

"You should stop bleeding through your shirt," he answered, glancing at the seam under my coat.

I touched my side. Fresh wetness from yesterday's movement split. Not much, but enough to track.

He had seen it before I had.

"Who do you work for?" I asked.

"Who pays first," he said.

Market bells hit first trade hour. A notice crew rolled into brazier square with white masks and levy slates. People turned away before the first name was read. I watched which alleys emptied fastest.

Yarrow watched the same routes.

Competence is always visible if you know where to look.

"Breakfast," I said.

He narrowed his eyes.

"With what coin?"

"Mine."

"Then you'll ask for something after."

"Yes."

He considered that as if I had offered him a legal contract instead of stale bread.

"All right," he said. "But I pick the stall."

He chose a widow's griddle near the kiln lanes, the one that watered broth less than the others and took quiet bribes to serve without questions. He knew who watched from the corner shrine, which warden pair took kickback from the spice booth, and which side of the stall kept rain off your plate and line of sight on both exits.

He sat with his back to nothing.

I let him.

We ate rye heel and fish skin fried in old oil while two lanes over a levy clerk seized a cart and called it corrective taxation.

"How old are you?" I asked.

"Depends who's counting," he said, then added, "Last woman who stitched me said eleven. That was winter."

"Who stitched you?"

"Surgeon with the burned wrists."

Nara's apprentice.

He saw recognition flicker across my face and said, almost proud, "I know that clinic's back door knocks."

Useful, I thought.

I hated the speed of that thought.

After food, I gave him two cinders and my old guard button.

"For what?" he asked.

"Map me three safe courier cuts between kiln lanes and tannery rise. No shrine roads. No toll arches. No mirror posts."

He turned the button between his fingers.

"By when?"

"Sunset."

"Price is wrong," he said. "That's patrol timing, not walking directions."

He was right.

I gave him one more cinder.

"Now it's insulting instead of wrong," he said.

He still took it.

By late afternoon he brought me six routes, not three, scratched in charcoal on packing paper with marks for dog pens, blind corners, bribe booths, and one chapel bell tower that changed watch patterns every second day instead of daily.

I checked the first two myself.

They held.

At dusk I took him to Nara's clinic because he had a split knuckle and because I needed to see whether she would throw him out when she knew he was working my lanes.

She opened the back room curtain, saw him, and looked at me before she looked at the wound.

"Table," she said to him.

To me: "You again."

"He needs stitches."

"He needs sleep and clean water," she said. "Stitches are for people who stop punching brick." She cleaned the knuckle anyway.

Yarrow sat still through the wash and only flinched once when she set the needle.

"Hold pressure," she told him.

He obeyed.

She turned to me while he looked away and lowered her voice.

"How old is he really?"

"Around twelve," I said.

"Around twelve is not an age." Her expression hardened. "He's running routes for you."

"He's running routes for coin."

"Don't play grammar with me." She tied off the stitch line and cut thread with her teeth. "Children who carry messages for hunted men get killed for punctuation errors."

I felt anger rise and sat on it.

"He's not a child in these lanes," I said.

Nara's eyes sharpened, and I understood too late I had given exactly the answer she feared.

"No," she said. "He's a child in every lane. You just need him not to be."

Yarrow watched us without moving, learning what tone buys silence.

Nara wrapped his hand and sent him to the front bench with half a pear and strict instructions to keep pressure on the bandage. He went.

When the curtain dropped behind him, she stepped close enough that only I could hear.

"You don't get to make him your knife because yours got taken," she said.

"I need scouts."

"You need twenty things. Pick a few you can live with afterward." She held my gaze. "Or at least stop pretending you'll live with them cleanly."

I wanted to tell her she sounded like my father when he was tired and right.

I said nothing.

She handed me a folded dressing packet instead.

"Your seam's pulling again," she said. "Change this tonight. And don't come back with wardens behind you."

Her fingers brushed mine as she passed the packet, brief and accidental, the kind of contact people forget if they can afford to.

I noticed.

I noticed she noticed that I noticed.

No one said anything about it.

Outside, evening rain started, thin and warm. Late-summer rain that smells like rust and tannin and river rot.

I sent Yarrow home with two more cinders and a list of couriers to avoid.

"I don't have a home," he said.

"Then a place you sleep twice," I said.

He accepted that definition without argument.

Three nights later he returned at second watch with blood on his sleeve that was not his.

He didn't knock at the front. Three taps, pause, one tap at the clinic back door, exactly right.

Nara opened and swore once when she saw him.

"Inside."

I was already there, sorting route slips by lamp.

Yarrow stood in the doorway, breathing too fast, one cheek streaked with grime and tears he refused to wipe.

"I had to," he said before either of us spoke. "He had my satchel and he knew your name."

Nara took his arm and sat him on the table.

"Whose blood?"

"Warden auxiliary," he said. "Maybe. Coat looked official but cheap."

"Where?"

"Sluice stairs by rope bridge."

He said it like coordinates in an inventory, not confession.

Nara cleaned a shallow cut under his ribs while asking practical questions in a flat voice.

"Blade size?"

"Palm knife."

"How deep did you cut him?"

"Deep enough."

She glanced at me over his shoulder. There was no accusation in the look. That would have been easier to answer.

There was assessment.

Outcome measured against inputs.

I had introduced him to patrol maps, dead zones, and courier leverage.

Now there was a body near a bridge and a boy holding still while a surgeon stitched him.

Cause and effect did not need moral language to remain true.

"You dizzy?" Nara asked him.

"No."

"Nauseous?"

"No."

"Scared?"

He thought about that longer.

"Not now," he said.

I looked away first.

After she dressed the cut, Nara put a cup of hot bitterleaf in his hands and told him to drink slow. Then she stood, washed blood from her wrists, and came back to where I waited by the ledger desk.

"This is your first one," she said quietly.

"My first what?"

"Your first time turning skill into a child-shaped weapon and calling it necessity." She did not blink. "You won't be able to pretend you didn't know after tonight."

The room was hot with steam and lye. Rain ticked on the roof in patient drops. Yarrow sat ten feet away sipping tea like this was an ordinary injury and not a line crossed.

"If I hadn't used him," I said, "someone else would've."

"Maybe," she said. "And now that someone is you."

I had no clean defense.

Only arithmetic.

Patrol pressure was tightening. My bounty was high enough to buy a district's winter grain. Small bodies moved where mine could not. Information came cheapest from the ignored.

All true.

Still rot.

Before dawn I walked Yarrow to an old kiln alcove I'd secured with two blankets and a lock loop nobody could see from lane level. He checked the roofline, the drain run, and the exit wall before sitting.

"You going to stop using me now?" he asked.

The honest answer would have sounded like cruelty, so I gave him honesty anyway.

"No," I said.

He nodded once, unsurprised.

"Then teach me better," he said. "If I'm doing this, don't let me do it stupid."

I said I would.

He fell asleep with a knife in his sleeve and one hand on the satchel strap.

I stayed awake and watched first light crawl across broken tile.

In the puddle by the door, my reflection moved when I didn't, just a fraction early, mouth shaping words I couldn't hear.

I looked away before it finished.

At noon I opened the ledger and wrote until my hand stopped shaking.

Entry Four.

Late summer. District Nine courier network established through kiln lanes and tannery rise.

Primary scout acquired: Yarrow, approximately twelve, no fixed household, high route literacy, strong risk tolerance.

Operational result: six viable no-toll routes mapped; patrol evasion improved.

Consequence: Yarrow involved in lethal contact at sluice stairs. First confirmed kill (self-report, plausible).

Medical follow-up: treated at Nara Quill's clinic; superficial rib cut; stable.

I stopped there, then added the line I did not want in my own handwriting.

Personal note: I found a tool that looks like a child. The tool works exceptionally well.

The sentence sat on the page like a verdict and a confession sharing the same ink.

That evening, market children were back to running courier tags through smoke lanes, laughing when they won, vanishing when wardens rounded corners.

By night, Yarrow was memorizing patrol schedules beside my cot and correcting my assumptions about which shrines took bribes in grain instead of coin.

By dawn, I understood exactly how people get broken in this city.

Not all at once.

Young, useful, and trained by hands old enough to know better.
