I found Yarrow in the archive crawlspace with Ledger B under his coat.

He did not run.

"Put it down," I said.

"Not unless we change the system," he answered.

Lio arrived furious, breathless, already reaching for guards.

Yarrow faced both of us and spoke like a clerk presenting policy:

"Keep murder, treason, convoy sabotage. Burn leverage pages on debt shame and private sins. We are governing with permanent blackmail."

He had planned reform by burglary.

I should have punished him.

Instead I called emergency council and issued a restructure.

Ledgers remain active.
Custody moves to Yarrow under oath-chain supervision.
Access requires dual witness: Yarrow and Lio.
Nonviolent confessions older than one year move to sealed amnesty annex.

Lio objected until I made the alternative explicit: full trust collapse.

Yarrow took custody oath in quarry hall without shaking.

"I will guard this archive against enemy breach and command abuse," he said.

The room heard the second half.

Afterward he asked me quietly, "Did I do wrong?"

"You did necessary," I said.

"Not the same thing," he replied.

"No."

For the first time in years, power moved out of my hand and the structure got stronger.

I published confessions and called it transparency.

The packet was curated.

Three captains for ration theft.
One quartermaster for convoy-time sales.
Two magistrates for bond-child laundering before the White Hair Edict.

All true.
None of my worst truths included.

Crowds cheered in Chimney Court. Two bread riots dissolved without force. Recruitment rose in districts that read the release as courage.

Yarrow and Lio managed redactions. When I moved to strike a paragraph protecting a favored officer, Yarrow stopped his pen.

"Either this is rule or theater," he said.

"It is stability," I answered.

He marked it for partial release and wrote my compromise into the margins.

Nara sent one line by courier:

CURATED HONESTY IS STILL A LIE.

She stopped attending public ceremonies after that.

Operationally, the release worked. Mid-level corruption tightened. Officers self-reported minor offenses for amnesty before exposure cycles hit.

Morally, nothing changed: truth remained a tool, not a principle.

That night I listed three acts I had withheld.

Escort-anchor deaths.
Lime room execution.
Family funeral assassinations.

I folded the page, sealed it, and put it back in the dark.

The break happened over a scouting order.

I approved a catacomb probe knowing first team losses were likely and useful for ward mapping.

Nara read the sheet, set it down, and asked one question.

"Will you go first?"

I did not answer.

Four scouts died by night bell. We gained three ward signatures and one partial route.

Nara did not come to the next council.

She sent notice instead:

Triage caravans continue.
Strategic attendance terminated.

No threat. No plea. Final grammar.

After that, clinic and command ran as parallel states sharing one city. We coordinated through manifests and couriers only.

At council I acted unchanged. Morn patched medical gaps with field sawbones and clerks. Halvek watched me avoid Nara's empty chair like it carried plague.

At night I unfolded an old map where Nara's hand still marked safe crossings in slanted ink. The notes had faded from rain and handling. I still slept with it under my blanket.

Yarrow found it once and quietly put it back when I woke.

In ledger I wrote two lines:

Medical coordination: functional.
Moral coordination: broken.

Then I moved to the next operation because moving had become the only thing I still knew how to do.

Sereth's legitimacy was thinning, so I cut it faster.

House Veyr had split into loyalists, opportunists, and frightened neutrals pretending to be both. I funded two branches through deniable channels and fed each forged orders proving the other had signed assassination contracts.

The first killing happened before midnight on East Veyr Row.

By second bell, manor districts were on fire. Guards torched archive rooms before rivals could seize debt ledgers. Servants and children were trapped in courtyards built for prestige, not evacuation.

I watched from a roof with Irix.

"You funded both sides," he said.

"Yes."

"Then this is yours."

He was right.

Nara's caravans crossed active kill lanes anyway. We coordinated corridor clearances by signal flag and did not exchange a single personal word.

By dawn, Sereth's command web had visibly thinned. Messengers ran double routes. Council meetings were canceled because no branch could guarantee safe attendance.

Operationally: tempo gained.
Humanly: arson by design.

I issued a public statement condemning "intra-House extremism" and offered mediation through Chimney Court.

In private I wrote the truthful line:

I fed both fires and called the smoke strategy.

The Cinder Throne sent Envoy Carth in silk mail and polished courtesy.

His offer sounded elegant and meant surrender: shared governance in outer districts if I disbanded private warlock units and submitted ritual operations to Throne licensing.

I hosted him for three nights.

He believed he was measuring me for annexation. I was harvesting him for routes.

At dinner he gave patrol timings, shrine resupply rhythms, levy-bell windows, and relic wagon cycles disguised as small talk. On the final night he raised a cup and said, "History favors order over ego."

"Then we are both at risk," I answered.

He departed under white truce pennants at dawn.

I sent Irix after him at noon.

"Kill the envoy," I said. "Erase records."

Irix made me repeat it once, clean and audible, then left.

Carth was gone by dusk.

I gained maps and burned diplomacy in the same stroke.

Council called it decisive.

My ledger called it exact:

Intelligence harvest high.
Negotiation future destroyed.

Three days after Carth vanished, decrees arrived from three regions naming me envoy-killer and lawful correction target.

Then roads filled with armies.

Levy columns, shrine guns, sanctified wagons. Outer scouts reported the same shape every hour: more movement, tighter lines, less room for error.

This was no longer family war.
This was continental attention.

Ambient mana thickened with the pressure. Bell glass hummed without touch. Ward circles brightened at midday as if fed from below.

Halvek called it saturation.

"Too many rites in one theater," he said. "Too much vow debt and blood law. Casting gets easier. Misfire gets catastrophic."

I converted Carth's stolen maps into defense lattice plans while Morn ran mobilization and Yarrow reorganized archive evacuation in case Nine Chimneys fell.

No one asked whether killing the envoy had been worth it.

Nara sent one courier line:

BURN WARD EXPANSION STARTED. SEND MORPHINE IF YOU STILL REMEMBER PEOPLE.

I sent morphine.

By first snow, tribute towns were hedging openly, paying half and holding half for whichever army reached them next.

In ledger I wrote what the room already knew:

The war now has momentum larger than command.

Halvek spread three maps across the forge table: casualty heat, spontaneous ward ignition, and mana density.

Every red zone overlapped.

"There is no ceiling now," he said. "Mass death is widening ambient flow. Progression caps are dissolving."

"For me?"

"For everyone. For you fastest, because you built infrastructure to absorb it."

That should have sounded like victory.
It sounded like sentence.

If catastrophe fed power and power produced catastrophe, then war had become its own fuel chain.

The mirror voices were ecstatic.

Scale now.
Become larger than ending.

At dawn I signed escalation orders: outer fortification, tribute increases where compliance held, fragment research tripled.

I wrote the logic in the margin so I could not pretend later:

If the system rewards catastrophe, refusing to scale is surrender.

Then I chose scaling anyway.

They ringed Nine Chimneys in three layers: sanctified infantry, hill knights, and hymn guns.

Relic trebuchets opened first. Choir shells followed, scrambling ward timing and command calls. By day two we were losing interior floors.

Morn wanted breakout with heavy losses.
Irix wanted artillery decapitation.
Halvek offered full pain-lattice integration.

"Link all nine chimneys through your nervous system," he said. "You become conductor. Cost may be permanent."

I agreed at dusk on day three.

Needles in spine points. Bronze forks at tower roots. Blood script on both forearms. Once linked, every enemy impact landed inside my body as electrical fire.

We held three more days and broke the siege lanes with synchronized pressure surges that turned every approach lethal.

Nine Chimneys survived.

When I shut the lattice down, my right arm did not move.

No pain. No response. Dead weight from shoulder down.

Nara tested reflexes, then said, "Possibly permanent."

I signed casualty rolls left-handed and wrote one sentence beneath them:

City preserved by sacrificing limb to architecture.

Four days after the siege, I tested the arm with a knife.

Wrist cut. No pain.
Elbow cut. No pain.

Only blood proving tissue still belonged to me.

The mirror called it optimization.

I wrapped the cuts and went to council.

If maiming was now structural, we needed structure for it. I expanded prosthetic workshops: hook rigs, brace joints, lever wrists, cargo harnesses. Quartermasters called it recovery doctrine.

Nara called it adaptation to mass harm.

"Mercy would have been fewer maimings," she said.

By week's end, one-armed lift crews and brace-fitted scouts were routine. The language shifted from wounded to converted.

I heard that shift and let it stand.

At night, phantom pain sparked in fingers I could no longer command. I logged the same line twice in two books:

Body becoming equipment.

Casualties climbed past what field medicine could absorb.

Neutral clinic corridors.
No arrests or conscription inside marked lanes.
Violation names published on first breach.
Caravan withdrawal on second.

It looked weak.
It worked.

Survival numbers improved immediately.

Nara attended compliance audits and supply reviews only. No strategic planning, no personal reconciliation.

"Do not confuse cooperation with repair," she said.

Lives saved by treaty.
Trust not restored.

Yarrow commanded roof scouts by then.

Not courier work. Not symbolic rank. Command.

Teen crews moved over canal roofs and collapsed chapels where adult teams made too much noise. Yarrow gave orders from gutters with no tremor in his voice.

"Two north to bell lane. One cut runner if he reaches shrine wall. No chase past lantern line."

"Only routes."

Loyalty intact.
Childhood unrecoverable.

No apology language. Just logistics.

"You chose efficiency," she said.

Vanishing by paperwork.

No corpse. No absolution. Same weight.
