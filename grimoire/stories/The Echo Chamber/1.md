# Chapter 1: Thread One

Nate Wilkes made his account at 1:13 a.m. because sleep wasn't working again and scrolling felt like swallowing static.

His bio said: *I'm nobody. I just connect dots.*

He meant it as a joke.

The post that started everything was six lines long and mostly irritation.

> Why are all the city clocks off by two minutes? Not one clock. All of them. Bus station. Courthouse. Mall tower. Either everyone is lazy at once, or someone likes us late.

He added a blurry photo of the courthouse clock and went to make instant noodles.

By the time he came back, the post had twelve comments.

By morning, it had three thousand.

Most of the comments were normal internet chaos.

*bro discovered daylight savings*

*have u considered batteries exist*

But then there were threads nested under threads from people who wrote with the cold certainty of believers.

*I've been timing this for months. It's not random.*

*The train board always updates 119 seconds late.*

*My hospital pager drifts exactly 2:00 every Tuesday after midnight.*

Nate stared at the screen over his coffee and laughed once, a short startled sound.

"Okay," he said to no one. "We're doing this, apparently."

He posted again that evening.

> Fine. If anyone has proof, drop it. Screenshots, timestamps, anything clean.

The replies came in like rain on tin.

Documents. Videos. Forum archives from 2014. A scanned newspaper clipping about a failed software rollout that had lasted "only forty-eight hours" before being "fully corrected."

Nate made folders.

He told himself he liked order. That was all.

At work the next day, he answered support tickets while a second monitor filled with files named things like `clock_drift_compilation_v2` and `CITY-WIDE_SYNC_ANOMALIES`.

By Friday, someone had made a fan account that quoted him in black-and-white graphics.

By Sunday, he had a server with six thousand members called **The Echo Chamber**.

He hated the name.

He kept it anyway.

---

The first sign it was getting out of hand was when people stopped asking questions and started assigning roles.

Nate woke up Monday to seventy-two direct messages, including:

*Thank you for opening my eyes.*

*I've told my family to reset all watches at dawn, as instructed.*

*When is the first gathering?*

He had instructed no one to do anything.

He typed fast.

> I'm not a leader. I'm just collecting weird data.

A moderator named Rook pinned the message.

Then Rook replied beneath it:

*Classic Nate humility. Focus on the signal, everyone.*

Three hundred reactions. Fire emojis. Clock emojis. Eye emojis.

Nate deleted his own post.

Someone had already screenshotted it.

That night he got takeout, sat on his apartment floor, and watched a livestream from someone in another state holding a kitchen timer up to their webcam.

"Two minutes," the streamer whispered. "Two minutes exactly."

The chat moved too fast to read.

His username was everywhere.

*nate was right*

*thread one was prophecy*

*trust the drift*

Nate pressed his palms into his eyes until his vision filled with sparks.

He was not right.

He had pointed at a cracked wall and now people were walking through it like it was a door.

At 2:00 a.m. he opened the server settings and hovered over **Delete Community**.

He could not make himself click.

Instead, he made another post.

> New rule: no real-world actions. No meetups, no confrontations, no "operations." This is research only.

The post got shared as a sacred text before sunrise.

Someone printed it on a t-shirt.

At work, his manager asked why two local reporters had called the help desk asking for him by full name.

Nate said there had been a misunderstanding online.

His manager said, "Fix it," with the tone people use when they believe problems can be fixed by wanting them fixed.

At 11:58 a.m., every monitor in the office flickered.

At 12:00 exactly, all the clocks in the building jumped backward by two minutes.

No one else seemed to notice.

Nate did.

On his phone, twelve new notifications appeared in a single pulse.

The top one was from Rook.

*The city just confirmed your findings. We're ready for Phase Two.*
