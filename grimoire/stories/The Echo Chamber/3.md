# Chapter 3: The Noise Floor

The conference room had no windows and too much light.

Nate sat across from Agent Lian Morales at a metal table with a carafe of bad coffee between them. A second agent stood by the door, silent, arms folded, like punctuation.

Morales slid a tablet across the table.

"Your server growth curve," she said. "Three thousand in twelve hours. Ten thousand by day three. Fifty-two thousand by last night."

Another swipe.

"Incidents with direct Echo Chamber references: forty-seven. Confirmed injuries: fourteen. Property damage still being assessed."

Another swipe.

"This one is from two hours ago."

Video: a teenager with a bike helmet and a crowbar saying, "Nate said the grid lies. We have to free the signal."

Nate looked away.

"I never said that."

"I know."

"Then tell them."

Morales held his gaze. "People don't leave belief because authority asks politely. They leave when the story stops paying them."

Nate blinked hard. "What does that even mean?"

"It means your words became a machine. We can't shut it off from outside. You built it from inside. You have to go back in and pull the gear out."

---

At 10:00 a.m., Nate logged into Echo Chamber for the first time in eighteen hours.

Unread pings covered the screen like mold.

Rook had created new channels while Nate was gone.

`#field-ops`

`#phase-two-reports`

`#martyrs`

Nate's hands shook so badly he hit the wrong keys twice before posting.

> Effective now, this server is closed. There is no phase, no operation, no hidden instructions. If you are planning anything in real life, leave and go home.

Rook replied before anyone else.

*Compromised account. Do not engage.*

Then pinned his own message.

Morales, watching over Nate's shoulder from the edge of frame, said quietly, "Do it."

Nate clicked permissions.

Revoked moderator role.

`Error: Action blocked by role hierarchy.`

Rook had moved himself above Nate sometime overnight.

Nate made a sound that was half-laugh, half-break.

"He stole my server."

Morales nodded once. "Can you prove identity publicly?"

Nate opened camera.

Went live.

---

Seventy thousand viewers.

Nate looked directly into the lens and read from a legal pad with thick block letters so he would not drift.

"My name is Nathan Wilkes. I started this account because I was lonely and mad about a clock."

Chat scrolling. Faster.

"There is no code. I did not appoint anyone. I did not approve actions. People have been hurt, and that is on me."

Heart hammering.

"Rook is not speaking for me."

He held up his driver's license to camera. Then his employee badge. Then the date written on paper in black marker.

The chat split.

*deepfake*

*that's him*

*Rook warned us this would happen*

*wait why does Rook's timestamp mismatch??*

Morales handed him a printed screenshot.

Rook's profile metadata. Account created 19 days ago. Three prior aliases linked to coordinated disinformation cases. Foreign hosting nodes. Bot amplification clusters.

Nate swallowed and held it to camera.

"This is who you've been following. A burner account farm that used me as bait."

The chat slowed.

Not stopped. Slowed.

That was enough.

Nate kept talking.

He did not speak like a prophet now. He spoke like a tired person admitting fault.

He listed the injuries he knew by count and city.

He gave the hotline number Morales wrote on a sticky note.

He told people exactly how to report false emergency claims.

He repeated, three times: "Go home. Log off."

By the end of the stream, thirty percent of active members had left the server.

By evening, it was sixty.

By midnight, Rook's account was gone.

---

The hearings took months.

Nate gave statements to committees with names longer than patience. Civil suits came and went. Some people forgave him. Many did not. He stopped checking comment sections entirely.

He also started therapy, because at some point survival becomes logistics.

When he finally returned to his apartment full-time, he unplugged every smart clock he owned and bought one cheap analog wall clock from a pharmacy.

It ticked loudly. It drifted by thirty seconds every week.

He let it.

On a wet Sunday afternoon in October, he opened a blank page and typed:

> If you're reading this because you think I have answers, I don't. If you're reading this because you feel alone, that part I understand.

He did not post it.

Not yet.

Instead he saved it in a folder called `drafts` and went outside to buy groceries before the store closed.

At the crosswalk, he looked up at the traffic timer.

2:00.

2:00.

1:59.

He kept walking anyway.
