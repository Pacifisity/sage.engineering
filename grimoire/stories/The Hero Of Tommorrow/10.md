# Chapter 10: Eventually

The inside of the Obsidian Citadel smelled of old candles and, improbably, fresh bread.

Ren had expected stone and screams and the lurid red glow of concentrated evil. There was stone, yes, and a glow — but the torches were ordinary orange, several of them guttering low because no one had trimmed the wicks, and the corridors were lined with oak filing cabinets labeled in a neat, slightly cramped hand.

*WESTERN CAMPAIGN — ONGOING* was half-shut, a scroll jammed in the drawer.

*PROPHECY CORRESPONDENCE — SORTED BY DATE* was apparently overflowing, because additional letters had been bundled and stacked beside it in a neat pile. Ren recognized the handwriting on the top envelope and chose not to acknowledge this.

The kitchen smelled of rosemary. Two very nervous kitchen staff were stirring something large.

Beyond it: a meeting room, maps layered on maps, the original table surface long since buried. Beyond that: a corridor of sealed scrolls marked *PLANS — REVISED — DO NOT DISCARD*.

Then the throne room door. Open.

---

Mordavel was at a desk.

Not the throne — the throne was behind him, clearly ornamental, probably original to the building. The desk was practical, covered in ledgers and annotated scrolls and three ink pots in varying states of dryness. The man behind it was average height, wearing black robes that had seen easier days, with reading glasses he removed when Ren stepped through the door.

He had ink on his left hand.

"You came," he said.

"I came," Ren agreed.

"Sword out, I notice."

Ren looked at the blade. "Right." Not sure whether to sheathe it. Kept it. "I wasn't sure what to expect."

"Reasonably cautious." Mordavel set his glasses on the desk and folded his hands. "I had myself ready on three separate occasions in the last two months. You didn't come on any of them."

"I was working up to it."

"I know." A pause, neither reproachful nor forgiving — just accurate. "Petra sends updates."

Ren went very still. "She *what*?"

"We attended the same archive school. Thirty years ago. We stayed in correspondence." The faintest shadow of something crossed Mordavel's face. "She was worried about you. She felt someone should be aware if you actually made it."

The architecture of several weeks rearranged itself in Ren's head.

"So you knew."

"I *related*. There is a difference." Mordavel gestured to the desk, to the overflowing filing cabinets visible through the open door, to the general atmosphere of a man who had been putting something enormous off for a long time and was tired. "The Final Darkness is not something I designed. It was bequeathed. I am the fourth Demon King. The first three left detailed notes and zero opt-out instructions."

Ren lowered the sword. Not all the way. Halfway.

"You don't want to end the world."

"I have never wanted to end the world," Mordavel said, with the quiet firmness of someone who has wanted to say this for years. "I want to cancel the contract. I have been trying to find the cancellation clause for twenty-six months. The shadow armies run on an old binding spell that predates me by four centuries. The caterers are under a standing order I cannot countermand without the original seal." He opened a drawer and produced a thick file. "I wrote to twelve legal scholars. Eight didn't respond."

Ren sat down in the chair across from the desk. Uninvited. Mordavel did not object.

"I have plans," Ren said.

"I know. Petra also shared those."

"Was Phase Three—"

"The approach through the lower Citadel to the Pillar of Binding, yes." Mordavel turned to a cabinet and extracted Ren's folder, which had sticky annotations on a significant portion of the pages. He had read every word. "If the Pillar can be invoked with the right seal, it suspends all active binding contracts originating from this site. The shadow armies stand down. The Final Darkness unschedules itself." He set the folder on the desk between them. "There is, of course, substantial scroll-work to follow. Months of it, probably."

"But it ends."

"It ends."

Ren looked at the plan. All those phases. All the careful arrows and the rain variant and the glossary. The plan that had been a destination and a procrastination and a portrait of someone not yet ready.

It was, despite everything, the right plan.

"The Pillar is below us?" Ren asked.

"Three levels down. I can take you there myself."

"Now?"

Mordavel considered. Outside, the smoke on the horizon was visible even through the Citadel's high window — the inertia of centuries, still grinding forward while the two people responsible for stopping it sat in a room and figured out how.

"Now seems like the right time," he said. He stood, and reached behind him, and from a hook on the wall took a coat that had the seal of the First Demon King embroidered on the collar — the seal that could invoke the Pillar. He had been keeping it polished.

Ren stood too, and sheathed the sword.

"I was never going to fight you," Ren said. "I think I knew that for a while."

"I suspected you knew," Mordavel said. "The sword was always a formality." He moved toward the door, then paused. "The caterers have made bread."

"I noticed."

"We could take some with us."

"Yes," Ren said. "Absolutely yes."

---

The mark on Ren's left palm faded entirely at the third level down, while the Pillar's light filled the chamber and Mordavel spoke the invocation from the first seal and Ren pressed the founder's crest into the old stone and the binding contracts dissolved one by one like ice in spring water.

No rewinds. No undoing. Just the permanent, irreversible present.

Which turned out to be exactly enough.

Later — much later, after the scroll-work and the long careful process of dismantling a four-century war machine through legal channels — Ren would sometimes look at the faded mark on the palm. The hourglass had gone smooth, just skin now, no longer an eye or a question.

Elda asked once if Ren missed it.

Ren thought about it honestly.

"No," Ren said. "Going forward is harder. But it's the only direction anything ever actually happens."

Outside, the smoke on the horizon had been gone for a week.

The wildflowers on the Ashmarsh ridge were, reportedly, excellent this year.
