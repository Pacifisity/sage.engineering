# Chapter 3: The Art of the Perfect Plan

Wednesday also came and went.

Then a week that Ren had loosely categorized as "planning and preparation" in a handwritten schedule that now covered three sheets of parchment and one napkin.

To be fair to Ren, the plans were *impressive*. There were annotated maps. There were troop movement projections. There was a ranked list of the Demon King's known generals, cross-referenced with their weaknesses, color-coded in four different inks. There was a glossary.

"This is extraordinary work," Elda admitted, standing over the desk. She had not meant to sound impressed. It came out anyway.

"Thank you," Ren said, genuinely pleased. "I think if I just add one more layer of contingency — see, Phase Three assumes good weather —"

"It's been two weeks."

"I know."

"The western mill town fell yesterday."

"I saw the smoke." Ren added a note about weather contingencies and drew an arrow to a footnote. "I think Phase Three needs a rain variant."

Elda picked up the pages and held them quietly.

"Ren," she said. "This plan is perfect. It's the most thorough battle plan I have ever seen in forty years of study."

"That's kind of you."

"The plan does not need a rain variant. The plan needs to be *used*."

Ren sat back and looked at all those careful arrows, all that ink.

"I just feel," Ren said slowly, "that I should be completely ready before I go. Because I have the time-reversal thing, right, and that means if something goes wrong I can restart. But I'd *prefer* not to have to use it. If I go in ready, I probably won't need to rewind at all."

"That is a reasonable attitude."

"And if I go in and something goes wrong and I *do* rewind, I lose everything I've experienced since that point. So if I've trained a lot and then I have to rewind, all that training is gone. Which means I should do all my training before my *first* attempt, to avoid losing it."

Elda squinted. There was something philosophically fishy about this logic but it was moving too fast for her to catch it.

"What I'm saying," Ren continued, "is that the time reversal actually creates an incentive to prepare *more*, not less. I'm accounting for variance. It's disciplined."

"The northwestern road is on fire," Elda said.

Ren added a footnote about fire.

---

The rain variant took three more days.

Sage Petra, who had begun quietly cataloguing the stages of the situation in a private journal, wrote the following entry:

*Day nineteen. The plan now has fourteen phases, two appendices, and a cover illustration. Ren drew a small hero figure on the cover with a sword raised. It is actually quite good. The hero in the drawing is notably more decisive than the hero in my kitchen eating porridge. I am beginning to suspect the plan is not a plan but a habitat — a comfortable place where intentions live because intentions require no bravery.*

She put the journal away when Ren knocked to borrow more ink.
