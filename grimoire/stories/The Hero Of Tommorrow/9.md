# Chapter 9: The Road That Doesn't Reset

Ren left before dawn, without ceremony.

No speech. No final breakfast. Just Ren, already dressed, leaving a note on the desk weighted down with a pear — the last one from the bowl, which felt appropriate in a way that was hard to explain.

*Elda — Going. Don't send anyone after me. Southwestern gate. —Ren*

The road north was the same road it had always been. The same pines. The same cool air. The same rhythm of hoof on stone. Ren had been down this road four times now, had carried the same provisions, slept at the same campsites, seen the same ridgeline emerge from morning fog.

But this time the road felt different in the way roads only feel when you know you are not coming back the same way.

Every footfall was committed. Every stone passed was behind you, permanent, added to the record of the day. No mark to press. Not even the option, not really — Ren had made a quiet decision in the tower before leaving: the last rewind was not for fear. It was not for a patrol that turned to look, or a shadow at the gate, or the dread arriving in the chest the way it always did. It was for something that genuinely could not go any other way.

Ren had not defined what that was. But it was not fear.

---

On the first night, camped where the Sal River used to cross the road before the shadow armies rerouted it into the ash flats, Ren read Mordavel's final letter again by firelight.

*I will say what I did not say in earlier letters: I do not want to destroy this world. I was given a responsibility I did not ask for, the same as you were given yours. The difference is that my responsibility moves forward automatically unless someone stops it. Yours requires you to act. I have always found it somewhat unfair that the burden of movement falls on the hero and not the villain, but I did not write the prophecy.*

*Come, then. I will tell the gate guard you are expected.*

Ren folded the letter and put it away and looked at the fire for a while.

---

The Citadel appeared on the morning of day three.

Ren stopped on the Ashmarsh ridge and looked at it.

The dread came, right on schedule. Tight chest. Loud thoughts. The exact weight of being one person pointed at a very final problem. The hourglass mark on the left palm pulsed once, faintly — not activating, just reminding.

*You have one.*

Ren looked at the hand.

Then looked at the Citadel.

Took one breath.

Took a second.

Started walking.

---

The southwestern gate was exactly as Mordavel's letters had described: four guards on a three-hour rotation, the stone patched twice and due for a third repair that had not been scheduled yet. Ren arrived in the window between shifts, which was either good planning or good luck or the two things being the same.

The gate was open a crack. Just enough. The letter had said: *I will tell the gate guard you are expected.*

There was no guard.

There was a small folded note tucked into the gap in the door, sealed with a skull-and-bow stamp.

It read: *See you inside.*

Ren's hand found the sword's hilt without thinking. The grip was exactly right — three weeks of adjusting it had eventually paid off. The blade cleared the sheath quietly.

*You can still press the mark,* said the part of Ren's brain that had been in charge for most of the past two months. *You still have one. You could go back to yesterday morning. You could—*

"No," Ren said.

To the voice. To the hand. To all the comfortable doors that had been built and rebuilt out of fear and called by better names.

And walked through the gate.
