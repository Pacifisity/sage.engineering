# Chapter 8: Running Out of Tomorrows

Ren noticed the first echo on a Wednesday morning.

It appeared in the kitchen doorway: a ghost-thin version of Ren from three weeks ago, walking through carrying yesterday's bread, visible for exactly one second before dissolving. It was precise in the way only real things are — the exact rumple of the old shirt, the particular way the previous-Ren had touched the door frame.

Ren set down the current bread and stood very still.

Petra confirmed it when Ren showed her the spot, keeping voice deliberately steady.

"Temporal echo," Petra said, looking at the faint impression with her lens. "The mark produces them when it's been overused. You are leaving impressions in the shared timeline."

"Is that dangerous?"

Petra chose her next words carefully.

"It's a warning. The mark was designed for genuine emergencies. One use. Perhaps two. It was not built for—" she paused, doing arithmetic "—how many times have you used it, total?"

Ren thought it through.

"Five confirmed." A pause. "Six. There was a small one. I rewound four hours because I said something embarrassing to the stable boy and it was still bothering me in the evening."

Petra set down her lens.

"Ren."

"I know."

"The mark has a limit. The old texts are specific: seven reversals before the mechanism frays completely." She held up her lens so the echo in the doorway was visible again — faint, fading, a ghost of a missed moment. "You have one left."

Ren looked at the mark. The hourglass at the center was thinner than it had been on arrival, the lines feathered at the edges like an old sketch.

One.

"If you go to the Citadel and you panic," Petra said, "you get one retreat. After that, whatever happens, happens."

"That's how everyone lives," Ren said.

"Yes."

"All the time."

"Every moment of every day. Without the option to undo it." Petra folded her lens away. "It is, for most people, simply called *being alive*."

Outside, the horizon was no longer a line of smoke. It was a wall. The shadow armies had moved deep into the valley. Ren could see torchlight in the lowlands at night now, a constellation of the wrong kind.

"What if I waste the last one on something small again?" Ren said. "What if I get there and I panic and I use it, and then I'm out, and I'm still not ready—"

"Then you won't be ready, and you'll have to go anyway." Petra said it plainly. "The mark was never going to make you ready, Ren. It was only ever going to make you comfortable with not being ready. Which is not the same thing."

Ren sat with that for a while.

"I've been using it as a door," Ren said. "Not a weapon. Just... a door I can always walk back through."

"Yes."

"And now the door is almost locked."

"One turn of the key left."

The Citadel was three days north. One rewind left. A world that was running out of the patience that geography provides before armies arrive at your actual doorstep.

Ren looked at the mark a long time.

Then went to find Elda.

"I'm going tomorrow," Ren said. "For real. I'm telling you now so I have to."

Elda did not say *you've said that before*. She said: "I'll have the horse ready at dawn."

"Also," Ren added, "I might need you to take my last letter to Mordavel. I want to go on the right terms."

Elda's eyebrow elevated slightly.

"What do the right terms look like, between a hero and a demon king?"

Ren thought.

"Honest ones."
