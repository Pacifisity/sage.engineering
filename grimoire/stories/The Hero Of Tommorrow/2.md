# Chapter 2: Tomorrow, I Promise

The room they gave Ren was in the upper east wing of the Sage Council's tower: a wide, airy chamber with a feather bed, a writing desk, and a window that looked over the whole eastern valley. Wild wheat moved in the lowlands like breathing gold.

"It's a lot prettier than I expected the end of the world to look," Ren mentioned to Elda.

"It won't look like this much longer," Elda replied.

"Right, right. All the more reason to appreciate it while I can."

They had given Ren a sword because the prophecy required one. It stood in the corner, leaning against the wall, dressed in its sheath. Three days in a row Ren had picked it up, made several confident practice swings, concluded that the grip needed adjusting, and put it back.

On the fourth day, Sage Petra arrived to find Ren at the writing desk with a mug of tea, reading a local history book borrowed from the archive.

"We found a map of the Citadel's outer walls," Petra said, sliding a roll of vellum across the desk.

"Excellent." Ren barely looked up. "I'll study that tonight."

"The eastern garrison fell this morning."

Ren did look up then. Outside, a column of smoke was visible on the horizon, slow and dark against the pale sky.

"That's... that's actually closer than I thought," Ren admitted.

"Yes."

"And you said three days' ride?"

"When the eastern garrison stood between us and the Citadel, yes. With it gone—" Petra paused. "Possibly two."

Ren nodded soberly. Picked up the mug. Drank. Set it down.

"I think I need to be at my best for this," Ren said. "I've been a bit anxious, honestly. It affects my concentration. I read that a person performs better when they're well-rested and in a stable mental state. I'm thinking if I give myself until Sunday—"

"It's Friday."

"Right. So Sunday gives me two full days to center myself, review the maps, maybe do some light training. I want to be sharp."

Petra stared.

"I want to be sharp too," she said at last. "I want to be sharp enough to believe any of this." She rolled the vellum back up and left.

Ren watched the smoke on the horizon a little longer.

"Sunday," Ren said again, as if sealing a contract. Then went back to the history book.

---

Sunday came and went.

Then the following Tuesday, which Ren had privately reclassified as "the new Sunday" without mentioning this to anyone.

Elda stopped by each morning. She stopped asking when. She simply stood in the doorway with a look that communicated everything without vocabulary, then went to manage the ongoing catastrophe in the rest of the kingdom.

By Ren's count, four villages had been swallowed by the shadow armies. The smoke on the horizon was no longer a single column. It was a smear.

"I'll go Wednesday," Ren told the window.

The window offered no objection.

Ren took this as agreement.
