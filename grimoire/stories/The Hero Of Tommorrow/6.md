# Chapter 6: What the Sage Saw

It was Sage Petra who formally confirmed it, though Elda had already known for two weeks.

Petra had been indexing temporal distortions around the tower as routine maintenance — residue readings, resonance checks — when she found the signature. A thread the color of old copper, unspooling from Ren's room and stretching northward for approximately three days' ride before snapping back, hard and clean, the way time moves when someone has undone it.

She found three separate threads.

She called Elda into the reading room and showed her the lens.

"That one is four days old," Petra said. "That one is nine. This faint one here — possibly two weeks, but the edges are degraded."

"How far north do they reach?"

Petra moved the lens. "The first two reach the Ashmarsh ridge. The third one is closer. Someone lost their nerve earlier on that occasion."

"And they all snap back here."

"To this building. To that room." Petra set down the lens. "Someone with the mark has been pressing it. Repeatedly."

They heard Ren coming up the stairs.

Both stood casually when the door opened.

Ren looked at two quiet faces arranged in the deliberate posture of people who have recently discussed something, and understood immediately.

"So," Ren said.

"Yes," said Elda.

"How long have you known?"

"Officially? Five minutes. Unofficially?" Elda tilted her head. "I tasted the air two weeks ago and recognized the residue from the old texts. I was waiting to see if you'd come to us."

Ren set down a bread roll and sat on the window ledge.

"How many times?" Petra asked.

"Three," Ren said. Then, quieter: "Four. I went a fourth time last week. I didn't tell you because on the fourth time I didn't even get as far as the third time and I was— I was embarrassed."

The room was quiet for a moment.

"So you've *been* there," Elda said.

"I've been there."

"You've seen the Citadel."

"I've seen it." Ren looked at the hands. Both of them, the marked palm and the other. "It's very real. More real than the plan. More real than anything I've read. And every time I get there I think: *today I'm ready*, and then I see it, and all that readiness just— dissolves. And the mark is right there. One word and I'm back here and nothing bad happened. It's so easy."

The smoke on the horizon was a curtain now, not a column. Three towns. Maybe four since yesterday.

Elda sat down beside Ren in a way that was not professorial.

"You're afraid," she said.

"Obviously."

"Good."

Ren looked up.

"Fear means you understand the stakes," Elda said. "Every Chosen One in every prophecy I have ever read was afraid. The prophecy does not say *hero arrives confident and fully rested*. The prophecy says *will*. Future tense. That means you haven't yet. It does not mean you can't."

Ren turned the mark over in the light. The hourglass at its center was quiet, patient.

"What if I get there and I press it again?" Ren asked. "What if I just keep pressing it every time it gets real?"

"Then eventually," Elda said, "you run out of *Returs*. And you'll have no choice but to go forward."

She said it gently.

It was somehow the least comforting and most honest thing anyone had ever told Ren.
